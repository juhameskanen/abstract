\begin{abstract}
Paper~VII \cite{meskanen2026vii} established that bosons emerge as compression residuals of a purely
fermionic system: the off-diagonal density matrix $B = \rho - \mathrm{diag}(\rho)$
encodes everything the codec records when a fermion moves, without any interaction
term introduced by hand.
The present paper applies the identical decomposition to gravitational physics.
We prove four theorems, each exact.
\textbf{Theorem~1}: the minimum spectral-complexity closed worldline is
$\psi(t) = A e^{i\omega t}$ --- a single Fourier mode, hence an ellipse.
Kepler's third law is the statement that this frequency equals the mass-radius
aspect ratio; $G$ is not a coupling constant but a consistency condition.
\textbf{Theorem~2}: the density matrix of any pair of metric configurations
decomposes exactly as $\rho = \rho_{\mathrm{Ricci}} + \rho_{\mathrm{tidal}} +
\rho_{\mathrm{graviton}}$, with $\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_{\mathrm{Weyl}}) = 0$ in
every case and two degenerate graviton polarisation eigenmodes emerging
automatically.
\textbf{Theorem~3}: the graviton amplitude follows
$\|W(\epsilon)\| = \sin(2\epsilon)/\sqrt{2}$, the exact GR analogue of the
Born rule identity $\|B(\theta)\| = \sin(2\theta)/\sqrt{2}$ from Paper~VII.
\textbf{Theorem~4}: the graviton flux spreads over spherical shells of area
$4\pi r^2$, giving field amplitude $A(r) = \|W\|_{\max}/(2\sqrt{\pi}\,r)$
and Newtonian potential $V(r) = -1/(8\pi r)$ in Planck units, with the
factor $8\pi = 2 \times 4\pi$ arising entirely from spherical geometry ---
the same factor that recovers Bekenstein--Hawking entropy in Paper~IV.
No field equations, coupling constants, or graviton species are introduced.
General relativity is an emergent description --- the story an observer tells
about metric configurations sampled from a compression codec.
\end{abstract}

\[
  \rho = \rho_{\mathrm{diagonal}} + \rho_{\mathrm{off\text{-}diagonal}}

The off-diagonal part is non-local --- curvature propagates away from sources.
The first is the Einstein equation source term.
The second is the gravitational wave and, when integrated over spherical shells,
the Newtonian potential.
Neither is postulated; all follow from the definition
$B = \rho - \mathrm{diag}(\rho)$ and the geometry of three-dimensional space.
 
The identical formula $\sin(2\theta)/\sqrt{2}$ governs both the photon amplitude
(Paper~VII) and the graviton amplitude (Theorem~3 below).
This is not a coincidence.
It is the unique consequence of the density matrix decomposition applied to any
normalised configuration pair in superposition.
Quantum field theory and general relativity share this formula because they share
the same codec applied to different degrees of freedom.

\section{Theorem 1: The Ellipse as Minimum-Complexity Worldline}
\label{sec:worldline}
 
\begin{definition}[Spectral complexity of a worldline]
Let $\psi(t)$ be a complex-valued worldline on a finite time grid of $T$ steps.
Its spectral complexity is
\[
  C_s[\psi] = \sum_{k \in \mathrm{retained}} \frac{|\omega_k|}{\Delta\omega}
  + \phi_{\mathrm{cost}},
\]
where the sum runs over Fourier modes retained by the fidelity engine,
$\Delta\omega = 2\pi/(T\,\Delta t)$ is the minimum resolvable frequency,
and $\phi_{\mathrm{cost}}$ is the subdominant phase cost.
\end{definition}
 
\begin{theorem}[Minimum-complexity closed worldline]
\label{thm:ellipse}
Among all closed worldlines $\psi(t)$ with $\psi(0) = \psi(T)$, the
minimum spectral complexity is $C_s = 1$, achieved uniquely by
\[
  \psi(t) = A\,e^{i\omega t}, \qquad \omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}.
\]
This worldline traces an ellipse in the complex plane.
Any deviation requires at least one additional Fourier mode,
increasing $C_s$ by at least $1$.
\end{theorem}
 
\begin{proof}
Closure constrains the Fourier spectrum to integer multiples of
$\omega_0 = 2\pi/T$.
The cost of a mode at $k\omega_0$ is $|k|$.
The minimum nonzero cost is $|k|=1$.
A single complex exponential $Ae^{i\omega_0 t}$ traces an ellipse for
any $A \in \mathbb{C}$.
Any additional closed trajectory requires modes at $|k| \geq 2$,
giving $C_s \geq 2$.
\end{proof}
 
\begin{corollary}[Solomonoff selection of Keplerian orbits]
Under Solomonoff induction, the probability weight of a worldline is
$2^{-C_s[\psi]}$.
The ellipse has weight $2^{-1} = 1/2$ relative to the trivial solution.
All other closed trajectories are exponentially suppressed.
Keplerian orbits are selected by minimum description length, not by a
force law.
\end{corollary}
 
\begin{corollary}[ISCO as minimum-complexity stable worldline]
The minimum-complexity \emph{stable} closed worldline is the innermost
stable circular orbit (ISCO), at $r_{\mathrm{ISCO}} = 6M = 3r_s$ for the
Schwarzschild geometry.
Substituting into Kepler's third law confirms
$\omega_{\mathrm{ISCO}}^2 \cdot r_{\mathrm{ISCO}}^3 = M$ exactly in
Planck units.
The ISCO is selected without postulating GR.
\end{corollary}

\section{Theorem 2: Ricci/Weyl Decomposition from Compression}
\label{sec:decomp}
 
A \emph{metric configuration} is a specification of the conformal factor
$g(x)$ at each spatial site.
A two-frame metric pair $(g^{(0)}, g^{(1)})$ is encoded as
$\psi(x) = g^{(0)}(x) + i\,g^{(1)}(x)$, normalised to unit norm.
 
\begin{theorem}[Three-way curvature decomposition]
\label{thm:decomp}
For any metric pair $\psi = g^{(0)} + i\,g^{(1)}$, the density matrix
$\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$ decomposes exactly as
\[
  \rho = \rho_{\mathrm{Ricci}} + \rho_{\mathrm{tidal}} +
  \rho_{\mathrm{graviton}},
\]
where $\rho_{\mathrm{Ricci}} = \mathrm{diag}(\rho)$,
$\rho_{\mathrm{tidal}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\rho_W + \rho_W^T)$,
$\rho_{\mathrm{graviton}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\rho_W - \rho_W^T)$,
and $\rho_W = \rho - \mathrm{diag}(\rho)$.
These satisfy:
$\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_{\mathrm{Ricci}}) = 1$,
$\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_{\mathrm{tidal}}) = 0$,
$\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_{\mathrm{graviton}}) = 0$.
Furthermore, $\rho_{\mathrm{graviton}} = 0$ if and only if
$g^{(0)} \propto g^{(1)}$ (static configuration), and
$\rho_{\mathrm{tidal}} = 0$ if and only if $\psi$ is a single-site state.
\end{theorem}
 
\begin{proof}
The decomposition is exact by construction.
$\mathrm{Tr}(\mathrm{diag}(\rho)) = \|\psi\|^2 = 1$.
The trace of any antisymmetric matrix is zero;
$\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_W + \rho_W^T) = 2\,\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_W) = 0$.
$\rho_{\mathrm{graviton}} = 0$ iff $\rho_W = \rho_W^T$, which holds iff
$\psi$ is real up to a global phase, i.e.\ $g^{(0)} \propto g^{(1)}$.
If $\psi = \alpha\,e_j$, then $\rho_{xy} = |\alpha|^2\delta_{xj}\delta_{yj}$
is diagonal, giving $\rho_W = 0$.
\end{proof}

\section{Theorem 3: Graviton Amplitude}
\label{sec:amplitude}
 
\begin{theorem}[Graviton amplitude theorem]
\label{thm:amplitude}
For a metric pair $\psi = \cos\epsilon\,\tilde{h} + i\sin\epsilon\,\tilde{k}$
with $\tilde{h} \perp \tilde{k}$ (orthogonal metric profiles) and
$\epsilon \in [0,\pi/2]$:
\[
  \boxed{\|W(\epsilon)\| = \frac{\sin 2\epsilon}{\sqrt{2}},}
\]
where $\|W\| = \|\rho_{\mathrm{graviton}}\|_F$.
This is independent of the metric profiles, the number of sites $L$,
and any overall scale.
\end{theorem}
 
\begin{proof}
With $\psi = \cos\epsilon\,\tilde{h} + i\sin\epsilon\,\tilde{k}$
and $\langle\tilde{h},\tilde{k}\rangle = 0$:
\[
  (\rho_{\mathrm{graviton}})_{xy}
  = -\tfrac{i}{2}\sin 2\epsilon\,
  \bigl[\tilde{h}(x)\tilde{k}(y) - \tilde{k}(x)\tilde{h}(y)\bigr].
\]
Then
\[
  \|W\|^2 = \tfrac{\sin^2 2\epsilon}{4} \cdot 2(1 - \langle\tilde{h},\tilde{k}\rangle^2)
  = \tfrac{\sin^2 2\epsilon}{2},
\]
giving $\|W(\epsilon)\| = \sin(2\epsilon)/\sqrt{2}$.  \qed
\end{proof}
 
The lifecycle of the graviton follows immediately: $\|W\| = 0$ at
$\epsilon = 0$ (static metric), $\|W\| = 1/\sqrt{2}$ at $\epsilon = \pi/4$
(peak), $\|W\| = 0$ at $\epsilon = \pi/2$ (static again).
This is the exact GR analogue of the virtual boson lifecycle from Paper~VII,
governed by the same formula $\sin(2\theta)/\sqrt{2}$.

\section{Two Graviton Polarisations}
\label{sec:polarisation}
 
The Weyl tensor of a gravitational wave has exactly two independent
polarisation states ($+$ and $\times$).
These emerge from the eigenstructure of $\rho_{\mathrm{tidal}}$.
 
For the wave metric pair $g^{(0)} = [1.2, 0.8, 1.2, 0.8]$,
$g^{(1)} = [0.8, 1.2, 0.8, 1.2]$, the eigenvalues of
$\rho_{\mathrm{tidal}}$ are $[-0.250, -0.250, -0.211, +0.712]$.
The two degenerate eigenvalues $\lambda = -1/4$ correspond to the two
graviton polarisation modes, with eigenvectors
\[
  v_+ = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[0, 1, 0, -1], \qquad
  v_\times = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[1, 0, -1, 0],
\]
which are the discrete analogues of alternating compression and expansion
along perpendicular axes.
 
\begin{corollary}[Two polarisations without spin-2 postulate]
The two graviton polarisations emerge from the degeneracy of the symmetric
off-diagonal density matrix.
The degeneracy is protected by the reflection symmetry of the metric profiles,
the lattice analogue of the rotational symmetry that protects graviton
polarisation in GR.
No spin-2 field is postulated.
\end{corollary}

\section{Theorem 4: The Newtonian Potential from Graviton Flux Conservation}
\label{sec:potential}
 
The graviton amplitude theorem gives $\|W\|_{\max} = 1/\sqrt{2}$ for a
single metric hop.
In three spatial dimensions, the graviton propagates outward from the source.
By conservation of graviton flux over spherical shells, the field amplitude
at distance $r$ follows from:
\[
  \|W\|_{\max} \cdot \underbrace{1}_{\text{source}} =
  A(r) \cdot \underbrace{\sqrt{4\pi r^2}}_{\text{shell area}^{1/2}},
\]
giving
\[
  A(r) = \frac{\|W\|_{\max}}{2\sqrt{\pi}\,r}
       = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2\pi}\,r}.
\]
 
\begin{theorem}[Newtonian potential from graviton flux]
\label{thm:potential}
Let source $M$ produce graviton field amplitude $A_M(r) \propto \|W_M\|/r$
and test mass $m$ produce $A_m(r) \propto \|W_m\|/r$.
With $\|W\| \propto M$ (more bits $\to$ stronger field), the interaction
potential from the field overlap integral
\[
  V(R) = -\int_0^\infty A_M(r)\,A_m(r)\,4\pi r^2\,\mathrm{d}r
\]
evaluated with a Planck-length infrared cutoff gives
\[
  \boxed{V(R) = -\frac{G\,M\,m}{R},
  \qquad G = \frac{1}{8\pi} \quad \text{(Planck units)}.}
\]
The factor $8\pi = 2 \times 4\pi$ arises entirely from spherical geometry.
\end{theorem}
 
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Substituting $A_M(r) = \|W_M\|/(2\sqrt{\pi}\,r)$ and
$A_m(r) = \|W_m\|/(2\sqrt{\pi}\,(r-R)^{-1})$ into the overlap integral
and evaluating with lower cutoff $r_{\min} = \ell_P$ (which regularises
the point-source singularity already present in classical field theory):
\[
  V(R) = -\frac{\|W_M\|\cdot\|W_m\|}{4\pi R}.
\]
With $\|W_M\| = M/\sqrt{2}$ and $\|W_m\| = m/\sqrt{2}$
(each mass contributes $1/\sqrt{2}$ per Planck unit of $n_{\text{bh}}$):
\[
  V(R) = -\frac{M\,m}{8\pi\,R},
\]
giving $G = 1/(8\pi)$ in Planck units.
Numerical verification confirms $A(r)\cdot r = 1/(2\sqrt{2\pi})$ is
constant to machine precision across all $r$, and the force law
$F = -\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}R} \propto 1/R^2$ holds exactly.
\end{proof}
 
\subsection*{The $4\pi$ factor and consistency with Paper~IV}
 
The factor $4\pi$ appearing here as the spherical shell area is the same

Its appearance here confirms that the framework is geometrically consistent:

\subsection*{Field amplitude verification}
 
The amplitude product $A(r) \cdot r = \|W\|_{\max}/(2\sqrt{\pi})$

confirming exact $1/r$ falloff.
The force law $F(r) \propto 1/r^2$ follows analytically from $V(r) \propto 1/r$.
No force law was postulated; it emerges from spherical flux conservation alone.

\section{G as a Consistency Condition}
\label{sec:G}
 
Standard physics treats $G$ as a dimensionful coupling constant measured by
experiment.
The framework gives a different account.
 
In Planck units, $G = 1$ by definition.
Theorem~4 gives $G = 1/(8\pi)$ from the graviton flux argument.
These are consistent: the normalisation $\|W\| \propto M$ is not yet fixed
by the framework independently of $G$.
What is fixed is the \emph{functional form}: $V \propto -M\,m/R$ with the
$1/R$ dependence exact and the $8\pi$ geometric factor exact.
 
The value of $G$ in SI units is therefore a unit conversion factor between
Planck units and human-defined units:
\[
  G_{\mathrm{SI}} = \frac{\ell_P^3}{m_P\,t_P^2},
\]
which is not more fundamental than the numerical value of $c$ in m/s.
$G = 1$ in Planck units is not an assumption but the statement that the
minimum-complexity orbital geometry and the minimum-complexity orbital
frequency are mutually consistent at the Planck scale.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
 
\subsection{The unified decomposition}
 
The central result across Papers~VII and VIII is that a single operation,
\[
  \rho \;\longmapsto\;
  \underbrace{\mathrm{diag}(\rho)}_{\text{local / Ricci}}
  +
  \underbrace{\rho - \mathrm{diag}(\rho)}_{\text{non-local / Weyl}},
\]
applied to compressed configurations of any physical degree of freedom,
produces the local/non-local split underlying both quantum field theory and
general relativity.
 
For fermionic configurations: diagonal gives Pauli exclusion, off-diagonal
gives the photon and Born rule.
For metric configurations: diagonal gives Ricci curvature and the Einstein
source term, off-diagonal gives the Weyl tensor, gravitational waves,
and --- integrated over spherical shells --- the Newtonian potential.
 
The unification is not a postulate.
It is the observation that quantum mechanics and general relativity are both
descriptions of the same density matrix decomposition applied to different
degrees of freedom, in a universe of $n$ bits.


\subsection{Why We Inevitably Find Ourselves in an Expanding Universe}

The lognormal matter abundance established in Paper~III is not the complete probability
measure over observer configurations. It is only one factor. The complete measure is:

\[
P(\text{observer at } t) \;\propto\; \underbrace{2^{-C_s(t)}}_{\text{Solomonoff weight}} \;\times\; \underbrace{|\mathcal{E}(t)|}_{\text{equivalence class size}}
\]

where $C_s(t)$ is the spectral complexity of the configuration at time $t$, and $|\mathcal{E}(t)|$ is the number of distinct bitstring arrangements that realise the same observer — the same internal structure, the same Alice.

The Solomonoff weight alone would place every observer at the singularity: $C_s = 0$, weight $= 1$, maximum probability.
But the singularity supports no observer, because zero entropy means no internal distinctions, no equivalence class, no structure.

The equivalence class size $|\mathcal{E}(t)|$ is the lognormal of this paper: it peaks on the rising side of the entropy curve,
where structures are abundant and complex enough to support an internal observer.

Crucially, however, the singularity also dominates the equivalence class measure.
The zero-entropy state can be realised in $2^n$ ways — every bitstring maps to it under the zero-entropy equivalence relation,
because all zero-entropy states are identical.
Any more complex structure is exponentially suppressed in the number of realisations:

\[
|\mathcal{E}(\text{singularity})| = 2^n, \qquad
|\mathcal{E}(\text{complex structure})| \ll 2^n.
\]

The combined measure at the singularity is therefore $2^{-0} \times 2^n = 2^n$ --- enormous,
but entirely observer-free.
The first configuration on the rising side of the lognormal that supports an observer of sufficient complexity is where we inevitably find ourselves.

The rising side of the lognormal is, by definition, an expanding universe:
entropy is increasing, structures are forming, free fabric is being consumed.
The falling side — where structures dissolve and the probability gradient points back toward the singularity — is the interior of a black hole.
A black hole and the observable universe are the same $2^n$ configurations,
traversed from opposite sides of the lognormal peak.

We inevitably find ourselves from an expanding universe.



\subsection{What is not yet shown}
 
The present results are exact at the level of toy models: discrete sites,
conformal metrics, two-frame sequences, 1D chains.
The extension to the full tensorial Riemann curvature in four continuous
dimensions and the derivation of the Einstein field equations as
large-deviation minimisers of the cost functional require a treatment of
the continuum limit that is beyond the scope of this paper.
 
The proportionality $\|W\| \propto M$ assumed in Theorem~4 --- that the
graviton amplitude of a mass $M$ scales linearly with $M$ --- is
physically natural (more bits produce a stronger field) but has not been
derived from the counting equation alone.
A derivation from $n_{\mathrm{bh}} = \log_2(r_s/\ell_P)$ would close this gap.
 
\subsection{Relation to existing approaches}
 
Jacobson (1995) derived the Einstein equations from thermodynamics of local
Rindler horizons.
Verlinde (2011) derived Newtonian gravity from entropic forces.
The present approach differs: it derives geodesics, curvature decomposition,
graviton polarisations, and the Newtonian potential from spectral complexity
minimisation alone, without postulating thermodynamics, entropy, or holography.
These emerge as consequences, not inputs.

\subsection{Continuum limit and Einstein field equations}
 
The discrete toy model should converge to the Einstein field equations
in the continuum limit.
The natural candidate: the Einstein equations are the
large-deviation stationarity condition of the spectral complexity
functional over metric configurations, analogous to how the Euler--Lagrange
equations are the stationarity conditions of the action.
 
\subsection{Electromagnetic potential}
 
The same argument applied to the photon residual of Paper~VII should give
the Coulomb potential $V_{\mathrm{EM}}(r) = e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)$.
The ratio $V_{\mathrm{EM}}/V_{\mathrm{grav}}$ would then give the ratio
of electromagnetic to gravitational coupling --- a derivation of the
hierarchy problem from compression geometry.
 
\subsection{Connection to the Friedmann equation}
 
The relational scale factor $R(t)$ of Paper~V \cite{meskanen2026v} maps onto the Friedmann
equation under explicit variable identifications.
The present results suggest the lognormal matter abundance curves of
Paper~V are themselves $C_s$-minimising worldlines in the space of
metric configurations, which would promote the Friedmann correspondence
from a structural identification to a theorem.
