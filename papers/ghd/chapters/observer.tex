\chapter{Observers}

\section{Definition of Observer}

A full theory of everything should explain not only the observed universe but also the observer in the universe to be complete toe.

Observations (measurements) in Quantum Mechanics play an essential role. In quantum mechanics, an observer need not be conscious;
it is any system capable of interacting with a quantum system in a way that produces a definite outcome.
In other words, an observer is a physical entity that stores, processes, or records information about the universe.

Living organisms trying to develope theories of everything are a particularly sophisticated class of observers: they maintain their own structure, store information, and adapt to ensure their survival.

Modern science has not produced a universally accepted definition of life, but certain characteristics appear repeatedly across all
known living systems.

Life stores information, maintains internal structure against the tendency of entropy to increase, and operates as a self-organizing system capable of adaptation and evolution.

Due to second law of thermodynamics complex systems tend naturally toward disorder rather than order.
From a probabilistic perspective, the spontaneous emergence of highly organized and intelligent structures in a random environment is extraordinarily unlikely.
Evolution provides the only known mechanism capable of overcoming this statistical barrier.
By favoring structures that better preserve and replicate information, evolutionary processes gradually accumulate complexity and functionality.
Intelligence can therefore be understood as a powerful adaptive feature that allows living systems to maintain and defend their informational structure in a hostile, entropy-driven environment.

Living systems must possess stable mechanisms for storing information, protecting that information from environmental noise, and interacting with their surroundings in a way that allows them to sense and maintain their own structural integrity.
These requirements impose constraints not only on biological systems but also on the physical environment in which such systems can exist.

The importance of maintaining informational boundaries becomes evident when we consider how fragile living structures are in hostile environments.
A living organism is not merely a collection of atoms; it is an organized arrangement of matter that encodes and preserves information about its structure and function.
When the boundary between the organism and its environment fails, that information rapidly degrades.
For example, if a human toe is exposed to a sufficiently strong acid, the chemical reactions at the interface will begin to break down the molecular structures that maintain the tissue’s organization.
Proteins denature, cell membranes rupture, and the biological information stored in those structures is lost.
The atoms themselves remain, but the organized system they formed ceases to exist.
This illustrates a general principle: life depends not only on internal complexity but also on the ability to maintain a stable boundary that protects its informational structure from environmental processes that tend to dissolve order into disorder.


\section{Evolution}

While Charles Darwin's theory of evolution has been subject to heavy
religion-saturated debate in the past, believing in it is no longer a
question of faith. Modern science has revealed the genetic code in DNA
and confirmed the theory for all its essential parts. It appears that
all life on Earth shares a common ancestor.

There is a science fiction book, \emph{The Black Cloud} \cite{hoyle1957blackcloud},
in which an intelligent cloud arrives and proceeds to cause all sorts
of trouble. The reason the book is science fiction rather than science,
and why intelligent clouds cannot exist in real life, is that there are
no known laws of physics on which such a cloud could plausibly be based.

The more intelligent and complex a system is, the smaller the
probability that it could simply appear spontaneously. For a giant
intelligent cloud, the probability should be practically zero.

The only theory we currently have that explains the existence of
truly intelligent systems is evolution. For evolution to work there
must be many candidates and a mechanism of natural selection capable
of eliminating the less successful ones. In the case of intelligent
clouds, there would have to be lots of clouds, and natural selection
would need to eliminate the weak and disorganized ones while favoring
those capable of maintaining structure. Only then could intelligent
clouds gradually develop.

But how would evolution work for a cloud whose behavior is governed
by the Navier--Stokes equations of fluid dynamics? How could such a
cloud keep its information in order? What would happen if a storm
passed by and blew the cloud apart?

Even relatively small disturbances to a human brain can cause severe
damage. A violent disturbance would correspond to putting a brain into
a kitchen blender and switching it on. It is not difficult to see why
the brain would not think very clearly afterward.

In principle one could imagine some unknown form of matter with
properties suitable for maintaining information in a gaseous state.
However, astronomical observations give us little support for this
idea. When we analyze the spectrum of light coming from anywhere in
the universe—even from the most distant galaxies—we see essentially
the same electromagnetic fingerprints. This tells us that they are
made of the same raw materials as our own home sweet Milky Way.

An intelligent cloud would need to know its boundaries and maintain
a stable internal structure. A gas cloud has neither. It would not
remain organized long enough to evolve intelligence.

Intelligence requires stable structures capable of storing and
protecting information. Turbulent gases are exceptionally poor at
doing this. They certainly would not survive long enough to play ice hockey.



\section{Alien Life}

Occasionally, scientists suggest that life could be far more common than generally believed and might not necessarily be carbon-based. However, the challenge is that Earth appears to be an ideal habitat for diverse life forms. If life emerged easily, shouldn't we observe "exotic" organisms—perhaps non-DNA-based—coexisting with us? Instead, we find only one biochemical lineage, all rooted in DNA and evolution. Furthermore, our exploration of the solar system and our extensive monitoring of radio signals (SETI) have yielded no traces of life, intelligent or otherwise.

Exotic life could be here, but we simply aren't looking for it correctly.
Most of our tools (PCR, DNA sequencing) are designed specifically to find DNA. If a "non-DNA" microbe existed in the dirt, our current tests would likely dismiss it as "non-living" chemical noise.


Perhaps life exists on a different temporal scale. If their biological processes run significantly faster or slower than ours, we might perceive them as either stationary matter or a blur too rapid to recognize as sentient. However, while time-scale differences are a great sci-fi concept, biology is bound by the laws of thermodynamics and chemistry. Chemical reactions (the basis of life) happen at specific rates dictated by temperature and molecular stability. A creature "moving too fast" would likely burn up from the heat of its own metabolism; one "moving too slow" might not be able to gather enough energy to maintain its structure against entropy.


\section{Super-Symmetric Life}


The vast majority of matter in the universe consists of "dark matter," the nature of which remains one of science's greatest mysteries. String theory predicts a set of supersymmetric particles that could account for this phenomenon. If these particles are capable of forming complex structures—analogous to atoms and molecules—then it is statistically plausible (given that dark matter is five times more prevalent than visible matter) that "dark life" exists. We may be sharing the universe with an entire dark ecology that remains completely invisible to our senses.

Again, physics fights back. Dark matter (and most predicted supersymmetric particles) is collisionless.
It doesn't interact with electromagnetism, which means it doesn't "clump" the way normal matter does.

To have life, one needs complex molecules. Normal matter forms molecules because electrons attract and repel each other.
Since dark matter doesn't seem to interact via the electromagnetic force, it can't form "dark atoms" or "dark DNA."
It mostly just passes through itself and us like a ghost. Without a way to bond particles together, one can't build a "dark person."

Even if life could exist in exotic forms, the laws of physics restrict what counts as a stable observer.


\section{Spiritual Life}

If the Bible and other religious sources are to be believed, intelligent life
can exist in spiritual form—such as God, angels, and perhaps even Satan himself.

According to the Bible, God created mankind in His own image; in the image
of God He created them—male and female He created them.

Humans are undoubtedly a social species, which implies that God must also
have a social nature. But this raises a question: if God were the only one,
how could such a magnificent system have arisen? The probability of the
spontaneous creation of an omnipotent being is even smaller than the
spontaneous formation of a smart cloud governed by Navier–Stokes equations.
If there were only one God, how did He develop social properties that
normally emerge only in species living in groups?

All intelligence we observe on Earth is the result of a long process of evolution.
It would therefore be easier to accept the existence of highly developed
omnipotent creatures if they were subject to natural selection. However, for
evolution to operate, there would need to have been many Gods and a sufficiently
hostile environment to drive their development.

According to the Bible, there is only one God, so apparently something went
wrong, leaving only one God behind. He became lonely. Being a
social animal, God desired companionship, and thus created humanity.

This perspective may explain the friendliness of God. If only one God existed,
why would He display traits typical of social animals? He could act entirely
for Himself. If only one God were spontaneously created, it would be difficult
to explain how He became not only omnipotent but also socially attuned. Perhaps
Gods, forgetting their social origins, turned individualistic and annihilated
one another using blackhole bombs in the Cosmic Conflict-1.

By sending His son to Earth, God may be teaching humanity the value of social
cooperation, ensuring that we do not destroy one another as the Gods once did.

\subsection*{Eternal Universe}

According to Stephen Hawking, the universe could simply exist, and therefore
did not require a creator. If the universe can exist uncaused, perhaps God
can exist in the same way: self-existent and uncreated.  

Both constructions—an eternal universe or a self-existent God—are
extraordinary in their own right. One can attempt to evaluate which
is more probable. The greater the complexity and intelligence of a structure,
the smaller the chance of its spontaneous existence.

\begin{tcolorbox}[title=The Proportion for God]
\begin{equation}
\frac{\text{God exists}}{\text{Universe exists}} =
\frac{\text{Probability of God}}{\text{Probability of Universe}}
\end{equation}
\end{tcolorbox}

In other words, the ratio of the complexity of God to that of the universe
is inversely proportional to the ratio of their probabilities.

If God is capable of creating the universe, then His structure is likely
more evolved and intelligent than the universe itself. Correspondingly, the
probability of God's spontaneous existence is smaller than that of the universe.

\subsection*{The Omnipotence Paradox}

Stephen Hawking \cite{hawking1988brief} also commented on God by asking
whether God could create a stone too heavy for Him to lift.

From a physical perspective, lifting is a process of applying force to an object:
\begin{equation}
F = ma
\end{equation}

If the applied force \(F\) exceeds the gravitational force on the rock,
the rock can be lifted.

How strong can an almighty being be? By definition, God is infinitely strong.
Otherwise, there would exist a rock He could not lift, contradicting omnipotence.

Similarly, God would need to be able to create infinitely massive objects.
Thus, asking whether God can create a stone He cannot lift is equivalent
to asking whether an infinite force can lift an infinite mass.

Mathematics cannot fully answer this question. Possible resolutions include:
omnipotence does not exist, the question is nonsensical, or our understanding
of infinity is incomplete.


\section{Conclusions}

Life, intelligence, and consciousness are all manifestations of observers in the universe.
While physics provides constraints on what observers can exist and how they process information, many questions remain unanswered: the precise nature of consciousness, the emergence of intelligence, and the ultimate limits of observation.
Understanding the observer is therefore central to any theory of everything: without it, our physical theories remain incomplete.

If physics cannot give answers to these big questions; what is mean to be alive, what is the
equation of consciousness and pain, then what else is left?
