\chapter{Quantum Mechanics}


\section{The Strange Quantum World}

Quantum Mechanics is extraordinarily successful at describing microscopic phenomena. 
Its predictions have been confirmed to astonishing precision. Yet conceptually, it resists classical intuition. 

At small scales, matter does not behave like tiny billiard balls. 
Instead, it behaves according to a complex-valued object called the \emph{wavefunction}.

\subsection*{The Wavefunction}

The \emph{wavefunction} is an abstract entity: it possesses no mass, no electric charge, and it does not emit or absorb light.
It cannot be directly measured by any instrument. Yet, it exerts fundamental control over the universe at its smallest scales.

Every isolated quantum system is described by a state vector 
\[
|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H},
\]
where $\mathcal{H}$ is a Hilbert space. 

The wavefunction evolves deterministically according to the Schrödinger equation:

\[
i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H} |\psi(t)\rangle.
\]

This evolution is linear and unitary. No randomness appears at this level.


\subsection*{The Measurement}

When we perform a measurement, we do not observe the complex-valued wavefunction directly. Instead, we observe a definite, real-valued
outcome, a particle.

The double-slit experiment illustrates this vividly. 
Photons sent one by one through two slits produce an interference pattern. 
No classical particle passing through one slit at a time could generate such a pattern. 
What propagates through both slits is the \emph{wavefunction}. 
The particle becomes localized only upon detection.

Thus, there is a dual structure:

\begin{itemize}
\item Continuous, nonlocal, linear, complex-valued unitary evolution of wavefunction living in Hilbert space.
\item Discrete, localized classical measurement events in spacetime.
\end{itemize}

The wavefunction, despites its abstract nature, governs the probabilities of all physical events.  It encodes the potential behaviors of particles, dictating outcomes without being a tangible object itself.

This is particle–wave duality.


\subsection*{Superposition as Informational Encoding}

Consider a quantum coin \(\psi\rangle = \alpha \):

\[
|\psi\rangle = \alpha |H\rangle + \beta |T\rangle,
\quad
|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1.
\]

The system is not “half head and half tail” in a classical sense. 
Instead, the wavefunction encodes \emph{both possibilities simultaneously} 
within a single mathematical object.

Superposition allows interference. 
Amplitudes combine before probabilities are extracted:

\[
P = |\alpha + \beta|^2.
\]

This structure is crucial. The wavefunction does not store outcomes separately. 
It stores them in compressed, phase-sensitive form.

If one were to list all classical alternatives explicitly, 
the information content would scale exponentially with system size.
Instead, the Hilbert space vector stores all alternatives 
in a linear superposition.


\subsection*{Identity of Quantum Particles}

Quantum particles lack classical individuality.  
Electrons, for example, are indistinguishable not merely in practice, but in principle.  

The particle you send here and detect there cannot be said to be the same particle.  
In the quantum world, particles lack what we ordinarily call \emph{identity}.  

In Hilbert space, exchanging two identical particles corresponds 
to an operator acting on the state vector.

For fermions (matter particles):

\begin{equation}
\psi(x_1, x_2) = -\psi(x_2, x_1).
\end{equation}

If $x_1 = x_2$, then:

\begin{equation}
\psi(x, x) = 0.
\end{equation}

The Pauli exclusion principle follows directly. 
No two identical fermions can occupy the same quantum state.

For bosons (force carrying particles):

\begin{equation}
\psi(x_1, x_2) = +\psi(x_2, x_1).
\end{equation}

Multiple occupation is allowed, enabling coherent states and 
Bose–Einstein condensation.

Identity is therefore encoded algebraically, not geometrically. 
The symmetry properties of Hilbert space replace classical individuality.

\subsection*{Uncertainty and Localization}

The Born rule states:

\[
P(x,t) = |\psi(x,t)|^2.
\]

Probability is extracted from amplitude magnitude.

Consider a plane wave:

\[
\psi(x) = e^{ikx}.
\]

Momentum is precise: $p = \hbar k$.  
But position is completely delocalized.

To localize a particle, we must superpose many momenta:

\[
\psi(x) = \int a(k)e^{ikx} dk.
\]

The sharper the position localization, the broader the momentum distribution.

This is the content of the uncertainty relation:

\[
\Delta x \Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}.
\]

Localization requires informational expansion in momentum space.

In other words, precise position requires many Fourier components.
This is the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. It is not merely a limitation of our measuring devices, but a fundamental property of nature.



\subsection*{Entanglement}

Consider two particles in the joint state:

\[
|\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} 
\left(
| \uparrow \downarrow \rangle 
-
| \downarrow \uparrow \rangle
\right).
\]

This state cannot be factorized:

\[
|\Psi\rangle \neq |\psi_1\rangle \otimes |\psi_2\rangle.
\]

The system is described by a single vector in a tensor-product space. 
The subsystems do not possess independent states.


\section{Quantum Field Theory (QFT)}

Quantum Field Theory (QFT) represents our most sophisticated understanding of the subatomic world. In the QFT framework, space is not an empty void; instead, every point in the universe is permeated by fields. A helpful (regularized) analogy is to imagine space filled with an infinite grid of harmonic oscillators, each connected to its nearest neighbors. 

The dynamics of such a field in flat spacetime $\phi(x)$ are often described by a Hamiltonian density, representing the total energy of these oscillators:
\[ \mathcal{H} = \frac{1}{2} \Pi^2 + \frac{1}{2} (\nabla \phi)^2 + \frac{1}{2} m^2 \phi^2 \]

Where $\Pi$ is the conjugate momentum and $m$ is the mass. When a "node" is disturbed, the resulting vibration propagates through the grid as a wave. In QFT, these waves—or excitations of the field—are what we perceive as particles.

The universe is composed of several such overlapping fields, e.g:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Higgs Field:} A scalar field $\phi$ that endows particles with mass through spontaneous symmetry breaking.
    \item \textbf{Electromagnetic Fields:} Vector fields $A_\mu$ (having both magnitude and direction) that govern light and electricity.
\end{itemize}

These fields interact where they overlap. For instance, an electron field "wiggling" can kick the photon field. This framework is remarkably precise, provided the stage—space itself—remains a flat Minkowski metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}$.



\section{The Measurement Problem}

Quantum mechanics introduces a subtle complication to the role of the observer, namely the measurement problem.

According to quantum theory, reality is described as a superposition of possibilities, all of which exist until they are ``observed.'' Upon observation, one of these possibilities is realized as a definite outcome.

The question then arises: what qualifies as an observer?

Several mathematically equivalent interpretations attempt to address this issue.
In the Copenhagen interpretation, the ``observer'' is often treated as a classical measurement apparatus. However, the boundary between the classical and quantum domains is not precisely defined.
In the Many-Worlds interpretation, no special role is assigned to observers; instead, they are simply quantum systems that become entangled with their environment.
In this view, observations give rise to branching universes. Other approaches, such as objective collapse theories and quantum information–based or relational interpretations, attempt to formalize the notion of measurement in different ways.

Despite these differences, the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics does not uniquely specify what constitutes an observer. Apparently a pain sensitive observer does, fish, a single molecule, electron, quark? 

In modern physics, an “observer” is effectively any system that becomes irreversibly entangled with another system in a way that produces a stable record of information.

But what the heck does this even mean? 


\section{Irreversible Recording in Quantum Mechanics}

\subsection*{1. Interaction + entanglement (the starting point)}

When a system \( S \) interacts with an apparatus \( A \), the joint state evolves as:

\[
|\psi_S\rangle \otimes |A_0\rangle \;\longrightarrow\; \sum_i c_i \, |s_i\rangle \otimes |A_i\rangle
\]

This is called unitary evolution in quantum mechanics.

At this stage:

\begin{itemize}
\item No irreversibility yet
\item No ``record'' in the classical sense yet
\item The system is just entangled
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{2. Reduced density matrix (what one actually observe)}

One  ``ignores'' the environment and looks at the system:

\[
\rho_S = \mathrm{Tr}_A(\rho_{SA})
\]

This is where information starts to look ``lost.''

\subsection*{3. Decoherence (the key step)}

Due to interaction with the environment \( E \), the off-diagonal terms vanish:

\[
\rho_S =
\begin{pmatrix}
\rho_{11} & \rho_{12} \\
\rho_{21} & \rho_{22}
\end{pmatrix}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\begin{pmatrix}
\rho_{11} & 0 \\
0 & \rho_{22}
\end{pmatrix}
\]

This suppression is typically modeled as:

\[
\rho_{ij}(t) \sim \rho_{ij}(0)\, e^{-\Gamma t}
\]

where:

\begin{itemize}
\item \( \Gamma \) = decoherence rate
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{4. Irreversibility = entropy increase}

Now one can define the key quantity:

\[
S(\rho) = -\mathrm{Tr}(\rho \log \rho)
\]

This is the von Neumann entropy.

Irreversibility corresponds to:

\[
\Delta S > 0
\]

This is the quantum analogue of the Second Law of Thermodynamics.

\subsection*{5. Information loss = irreversibility condition}

A more precise information-theoretic statement is:

\[
I(S:A) \downarrow \quad \text{and} \quad I(S:E) \uparrow
\]

\begin{itemize}
\item Information about the system becomes encoded in the environment
\item That information becomes practically unrecoverable
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{6. The key operational definition}

In physics, ``irreversible recording'' means:

\begin{quote}
A process in which information about a system becomes redundantly encoded in many environmental degrees of freedom, such that reversing the dynamics would require control over an exponentially large number of variables.
\end{quote}

\subsection*{7. Why this is effectively irreversible}

The full evolution is still unitary (reversible in principle), but:

\begin{itemize}
\item The environment is too large
\item Information is dispersed into inaccessible degrees of freedom
\end{itemize}

So irreversibility is:

\begin{itemize}
\item practical irreversibility, not fundamental irreversibility
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{8. One-line ``equation-level'' summary}

There is no single equation, but the closest compact mathematical statement is:

\[
\rho \;\xrightarrow{\text{decoherence}}\; \text{diagonalized } \rho, 
\qquad \Delta S = -\mathrm{Tr}(\rho \log \rho) > 0
\]

\subsection*{Bottom line}

``Irreversible recording'' in physics is not a fundamental primitive—it is the emergent result of entanglement, decoherence, and entropy increase, where information is transferred into environmental degrees of freedom in a way that cannot be practically reversed.

Finally a clear definition for us-conscious observers: we are systems that performs irreversible recordings.
