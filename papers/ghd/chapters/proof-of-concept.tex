\chapter{Proof of Concept}

\section{From Formal Theory to Physical Demonstration}

This is the moment of truth.

After decades of developing the informational framework, we finally ask the only question that matters: Can anything that looks like physics come out of it — using nothing but the compression measure and observer-conditioned probability?

The answer is... partially. What you are about to see is a real, working simulation. It succeeds in surprising ways and fails in instructive ones.

We built a first proof-of-concept simulation with a sharply defined goal:

\begin{quote}
Given only an observer-conditioned informational measure and a compression principle, does anything resembling quantum behavior, inertia, or gravitational attraction emerge — without being explicitly programmed?
\end{quote}

No forces, equations of motion, or spacetime structures are hard-coded. We are building a “Non-Physical Engine” — a system that attempts to discover physics rather than obey it.

\section{The Observer Proxy}

The theory is fundamentally observer-centric, but modeling a full biological observer is computationally impossible. So we use a simple proxy: a localized Gaussian wavepacket with memory — basically a small, disk-shaped “blob”.

The simulation explores possible “observer walks” (sequences of configurations picked up by this observer filter) and weights them according to the fundamental measure. Paths with the minimum joint description length dominate.

\section{Important Clarification}

It should be noted that we are not deriving quantum mechanics or general relativity from the informational measure.
Both are taken as observational facts: the microscopic world behaves like waves, and gravity manifests geometrically.
We simply use wavefunction descriptions and geometric descriptions as practical tools to calculate informational cost — the two most efficient languages reality has shown us.

The simulation then asks one question: which observer histories minimize the joint spectral + geometric description length?
Whatever behavior emerges does so because it is the cheapest way to exist and be describable.

\section{What the Simulation Actually Does}

Instead of relying on standard image compression algorithms, we implemented a true complex-valued wavefunction class that directly computes spectral complexity from frequencies, phases, and amplitudes.
On the geometric side, we use a heavily simplified, linearized approximation of curvature to measure geometric description cost.

The simulation uses a Metropolis-Hastings sampler to explore the enormous space of possible observer walks, guided by the principle of minimum total description length.

\section{Emergent Phenomena}

Despite the absence of any programmed forces or laws, the simulation shows several encouraging behaviors:

\begin{itemize}
\item {textbf(Inertia:} Localized blobs tend to keep moving in roughly straight lines because smooth, predictable paths have lower spectral description length.
\item{Quantum Interference:} Wave-like interference patterns appear naturally as compression artifacts of complex-valued wavefunction.
\item{Weak Attraction:} The blobs show a statistical tendency to move toward one another. Configurations in which they are closer together often turn out to have lower joint description length than when they are far apart.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Limitation}

However, there is a major catch.

The three Gaussian blobs (our proxy observers, or “Alices”) eventually lose their individual identities and fuse into a single undifferentiated entity. There are no stable collisions or rebounds. The distinct informational structures simply blend together and become irretrievably mixed.
For any real world Alice this would have catastropic consequences.

In short: we see bosonic-like wave behavior and some effective attraction, but we see no sign of fermionic statistics or anything resembling the Pauli exclusion principle. Without a natural mechanism that keeps localized structures distinct, complex material reality remains out of reach.
