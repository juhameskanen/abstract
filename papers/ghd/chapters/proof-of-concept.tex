\chapter{Proof of Concept}

\section{From Theory to Simulation}

The ultimate test of any theory is whether it can produce something that resembles the world we observe, using only its own principles.

We built a minimal proof-of-concept simulation with a clear question:

\begin{quote}
Given \emph{only} an observer-conditioned compression measure, can anything resembling inertia, quantum interference, or mutual attraction emerge — without being explicitly programmed?
\end{quote}

No forces, equations of motion, spacetime metric, or physical laws were hard-coded. The system is a pure ``Non-Physical Engine'' that selects configurations solely according to the joint spectral-geometric complexity measure.

\section{The Observer Proxy}

Full biological observers are computationally intractable, so we use a simple proxy: a localized Gaussian wave packet with limited internal memory — essentially a small, coherent ``blob''. The blob size and memory capacity are adjustable parameters.

The simulation explores possible ``observer walks'' (sequences of configurations compatible with the blob) and selects them according to the induced observer-conditioned measure $P(c \mid O)$.

\section{Implementation}

We implemented a complex-valued wavefunction class that computes spectral complexity directly from frequencies, phases, and amplitudes. Geometric complexity is approximated via a linearized measure of curvature and boundary sharpness.

A Metropolis-Hastings sampler explores the space, guided exclusively by minimization of total description length.

\section{Emergent Phenomena}

Even with this minimal setup, several non-trivial behaviors emerge:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Inertia and Smooth Motion:} Blobs naturally follow smooth, curved trajectories. Sharp changes in direction require high-frequency spectral components, which carry high description cost and are heavily suppressed.
    \item \textbf{Weak Mutual Attraction:} Configurations in which blobs are closer together often have lower joint spectral-geometric complexity, leading to a statistical tendency to cluster.
    \item \textbf{Wave-like Interference:} Interference patterns appear naturally from the complex-valued wavefunction representation.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Fermion Problem}

Despite these successes, a major limitation appears: the Gaussian blobs eventually lose their individual identities and merge into a single undifferentiated structure. There is no stable repulsion or exclusion at close range.

In standard quantum mechanics, fermions obey the Pauli exclusion principle due to antisymmetry of the wavefunction. In our purely compression-based measure, symmetric (bosonic) configurations are strongly favored because they allow shared spectral modes and lower total description length. Antisymmetric configurations appear to require significantly higher complexity, and thus are exponentially suppressed.

Hard-coding antisymmetry would violate the spirit of the project. The central open challenge is therefore to find a natural mechanism within the compression measure that makes multiple occupancy of identical states dramatically more expensive when coherent observers are present.

This is currently the most important open problem of the theory.

\section{Conclusion}

The simulation demonstrates that key features of classical and quantum behavior (inertia, interference, weak attraction) can emerge from pure informational compression and observer conditioning. However, stable distinguishable matter requires something resembling fermionic statistics.

The hunt for emergent exclusion within a pure description-length measure is now the primary frontier.
