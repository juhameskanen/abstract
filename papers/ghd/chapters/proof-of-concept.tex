\chapter{Proof of Concept}

\section{From Formal Theory to Physical Demonstration}

The preceding chapters derive the theory at the level of axioms, measures, and limiting principles.
At this stage, the theory must be tested operationally: we must demonstrate that its defining assumptions are sufficient to produce structures resembling observed physics.

To this end, we construct a proof-of-concept simulation.
The purpose of this simulation is sharply defined:
\begin{quote}
Given only an observer-conditioned informational measure and a compression principle, do effective quantum behavior, inertia, and gravitational geometry emerge without being explicitly imposed?
\end{quote}
No forces, equations of motion, or spacetime structures are hard-coded.

This is the ultimate test of the theory. We are effectively building a ``Non-Physical Engine''---a system that discovers physics rather than obeying it.

\section{The Definition of the Observer}

The theory is fundamentally observer-centric; we assume the existence of a configuration $c$ such that $\mu_O(c) > 0$.
Modeling a biological observer is computationally prohibitive.
We therefore utilize a simplified proxy: let the observer be a \textbf{Gaussian wavepacket} with memory---a localized, two-dimensional geometric disk-shaped object. 

At the implementation level, this requires specifying:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Informational Capacity:} A finite number of bits $n$.
    \item \textbf{Observer Wavefunction ($\psi_O$):} A minimal observer filter defining what constitutes observer continuity across configurations.
\end{itemize}

The simulation explores the history space $\Gamma_O$ containing such an observer.
For each compatible configuration, all admissible observer walks (orderings of informational states matching the observer filter) are evaluated by the fundamental measure:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) = \frac{1}{Z_O} \exp\left(-\lambda\mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]$ is the joint spectral and geometric description length.
The configuration sequence admitting the \textbf{Minimum Description Length (MDL)} dominates the measure.


\section{Spectral Complexity and the Wavefunction Class}

To remain fully faithful to the informational ontology, we eschew standard lossy compression libraries (such as DCT-based JPEG or MPEG). While these algorithms are highly optimized for human perception, they utilize heuristic quantization tables and block-based encodings that do not correspond to the universal informational substrate. 

Instead, we implement a dedicated \textbf{Wavefunction Class} where the complexity $\mathcal{C}_Q(\psi)$ is computed directly from the spectral decomposition. In our simulation, the complexity of a state is defined as the total bit-count required to specify its active modes:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_Q(\psi) = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} \left[ \log_2(\omega_k) + \text{bits}(\phi_k) + \text{bits}(A_k) \right]
\end{equation}

where $\omega_k$ is the frequency, $\phi_k$ the phase, and $A_k$ the amplitude of the $k$-th mode. This formulation ensures that high-frequency modes (high $\omega_k$) inherently carry a higher informational cost, providing the mathematical basis for both \textbf{inertia} and the \textbf{low-pass filtering} of singularities.

\subsection*{Spectral Complexity Measure}

It would be tempting to use an existing standard MPEG/JPG Discrete Cosine Transforms (DCT) implementation to measure the spectral complexity.
However, DCT present two significant issues for a foundational physics simulation:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Discontinuity:} The DCT is defined over discrete blocks. Transitioning between blocks introduces high-frequency "edge artifacts". This would create artificial "shaking" in the observer's perceived geometry.
    \item \textbf{Non-Analytic Complexity:} In standard compression, complexity is often measured by the final size of a Huffman-encoded bitstream. This is a discontinuous function; a tiny change in a pixel can cause a "spike" in the compressed size. 
\end{enumerate}

So we implement real complex-valued wavefunction class with proper spectral complexity method, to ensure that the cost function is a smooth, differentiable functional based
on the real informational cost required to encode phases/frequencies in the wavefunction.

This allows the Metropolis-Hastings sampler to navigate the configuration space effectively, as the "gradient of simplicity" points clearly toward smooth, law-like trajectories rather than being lost in the algorithmic noise of a standard compression codec.


\section*{Basis Optimization and the Minimum Basis Principle}

A fundamental challenge arises from the non-uniqueness of spectral representations: any complex-valued wavefunction can be decomposed into an infinite variety of basis sets.
In standard quantum mechanics, the choice of basis (e.g., position vs. momentum) is arbitrary. However, in an informational ontology, the choice of basis is \textbf{ontologically decisive}.

To remain faithful to the MDL principle, the simulation must not assume a fixed basis. Instead, it must satisfy the \textbf{Minimum Basis Principle}:

\begin{quote}
The perceived reality is defined by the specific spectral basis that minimizes the joint description length of the observer and their environment.
\end{quote}


\subsection*{The Basis Search as Compression}

If Alice can be described as a sum of 10 modes in Basis A, but requires $10^6$ modes in Basis B, the measure $\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O)$ exponentially favors Basis A.
This explains why we perceive a world of discrete "particles" and "objects" rather than a smeared-out soup of frequencies: \textbf{Objectivity is a compression artifact.}
We perceive "things" because "things" are the most efficient basis for describing the correlations in the bitstring.


\subsection*{Implementation in the Simulation}

In the \texttt{qbitwave.py} class, the \texttt{complexity()} method does not simply sum a fixed set of Fourier coefficients.
Instead, it searches for the transform that yields the highest sparsity (minimal active modes). 

This is computationally expensive but necessary to demonstrate the emergence of physical constants.
The "Best Basis" typically aligns with the geometric symmetries of the observer, effectively deriving \textbf{Noether's Theorem} from informational efficiency:
symmetries in the bits lead to sparse bases, which lead to conserved quantities in the observer's experience.


\section{The Combinatorial Barrier and Heuristics}

A system with $n$ bits admits $2^n$ informational states, and the number of possible observer walks is $(2^n)!$.
To navigate this super-exponential growth, we apply a ``Search Heuristic'' to the Solomonoff Prior.
We are not ``coding'' physics; we are pruning configurations that have zero measure. 

The optimizations follow directly from the theory:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Entropy Gradient:} Observer walks with global entropy decrease are excluded, as they represent informationally expensive ``reversals'' that are exponentially suppressed.
    \item \textbf{Local Smoothness:} Sampling is restricted to geometrically coherent configurations.
      This corresponds to the \textbf{Large-Deviation Principle}, where we concentrate our search on the peak of the probability distribution.
\end{itemize}

\section{Linearized General Relativity}

To implement a practical simulation of geometry, we linearize the MDL-based GR functional around a flat Minkowski background:
\begin{equation}
g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}, \quad |h_{\mu\nu}| \ll 1
\end{equation}
where $h_{\mu\nu}$ represents small perturbations encoding deviations from flat spacetime. 

\subsection*{Linearized Geometric Cost}
We implement the variation of the $R^2$ functional.
Expanding the curvature proxy $R$ linearly, the geometric cost gradient $C_G$ simplifies to a form driven by the Laplacian of the field:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\delta C_G}{\delta h_{\mu\nu}} \approx \nabla^2 h_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}
The simulation reaches equilibrium when the geometric ``stiffness'' balances the informational density of the observer.

\subsection*{Linearized Worldline / Matter Term}
The matter encoding cost for point particles reduces to the \textbf{worldline cost functional} in the weak-field:
\begin{equation}
C_Q[\{x_a\} \mid h] \approx \beta \sum_a \int \left( \ddot{x}_a^\mu + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu\rho} \dot{x}_a^\nu \dot{x}_a^\rho \right)^2 dt
\end{equation}
where the Christoffel symbols are approximated linearly:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma^\mu_{\nu\rho}[h] \approx \frac{1}{2} \eta^{\mu\sigma} (\partial_\nu h_{\sigma\rho} + \partial_\rho h_{\sigma\nu} - \partial_\sigma h_{\nu\rho})
\end{equation}
This ensures that the ``cheapest'' paths in the simulation are approximate geodesics, justifying the discrete trajectory updates in the code.

\section{Simulation Architecture}

The simulation engine (\texttt{simulation\_engine.py}) proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Initialize the observer state as a localized wavepacket ($\psi_O$).
    \item Sample admissible observer walks using a \textbf{Metropolis-Hastings} path-integral approach, weighting trajectories by their informational action.
    \item Compute spectral ($C_Q$) and geometric ($C_G$) compressions.
    \item Identify the dominant structure: the path where $\mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]$ is minimized.
\end{enumerate}
