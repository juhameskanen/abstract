\chapter{Deep Nature of Quantum Mechanics}

\section{Introduction}

Now that we are, at least in spirit, fully trained practitioners of quantum mechanics, let us ask: what is the deep nature of the theory and its mysterious wavefunction? 

Why does everything at the subatomic level appear to behave like a wave?

If we view the system from a distance, we observe two things:
First, the microscopic building blocks are simple—a small set of fields with just a few parameters producing enormous emergent richness.
Second, the complex-valued amplitudes in the wavefunction do all the heavy lifting.
Interference, entanglement, tunneling and superpositions are simply consequences of existing within a complex-valued Hilbert space. 

If we don’t pay attention to the above-mentioned little details, what remains?


\subsection*{The Movie Analogy}

In a conventional MPEG-compressed movie, each frame is composed of pixels. If we were observers made of pixels living inside such a movie, what would we perceive as the "laws of nature"?

We would observe that our constituent pixels follow mysterious, abstract, yet deterministic wave-like patterns. These patterns are the result of the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) or Fourier transforms—the underlying mathematical tools that MPEG codecs use to compress raw pixels into a maximally dense format for efficient storage and transmission.

From this perspective, the Quantum Mechanical wavefunction is simply an advanced, complex-valued version of the sinusoidal building blocks used in JPEG or MPEG images.

Just as a Fourier transform spreads image information across coefficients, the wavefunction distributes amplitude across
basis states in Hilbert space.


\section{The Dithering Analogy}

Consider a graphics engine rendering a perfectly smooth, analytically defined sphere with an intended intensity of 0.85.
If the display hardware is limited to discrete intensities of 0.8 and 0.9, it cannot render the true value.
To compensate, the software employs dithering—a probabilistic rule that assigns colors to pixels such that the average intensity across
the surface approximates the ideal value, smoothing out banding and moiré patterns.

In this light, the quantum state:

\[
\psi = \alpha |0.8\rangle + \beta |0.9\rangle
\]

can be viewed as nature’s highly advanced, complex-valued version of this optimization.

Just as dithering maximizes perceived image quality while minimizing hardware resources, the Born Rule allows the universe to maintain a high-resolution,
compressed informational state within a framework of limited physical observables.

Quantum mechanics may simply be the most efficient rendering engine possible—using complex-valued probability amplitudes to maximize
the fidelity of the universe with a minimal expenditure of underlying information.


\section{Spectral Complexity}

Kolmogorov complexity, as utilized in Algorithmic Information Theory (AIT), provides a theoretical foundation for minimal description length.
However, it is neither computable nor continuous, which limits its utility as a functional physical principle. 

Observational evidence suggests that physical systems already possess a natural, inherent mode of representation: their decomposition into \textbf{spectral modes}. This motivates the replacement of abstract algorithmic complexity with a physically grounded alternative:

\textbf{Spectral Complexity ($C_s$):} The total information required to specify the amplitudes, frequencies, and phases of the modes composing a physical state.


\subsection*{The Cost Function}

If we implement a wavefunction as a computational class, the complexity is determined by the bits required to instantiate its constituent modes.
We can model the total spectral complexity $C_s$ of a state $\Psi$ as the sum of its algorithmic overhead and the parameters of its $N$ modes:

\[
C_s(\Psi) = \text{Cost}(\text{base}) + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ \text{Cost}(\phi_i) + \text{Cost}(A_i) + \log_2\left(\frac{\omega_i}{\Delta\omega}\right) \right]
\]

Where:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{$\text{Cost}(\text{base})$}: Represents the constant $O(1)$ cost of the `sin()` method. In a universe-scale description, this is vanishingly small and treated as a constant.
\item \textbf{$\text{Cost}(\phi_i)$}: The bit-cost of the phase. Since phase is bounded within $[0, 2\pi]$, this is a small, fixed-width cost per mode.
\item \textbf{$\text{Cost}(A_i)$}: The information required to encode the amplitude.
\item \textbf{$\log_2(\omega_i / \Delta\omega)$}: The dominant term. It represents the number of bits required to specify the frequency $\omega$ relative to the system's minimum resolution $\Delta\omega$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Solomonoff Suppression}

One might argue that a continuous universe implies an infinite number of modes, leading to infinite complexity.
However, within the framework of AIT, these infinities are naturally suppressed. According to the \textbf{Solomonoff Prior},
the probability $P$ of a state is exponentially inversely proportional to its description length $L$:

\[
P(s) \approx 2^{-L(s)}
\]


High-frequency "noise" and infinite-mode configurations are so spectrally expensive that their probability of manifestation vanishes. We do not find ourselves in a chaotic, infinitely complex universe for the same reason we do not find ourselves inside an uncompressed, raw-pixel movie: the simplest, most compressed descriptions are the ones that dominate reality. 


\section{Proof of Concept: Emergent Order through Spectral Compression}

To test the validity of the Compression Hypothesis, a series of computational simulations were conducted to contrast purely probabilistic entropy growth with growth constrained by spectral efficiency. 

\subsection*{From Nothing to Something}

The initial simulation modeled a system evolving from a zero-entropy state toward high-entropy configurations.
In this baseline, information was mapped to geometric spacetime through all possible permutations without a compression filter.
While this resulted in expanding "universes," the outcomes were dominated by chaotic, incoherent structures and "Boltzmann-style" fluctuations.
These results lacked the large-scale symmetry and smooth geometric continuity observed in our own cosmos.

\subsection*{The Wavefunction Compression Model}

The simulation was subsequently restructured to incorporate the principle of \textbf{Spectral Complexity}.
In this iteration, the evolution from zero entropy to higher entropy states was filtered through a complex-valued wavefunction representation.
This acted as a compression scheme, encoding all possible paths of increasing entropy into a spectral form.

According to the modified principles of Solomonoff induction, the "measure" of any given universe is determined by its compressibility.
Therefore, the most compressible configurations—those defined by law-like regularity and symmetry—dominate the probability space.

\subsection*{Results}

Upon performing random samples from this spectral distribution, the results shifted dramatically:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Emergence of Smoothness:} The chaotic, high-entropy noise of the baseline was replaced by smoothly evolving structures.
    \item \textbf{Geometric Symmetry:} Symmetric geometric shapes and interconnected "net-like" structures emerged spontaneously as the most probable outcomes.
    \item \textbf{Predictability as Measure:} The simulation confirms that law-like universes are not "fine-tuned" by chance, but are the most compressible, and hence the most probable configurations within a timeless superposition.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusions}

We have landed on a many-worlds-style view of quantum mechanics — all possible configurations exist simultaneously in a vast static superposition — but without any dynamic branching in an external time.
Each configuration corresponds to what an Everettian observer would experience as a separate world.
Together they form the timeless universe described by the Wheeler-DeWitt equation, emerging purely from information.

Quantum mechanics may be viewed fundamentally as a theory of representation.
The wavefunction encodes physical reality in a continuous, spectral form that naturally supports superposition, interference, and entanglement.

By replacing the uncomputable Kolmogorov notion of complexity with spectral complexity, we establish a bridge between physical structure and informational description. The remaining components of Algorithmic Information Theory (AIT) still apply. Specifically, Solomonoff induction explains why we find ourselves within a law-like, smooth universe governed by symmetries, rather than manifesting as chaotic Boltzmann brains.

In this view, the wave-like nature of the microscopic world is not a mystery, but a reflection of how information is most effectively organized.

The cosmos waves because we are observing compressed structure.

The wavefunction is the compression scheme of the universe.
