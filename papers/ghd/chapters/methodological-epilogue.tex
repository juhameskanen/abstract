\chapter{The Problem of Being Inside the System}


\section{The Internal Perspective}

Physics has a fundamental problem: we are trying to describe a system from the inside.

At least, it certainly appears that way. We appear to live in geometric space, which appear vast and in which we are small. In the language of set theory, we are the small subset $O$ attempting to map the superset $U$.

Because we are embedded, we cannot help but perceive reality through a human lens. We experience "time" as a flow and "matter" as solid, but these may simply be illusions of our vantage point. This makes it incredibly difficult to distinguish between a fundamental law of nature and a mere byproduct of our perspective. We are not external spectators watching a machine; we are observers whose own bodies are being ground between its gears.

The intuition that we are mere subsets of the universe feels "correct." We want to believe the cosmos would exist exactly as it is even if we never appeared. It feels arrogant to suggest that our thin layer of gray matter could play any role in the fundamental existence of the stars.

But here is the trap: even if the universe exists independently of us, our description of it—the physics we write down—is entirely filtered through our internal perspective. By striving for "God-eye" equations, we have ignored the most critical variable: the fact that the entity doing the math is a component of the equation.

\section{The Method}

If we are indeed axiomatic systems, there is a way out of this hall of mirrors.

We do not truly know what time "is"—likely because we are submerged within it. However, while the essence of time remains elusive, its logic is something we can simulate.

If we construct a digital universe that duplicates the essential properties of our own, then whatever "mystery" we fail to grasp must be present within the machine running the code. The problem of the "Unknown" is effectively reduced to the "Known."

We may not fully understand the cosmos, but we understand software. By moving the mystery from the vacuum of space into a block of silicon, we transform a metaphysical puzzle into a debuggable program.
