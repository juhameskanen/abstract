\chapter{The Deep Nature of General Relativity}

\section{Why Einstein, why not Hilbert?}

If the wavefunction is the fundamental reality, then maybe GR too serves as compression system.

But why would the nature use two different compression algoritms? 

Why do we not experience ourselves as observers in a high-dimensional Hilbert space, surrounded by complex-valued wave objects? 

Instead, we perceive ourselves as solid real-valued entities within a three-dimensional, approximately flat Euclidean theater.
Why \textbf{Geometric Einstein} instead of \textbf{Spectral Hilbert}?
Why does gravity—the force that governs the large-scale structure of the cosmos—take the form of geometry?


\section{Informational Boundaries}

As observers, we are finite informational structures.
We do not observe intelligent, fluid $H_2O$ clouds with gradient-like boundaries existing as coherent agents.
If two complex informational structures (such as humans) were to overlap even slightly, their internal organization would be disrupted.
In practice, even small, uncontrolled interactions can destroy high-level structure, often with lethal consequences. 
Every living organism possesses a well-defined ``inside'' property.

Defining such separation would be computationally and informationally very expensive in a high-dimensional, fully entangled Hilbert space.

In contrast, boundaries can be defined with extreme economy in low-dimensional geometric space.

Geometry grants us locality — the clear, unbreakable distinction between ‘here’ and ‘there,’ between ‘me’ and ‘everything else.’


\section{General Relativity as Compression}

If Quantum Mechanics is the ``spectral'' compression of reality, then General Relativity (GR) is its \textbf{geometric compression} counterpart.

Spacetime is essentially a geometric compression in which informational boundaries are easy (economic) to describe.


\section{Geometric Complexity}

Just as wavefunctions vary in complexity based on their frequency components (Spectral Complexity), geometries vary in their informational cost. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{A smooth, gently curved spacetime is ``simple'':} It can be described with relatively little information (low description length).
    \item \textbf{A high-curvature, wildly fluctuating, irregular geometry is ``complex'':} It requires vastly more information to specify every local deviation.
\end{itemize}

From a compression perspective, simpler geometries are overwhelmingly favored because they are shorter in their description.
This explains why, on large scales, spacetime appears smooth, continuous, and governed by simple, elegant laws rather than chaotic fluctuations.
The Weyl tensor represents the independent geometric degrees of freedom—the information of the vacuum—while the Ricci tensor reflects the local
density of spectral information.

\section{Joint Compressibility Measure}

This suggests that unification is not achieved by forcing the math of QM into the math of GR, but through a \textbf{Joint Compressibility Measure}.
The likelihood of any given physical state in the static landscape of all possible configurations depends on the total description length of its
dual representation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Spectral Description Length ($C_s$):} How efficiently the state is encoded as wave-like structures.
    \item \textbf{Geometric Description Length ($C_g$):} How efficiently the state is encoded as a spacetime manifold (primarily specified by the Weyl tensor).
\end{enumerate}

Our observed reality exists at the intersection of these two compression formats.
We find ourselves in configurations that dominate the measure because they provide the shortest joint description.


\section{Intuition and Consequences}

Reality appears the way it does because simple, compressible structures dominate the space of all possible descriptions.
Spectral representation alone is insufficient. We live in geometric space because we require well defined informational
boundaries to keep our internal information in order.
