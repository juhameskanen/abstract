\chapter{Geometric Complexity}

\section{Introduction}

Spectral complexity is obviously not enough. We don't sense ourselves as observers in high dimensional Hilbert space with
complex valued wavelike objects. Instead, we sense ourselves as geometric objects in three dimensional approximately
flat euclidean space. 

Why Einstein instead of Hilbert?

Why does gravity take the form of geometry?

Why do we experience a three-dimensional space with well-defined shapes and boundaries, instead of directly perceiving the high-dimensional, wave-like structure of the quantum world?


\section{Informational Boundaries}

The answer lies in how information is organized.

As observers, we are finite informational structures. To exist and persist, we must maintain clear boundaries—distinguishing
ourselves from the environment.

If two complex informational structures were to overlap even slightly, their internal organization would be disrupted.
In practice, even small uncontrolled interactions can destroy structure and turn out to be lethal. 

Stable existence therefore requires \emph{well-defined separation}.

Such separation is extremely difficult to maintain in a high-dimensional, fully entangled space of possibilities.
There are simply too many ways for structures to interfere with each other.

In contrast, low-dimensional geometric space provides a natural solution:
\begin{itemize}
\item Objects occupy distinct regions.  
\item Boundaries are clear and stable.  
\item Interactions are local and controlled.  
\end{itemize}

Geometry is therefore not just what we observe—it is what allows structured observers with well defined inside, like us, to exist at all.


\section{Geometric Complexity}

Just as wavefunctions can be simple or complex depending on how many frequencies they contain, geometries can also vary in complexity.

A smooth, gently curved spacetime is simple: it can be described with relatively little information.

A wildly fluctuating, irregular geometry is complex: it requires far more information to specify.

From a compression perspective, simpler geometries are overwhelmingly favored because they are easier to describe.

This is why, on large scales, spacetime appears smooth, continuous, and governed by simple laws.

The likelihood of a given experience depends on both:
\begin{itemize}
\item How efficiently it can be encoded spectrally (wave-like structure)  
\item How efficiently it can be encoded geometrically (spacetime structure)  
\end{itemize}

Our observed reality lies at the intersection of these two forms of compression.

\section{Intuition and Consequences}

Viewed through this lens:

\begin{itemize}
\item Spacetime curvature reflects how information is organized geometrically.  
\item Matter follows natural paths (geodesics) because these are the simplest trajectories to describe.  
\item Complex, irregular geometries are suppressed because they require more information to encode.  
\item Smooth, stable spacetime dominates because it is maximally compressible.  
\end{itemize}

Just as quantum mechanics favors simple spectral patterns, gravity favors simple geometric structures.

\section{Summary}

General relativity can be viewed as the geometric counterpart to quantum spectral compression.

\begin{itemize}
\item Quantum mechanics encodes possibilities efficiently using wave-like structures.  
\item General relativity encodes reality efficiently using geometric structure.  
\item Observers exist where both descriptions are simple and stable.  
\end{itemize}

Together, they suggest a unifying principle:

\begin{quote}
Reality appears the way it does because simple, compressible structures overwhelmingly dominate all possible descriptions.
\end{quote}

In this view, gravity is not just a force or a curvature of spacetime—it is the natural result of representing the universe in the most efficient geometric form.
