\chapter{Geometric Complexity}

\section{Introduction}

We don't sense ourselves as observers in high dimensional Hilbert space with
complex valued wavelike objects. Instead, we sense ourselves as geometric objects in three dimensional approximately
flat euclidean space. 

Why Geometric Einstein instead of Spectral Hilbert?

Why does gravity take the form of geometry?


\section{Informational Boundaries}

As observers, we are finite informational structures. To exist and persist, we must maintain clear boundaries—distinguishing
ourselves from the environment.

If two complex informational structures were to overlap even slightly, their internal organization would be disrupted.
In practice, even small uncontrolled interactions can destroy structure and are usually lethal. 

We need \emph{well-defined separation}. We need informational boundaries.

Such separation is extremely costly to define in a high-dimensional, fully entangled space of possibilities.

In contrast, boundaries can be defined economically in low-dimensional geometric space.

Geometry is therefore not just what we observe—it is what allows structured observers with well defined inside,
like us, to exist at all.


\section{Geometric Complexity}

Just as wavefunctions can be simple or complex depending on how many frequencies they contain, geometries can also vary in complexity.

A smooth, gently curved spacetime is simple: it can be described with relatively little information.

A high curvature, wildly fluctuating, irregular geometry is complex: it requires far more information to specify.

From a compression perspective, simpler geometries are overwhelmingly favored because they are shorter in their description.

This is why, on large scales, spacetime appears smooth, continuous, and governed by simple laws.


\section{Joint Compressibility Measure}

The likelihood of a given experience depends on both:

\begin{itemize}
\item How efficiently it can be encoded spectrally (wave-like structure)  
\item How efficiently it can be encoded geometrically (spacetime structure)  
\end{itemize}

Our observed reality lies at the intersection of these two forms of compression. 


\section{Intuition and Consequences}

Reality appears the way it does because simple, compressible structures overwhelmingly dominate all possible descriptions.
