\chapter{The Deep Nature of General Relativity}

\section{Overview}

If the quantum mechanical wavefunction is Nature's primary compression system, then General Relativity (GR) may serve as its geometric counterpart.

Quantum Mechanics (QM) and General Relativity (GR) are traditionally formulated in fundamentally different mathematical languages:
QM is expressed in terms of complex-valued Hilbert spaces and linear operators, while GR is formulated in terms of real-valued differential geometry on smooth manifolds.

Despite this structural divergence, let us try to view both theories as distinct forms of information compression.

\section{General Relativity as Geometric Compression}

General Relativity encodes gravitational physics through a real-valued metric tensor on a differentiable manifold:

\[
(M, g_{\mu\nu}), \quad g_{\mu\nu} \in \mathbb{R}
\]

The dynamics are governed by the Einstein field equations:

\[
G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}
\]

Rather than encoding probabilities, GR encodes the structure of spacetime itself. Curvature replaces force, and geodesic motion replaces acceleration under interaction.

The full geometric content is captured by the Riemann curvature tensor, which decomposes into Ricci and Weyl components:

\[
R_{abcd} = C_{abcd} + (\text{Ricci terms})
\]

The Weyl tensor in particular represents the free, propagating degrees of gravitational structure, independent of local matter content.
This tensor admits eigenstructure decompositions, suggesting a spectral interpretation of spacetime curvature.

Thus, GR can be interpreted as a \emph{real geometric compression scheme} encoding causal and relational structure of spacetime.

\section{Structural Similarities}

Despite their different mathematical foundations, QM and GR share several deep structural features:

\subsection{Spectral Structure}

Both theories rely heavily on spectral decomposition:
\begin{itemize}
\item QM: eigenstates of operators (Hamiltonian, momentum, etc.)
\item GR: eigenstructure of curvature tensors and differential operators
\end{itemize}

In both cases, physical observables emerge from invariant structures under transformation.

\subsection{Relational Encoding}

Neither theory stores absolute information:

\begin{itemize}
\item QM encodes relations between measurement outcomes via amplitudes.
\item GR encodes relations between events via spacetime geometry.
\end{itemize}

Absolute reference frames are replaced by relational structure.

\subsection{Constraint-Based Dynamics}

Both frameworks are governed by strong constraints:

\begin{itemize}
\item Unitarity in QM preserves total probability.
\item Bianchi identities and Einstein equations constrain curvature evolution in GR.
\end{itemize}

Dynamics in both cases arise from consistency conditions on underlying structures.


\section{Key Differences and Similarities from Compression Perspective}

However, if both frameworks are interpreted as forms of information compression, then their difference lies not in the presence or absence of structure, but in the domain over which compression occurs:

\begin{itemize}
\item QM compresses \emph{configurational possibility space}.
\item GR compresses \emph{causal-geometric realization space}.
\end{itemize}

From this perspective, both theories may be seen as projections of a deeper informational substrate, expressed in different mathematical languages optimized for different invariants.

Quantum mechanics and general relativity are not naturally compatible in their current formulations, yet they exhibit parallel structural features: spectral decomposition, relational encoding, and constraint-driven dynamics.

Interpreting both as compression regimes suggests that their disparity may arise not from fundamental incompatibility, but from distinct informational projections of a more primitive underlying structure.


\section{Why Einstein, why not Hilbert?}

But why would the nature use two different compression algoritms? 

Why do we not experience ourselves as observers in a high-dimensional Hilbert space, surrounded by complex-valued wave objects? 

Instead, we perceive ourselves as solid real-valued entities within a three-dimensional, approximately flat Euclidean theater.
Why \textbf{Geometric Einstein} instead of \textbf{Spectral Hilbert}?
Why does gravity—the force that governs the large-scale structure of the cosmos—take the form of geometry?


\section{Informational Boundaries}

As observers, we are finite informational structures.
We do not observe intelligent, fluid $H_2O$ clouds with gradient-like boundaries existing as coherent agents.
If two complex informational structures (such as humans) were to overlap even slightly, their internal organization would be disrupted.
In practice, even small, uncontrolled interactions can destroy high-level structure, often with lethal consequences. 

Therefore, as stated in Principle~\nameref{pr:observer-boundaries},
stable observation requires separability between observer and environment.

Defining such separation would be computationally and informationally very expensive in a high-dimensional, fully entangled Hilbert space.

In contrast, boundaries can be defined with extreme economy in low-dimensional geometric space.

Geometry grants us locality — the clear, unbreakable distinction between ‘here’ and ‘there,’ between ‘me’ and ‘everything else.’


\section{General Relativity as Compression}

If Quantum Mechanics is the ``spectral'' compression of reality, then General Relativity (GR) is its \textbf{geometric compression} counterpart.

Spacetime is essentially a geometric compression in which informational boundaries are easy (economic) to describe.


\section{Geometric Complexity}

Just as wavefunctions vary in complexity based on their frequency components (Spectral Complexity), geometries vary in their informational cost. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{A smooth, gently curved spacetime is ``simple'':} It can be described with relatively little information (low description length).
    \item \textbf{A high-curvature, wildly fluctuating, irregular geometry is ``complex'':} It requires vastly more information to specify every local deviation.
\end{itemize}

From a compression perspective, simpler geometries are overwhelmingly favored because they are shorter in their description.
This explains why, on large scales, spacetime appears smooth, continuous, and governed by simple, elegant laws rather than chaotic fluctuations.
The Weyl tensor represents the independent geometric degrees of freedom—the information of the vacuum—while the Ricci tensor reflects the local
density of spectral information.

\section{Joint Compressibility Measure}

This suggests that unification is not achieved by forcing the math of QM into the math of GR, but through a \textbf{Joint Compressibility Measure}.
The likelihood of any given physical state in the static landscape of all possible configurations depends on the total description length of its
dual representation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Spectral Description Length ($C_s$):} How efficiently the state is encoded as wave-like structures.
    \item \textbf{Geometric Description Length ($C_g$):} How efficiently the state is encoded as a spacetime manifold.
\end{enumerate}

Our observed reality exists at the intersection of these two induced measures.

\section{Intuition and Consequences}

Because the number of microstates (wavefunction configurations) compatible with a given description scales inversely with that
description's length, we are statistically bound to find ourselves in one of our most highly compressed, minimally described states.

Spectral representation alone is insufficient. We live in geometric space because we require well defined informational
boundaries to keep our internal information in order.
