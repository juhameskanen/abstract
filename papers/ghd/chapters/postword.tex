\chapter{Postword}

Human DNA consists of about three billion base pairs, and our brains contain roughly one hundred billion neurons. The number of interconnections between neurons is estimated at around 100 trillion — far more than the largest artificial neural networks today.

Given the rapid pace of technological advancement, it is conceivable that neural networks may one day surpass the human brain in sheer complexity. Perhaps this will happen in the next one or two decades. Yet even if they do, complexity alone does not capture the full richness of human abilities. Time will tell.

Simulating a single human brain at the level of every neuron would require an astronomical amount of computing power — a network of hundreds of billions of CPUs, occupying cubic kilometers of space and drawing the energy of a nuclear power plant, not to mention the surrounding infrastructure. And if we wanted to simulate a whole person convincingly, we’d also need a simulated universe around them — complete with trillions of cells and hundreds of billions of stars in countless galaxies.

In short, God — if there is one — does not need to worry about simulated souls any time soon. It is surely we humans who have some catching up to do.

At one point, I considered naming this book The Worst Book Ever. That may have been the obvious choice. Yet, exercising my illusion of free will, I chose otherwise.

Stephen Hawking once asked, in A Brief History of Time, what breathes fire into the equations and makes a universe for them to describe. The traditional scientific approach — constructing mathematical models — cannot answer why there should be a universe at all. Why bother existing?

The conclusion proposed in this book is that nothing breathes fire into anything. There is no more universe than there are abstract equations we call the laws of physics. The nature of everything is abstract.

It is easy to imagine total nothingness — no information, no logic, no celluloid, no pain. Perhaps this ease arises from our evolution: survival requires “having something” — air to breathe, solid ground beneath our feet, food to eat — and if we fail, we get nothing. In that sense, nothingness becomes the default, the background state against which life struggles to emerge. Yet from the set of all possible sets, existence arose. It is probable, then, that there is something rather than nothing. And while it is ultimately abstract, it feels astonishingly real.
