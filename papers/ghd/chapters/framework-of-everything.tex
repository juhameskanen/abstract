\chapter{The Theory}

\section{Primitive Ontology}

We assume nothing about the fundamental nature of reality.
In particular, we do not assume that the universe is made of information, that it is finite, or that any specific laws of physics exist a priori.
The concepts of ``information'', ``bits'', ``mathematics'', and ``finite configuration space'' are treated as descriptive tools available to conscious observers, not as ontological primitives.

Our single starting point is observational: there exists at least one conscious observer (``Alice'') who experiences pain, geometry, and a structured world.
From this and four minimal axioms, the theory is developed.

\section{Configuration Space}

For descriptive convenience we introduce a finite configuration space
\[
\mathcal{C} = \{0,1\}^n,
\]
where each $c \in \mathcal{C}$ is a complete static configuration.
The binary representation is chosen purely for convenience; the theory does not depend on it.

No spacetime, dynamics, or external laws are assumed.

\section{Descriptions and Complexity}

Each configuration $c$ admits many possible descriptions $\mathcal{D}(c)$.
A description $d \in \mathcal{D}(c)$ is any encoding that reproduces $c$ under suitable interpretation.

We define the complexity of a description primarily through its spectral content (wave-like representation):
\[
K_{\rm spec}(d) = \sum_i \left[ 
\log_2\left(\frac{A_i}{\Delta A_i}\right) + 
\log_2\left(\frac{2\pi}{\Delta \phi_i}\right) + 
\log_2\left(\frac{\omega_i}{\Delta \omega_i}\right)
\right],
\]
where $A_i$, $\phi_i$, $\omega_i$ are the amplitude, phase and frequency of spectral mode $i$, and the $\Delta$ terms reflect the precision required to distinguish configurations.

Higher frequencies and sharper features require more bits and are therefore heavily suppressed.
Smooth, low-frequency, predictable structures compress far better and dominate the measure.

A geometric complexity $K_{\rm geo}(d)$ is similarly defined to capture localized structure and boundaries. The total complexity is
\[
K(d) = K_{\rm spec}(d) + K_{\rm geo}(d).
\]

\section{Induced Measure}

The weight of a configuration is given by the multiplicity of its low-complexity descriptions:
\[
W(c) = \sum_{d \in \mathcal{D}(c)} e^{-K(d)}.
\]
This induces the measure
\[
P(c) = \frac{W(c)}{\sum_{c' \in \mathcal{C}} W(c')}.
\]

\section{Observer Functional}

An observer is characterized by a functional
\[
\mu_O : \mathcal{C} \to [0,1]
\]
that grades how well a configuration realizes an observer structure (for example, a spatially localized Gaussian blob with stable internal dynamics).

Observer-conditioned probability is then
\[
P(c \mid O) = \frac{\mu_O(c) \, W(c)}{\sum_{c'} \mu_O(c') \, W(c')}.
\]

\section{Emergence of Laws and Geometry}

Configurations supporting stable Gaussian-blob observers naturally produce emergent inertia and smooth elliptical orbits.
Sharp turns or chaotic motion require high-frequency spectral components, which dramatically increase description length and are exponentially suppressed in the measure.

Thus, observer-compatible reality is overwhelmingly dominated by smooth, predictable, law-like configurations.
Physical laws are not imposed from outside; they are large-deviation minimizers of the joint spectral-geometric complexity under the observer measure.

\section{The Internal Clock}

Time is not fundamental. For an observer $O$, it emerges as an internal ordering $\prec_O$ on configurations:
\[
c_1 \prec_O c_2 \quad \text{if $c_2$ contains a more stable and developed realization of $\mu_O$ than $c_1$.}
\]

The experienced flow of time is the observer's traversal along the most compressible available paths.
As informational content accumulates over a ``lifetime'', maintaining high compression becomes harder.
Eventually the supporting measure drops below a critical threshold and consciousness fades.

\section{Conclusion}

If the four observational axioms hold (DNA encodes conscious beings, matter is ordinary, processes are computable, and subjective experience has causal power), then the only universe any observer can ever know is the one that arises as the most compressible, observer-compatible structure within consciousness itself.

The apparent external world is a highly compressed internal construction. In the deepest sense, the universe we experience lives in us.
