\chapter{The Theory}

\section{Primitive Ontology}

The theory begins with minimal assumptions.
If reality is fundamentally informational, a natural question arises: is the total information finite or infinite?
If it were finite, one would have to ask: finite by how many bits? And who, or what, set that limit? Any fixed bound would itself require explanation.
A finite informational universe merely pushes the mystery back one level. 
The only non-arbitrary conclusion is that the deep nature of everything cannot be finitely bounded.
At the most fundamental level, reality must be infinite—perhaps not even informational in the familiar sense, but beyond any finite description.

Threfore, we do not presuppose that reality is fundamentally informational, finite, or governed by prior laws.

Based on observational evidence, we observers are finite structures.

The only firm starting point is observational: conscious finite observers exist and have structured experiences (including pain and geometry).

For descriptive purposes we introduce a finite configuration space
\[
\mathcal{C} = \{0,1\}^n,
\]
where each $c\in\mathcal{C}$ is a complete static configuration. No spacetime, dynamics, or external laws are assumed.


\section{Descriptions and Complexity}

Each configuration $c$ admits multiple descriptions $\mathcal{D}(c)$. We quantify the complexity of a description $d$ primarily by its spectral content:
\[
K_{\rm spec}(d) = \sum_i \left[ 
\log_2\left(\frac{A_i}{\Delta A_i}\right) + 
\log_2\left(\frac{2\pi}{\Delta \phi_i}\right) + 
\log_2\left(\frac{\omega_i}{\Delta \omega_i}\right)
\right],
\]
where the sum runs over spectral modes, and the $\Delta$ terms represent the precision needed to distinguish configurations.

A geometric complexity term $K_{\rm geo}(d)$ accounts for localized structure and boundaries. The total complexity is $K(d) = K_{\rm spec}(d) + K_{\rm geo}(d)$.

Lower total complexity corresponds to higher multiplicity and thus higher weight in the induced measure.


\section{Induced Measure}

The weight of configuration $c$ is
\[
W(c) = \sum_{d \in \mathcal{D}(c)} e^{-K(d)}.
\]
This defines the induced measure
\[
P(c) = \frac{W(c)}{\sum_{c'\in\mathcal{C}} W(c')}.
\]


\section{Observer Functional}

An observer is characterized by a grading functional
\[
\mu_O : \mathcal{C} \to [0,1]
\]
that measures how well a configuration realizes a stable observer structure (e.g., a localized Gaussian blob with coherent internal dynamics).

The observer-conditioned probability is
\[
P(c \mid O) = \frac{\mu_O(c) \, W(c)}{\sum_{c'} \mu_O(c') \, W(c')}.
\]


\section{Emergence of Laws}

Numerical simulations show that configurations supporting stable Gaussian-blob observers strongly favor smooth, low-frequency spectral content. Sharp accelerations or chaotic trajectories require high-frequency modes, which dramatically increase description length and are exponentially suppressed.

As a result, observer-compatible realities are dominated by configurations exhibiting inertia, smooth curved paths, and law-like behavior. Physical laws emerge as the paths that minimize joint spectral-geometric complexity under the observer measure.


\section{The Internal Clock}

Time is not fundamental. It emerges as an internal ordering $\prec_O$ on configurations, where $c_1 \prec_O c_2$ if $c_2$ supports a more stable realization of $\mu_O$.

The experienced flow of time corresponds to traversal along the most compressible observer-compatible paths. As accumulated information grows, maintaining high compression becomes increasingly difficult. Eventually the supporting measure falls below a critical threshold and consciousness fades.


\section{Conclusion}

Given the four observational axioms, the theory implies that the only universe any observer can know is the highly compressed, observer-compatible structure arising within consciousness. The apparent external world is an internal construction. In the deepest sense, the universe we experience exists within the observer.
