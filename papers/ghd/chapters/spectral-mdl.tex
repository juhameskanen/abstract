\chapter{Spectral Complexity}

\section{Introduction}

The Introduction to Quantum Mechanics chapter was breathtakingly long.

What is the deep nature of Quantum Mechanics and its mysterious abstract wave function?

Why does everything we observe at the subatomic level appear to behave like a wave?

If we back up a bit and try to view the system from distance, what do we observe?

First, the microscopic “building blocks” - the particles- are extremely simple. A small set of fields / particles
with just a few parameters (mass, charge, spin), yet enormous emergent richness!

Second, the complex amplitudes in the wavefunction are doing almost all the heavy lifting. 
Interference, entanglement, superpositions, all strange quantum effects are simply
consequences of the complex valued Hilbert space. 

If we don't pay attention to these little details, what is that we see?


\subsection*{Movie Analogy}

In a conventional movie, each frame consists of pixels with simple features, such as red, green, and blue intensities.
To compress such a movie efficiently, engineers use techniques like the discrete cosine transform (DCT) or Fourier transforms, distributing the information across coefficients (JPG, MPEG).

If you were an observer in such a movie, made of pixels, what would you observe?

You would observe elementary particles, called red, green and blue.  These would comprise
composite objects called pixels with emergent properties (like energy-intensity).

The underlying structure governing pixels would appear to follow hidden, mysterious wave-like patterns, even though
what you directly see are just individual pixels.


\section{Wavefunction as Compression}

The wavefunction can be viewed as an advanced, complex-valued version of the sinusoidal building blocks used in compression algorithms like JPG and MPEG.

In a movie, a pixel just has a color. In the wavefunction, a 'pixel' also has a phase (a kind of internal angle that determines how waves combine).

In the real universe, the “pixels” are elementary particles. Unlike RGB pixels, these particles carry rich internal features—spin, charge, flavor, and more.

Just as a Fourier transform spreads image information across coefficients, the wavefunction distributes amplitude information across all possible configurations.



\section{Born Rule as Dithering}

All our monitors have limited number of pixels and colors. The same apply to all printed media. This discrete nature is visible and is the main reason why
images in the newspapers and digital media are ``dithered''. The tecnique smoots out jagged edges and intensity bands.

Correspondingly, the Born rule acts like probabilistic \emph{dithering}: instead of reading the amplitude directly, nature samples it probabilistically.

Over many repeated measurements, these probabilistic outcomes faithfully reproduce the underlying continuous amplitude distribution, smoothing out “jagged edges” and preserving the global structure of possibilities.
In this sense, the Born rule emerges naturally as the way to extract discrete outcomes from a maximally compressed, deterministic amplitude field.

It allows the reality to be printed with least number of resources (pixels in movie industry, ink in printing industry).


\section{Compression Dominates Reality}

Quantum mechanics can therefore be seen as nature’s ultimate compression algorithm.  The deterministic evolution of amplitudes preserves all information (unitarity).  
Observed outcomes are probabilistic samples that faithfully reproduce the underlying amplitudes. Interference, superposition, and entanglement emerge naturally as
consequences of encoding complex, high-dimensional information efficiently.

Smooth, predictable patterns dominate because they are \emph{spectrally simple}: a low number of active modes suffices to encode them. 

Unlike Kolmogorov complexity, the wavefunction's complexity is both continuous and computable.
The complexity of the wavefunction is the number of phases and frequencies in them. It is both continuous and computable.

This perspective resolves the apparent mysteries of quantum mechanics.



\section{From Nothing To Something Compressed}

So let us refactor the simulations that mapped bitstring of increasing entropy to geometric spacetime and emergent particles. Let us
take all the possible $2^n$ configurations and with increasing entropy order and compress them into MPEG movies.

Then we make a random pick.

What do we see?

Well, almost certainly, we see nothing. Just empty space. Straight lines. Extremely simply universes.


Why? Because Salomonoff induction. The most compressible universes are those that compress the best, and are exponentially favoured.
