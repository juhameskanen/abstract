\chapter{Spectral Complexity}

\section{Introduction}

What is the deep nature of Quantum Mechanics and its mysterious abstract wave function?

Why does everything we observe at the subatomic level appear to behave like a wave?


\subsection*{Movie Analogy}

In a conventional movie, each frame consists of pixels with simple features, such as red, green, and blue intensities.
To compress such a movie efficiently, engineers use techniques like the discrete cosine transform (DCT) or Fourier transforms, distributing the information across coefficients (JPG, MPEG).

If you were an observer in such a movie, made of pixels, what would you observe?

The underlying structure governing pixels would appear to follow hidden, wave-like patterns, even though what you directly see are just individual pixels.


\section{Wavefunction as Compression}

The wavefunction is a more advanced, complex-valued version of the sinusoidal building blocks used in compression algorithms like JPG and MPEG.

In a movie, a pixel just has a color. In the wavefunction, a 'pixel' also has a phase (a kind of internal angle that determines how waves combine).

In the real universe, the “pixels” are elementary particles. Unlike RGB pixels, these particles carry rich internal features—spin, charge, flavor, and more.

Just as a Fourier transform spreads image information across coefficients, the wavefunction distributes amplitude information across all possible configurations.



\section{Born Rule as Dithering}

The Born rule acts like probabilistic \emph{dithering}: instead of reading the amplitude directly, nature samples it probabilistically.

Over many repeated measurements, these probabilistic outcomes faithfully reproduce the underlying continuous amplitude distribution, smoothing out “jagged edges” and preserving the global structure of possibilities.
In this sense, the Born rule emerges naturally as the way to extract discrete outcomes from a maximally compressed, deterministic amplitude field.

It allows the reality to be printed with least number of resources (pixels in movie industry, ink in printing industry).


\section{Compression Dominates Reality}

Quantum mechanics can therefore be seen as nature’s ultimate compression algorithm.  The deterministic evolution of amplitudes preserves all information (unitarity).  
Observed outcomes are probabilistic samples that faithfully reproduce the underlying amplitudes. Interference, superposition, and entanglement emerge naturally as
consequences of encoding complex, high-dimensional information efficiently.

Smooth, predictable patterns dominate because they are \emph{spectrally simple}: a low number of active modes suffices to encode them.

Unlike Kolmogorov complexity, the wavefunction's complexity is both continuous and computable.
The complexity of the wavefunction is the number of phases and frequencies.

\section{Summary}

Viewed through the lens of spectral compression:

\begin{itemize}
\item The Schrödinger equation is the \emph{compression algorithm} of reality.  
\item The Born rule is \emph{probabilistic sampling} (dithering) of the compressed amplitudes.  
\item Smooth, low-frequency configurations dominate because they require minimal spectral resources.  
\item Observers naturally inhabit these compressed trajectories, which appear as predictable physical laws.
\end{itemize}

This perspective resolves the apparent mysteries of quantum mechanics: wave-like behavior,
interference, superposition, and probability all emerge from the principle of maximal compression, without invoking external agents.
