\chapter{Spectral Complexity}

\section{From Kolmogorov to Spectral Measure}

Across all known domains—quantum fields, atomic spectra, and radiation—states decompose naturally into discrete frequency components, eigenstates, or harmonics.
This is not merely a mathematical convenience but an observational fact: the fundamental degrees of freedom we measure are spectral.

In quantum mechanics, systems are described by wavefunctions whose physically meaningful representations are expansions over energy or momentum eigenbases.
Interactions selectively excite a limited set of modes, and stable configurations concentrate amplitude into a small subset of them.

This is precisely what compression means in practice: most coefficients are negligible, and only a sparse structure carries the relevant information.

Unlike Kolmogorov complexity, which depends on an arbitrary choice of universal machine and is uncomputable and discontinuous, spectral representations are continuous, physically instantiated, and directly measurable.

The universe does not need to “search” for the shortest program; it already evolves in a basis where simplicity corresponds to concentration in a few modes.
Thus, spectral compression is not an imposed principle but a reflection of how physical reality is empirically organized—making it the natural operational realization of minimal description length.


\section{The First Notes}

If our four initial assumptions \cite{meskanen2008} hold, the universe is fundamentally information without inherent structure—pure, high-entropy configuration space.

Given $n$ bits, there are $2^n$ possible configurations.

Within this structure, what we call an “observer” corresponds to a consistent traversal through a sequence of configurations.
These traversals are not processes occurring in time, but ordered selections within a fixed set of relations.

Alice exists because, within this configuration space, there are consistent sequences of states that encode her identity.

Configurations with minimal description length dominate the measure, and minimal descriptions are those that compress best.


\section{The Universe as a Spectral Codec}

Each configuration admits a spectral representation, decomposing its structure into modes characterized by frequency, phase, and amplitude.

In this view, the universe is a static collection of configurations with varying degrees of spectral structure.
Some configurations are highly structured and compressible, while others resemble unstructured noise.


\section{Spectral Representation of Configurations}

A configuration is described by a set of modes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Frequencies: scales of structural variation
    \item Phases: relational alignment between components
    \item Amplitudes: distribution of spectral weight
\end{itemize}

Together, these quantities determine how efficiently a configuration can be represented spectrally.


\section{Defining Spectral Complexity}

To define complexity, we represent a configuration in terms of its spectral modes.

Each mode contributes to the informational cost of representation.
High-frequency structure requires finer specification, while low-frequency structure is simpler to encode.

We define a spectral measure over modes:

\[
p_k = |C_k|^2, \quad \sum_k p_k = 1.
\]

The spectral complexity of a configuration is then:

\[
K_\text{spectral}[\psi] = \sum_k p_k \, Q(f_k),
\]

where $Q(f_k)$ is a monotonic function representing the cost of specifying structure at frequency $f_k$.

In this formulation, complexity is determined by how spectral weight is distributed across modes.
Configurations dominated by low-frequency structure are simpler, while those with significant weight in high-frequency modes are more complex.


\section{Observer Paths}

An observer is defined as a consistent sequence of configurations within the static universe.

Such a sequence is not a physical trajectory, but a structured ordering of configurations that together form a coherent observer identity.

Different observer paths correspond to different traversals of the same underlying configuration space.


\section{Measure over Observer Paths}

Not all observer paths are equally significant. Some sequences admit more compact spectral descriptions than others.

We define a measure over observer paths based on their total spectral complexity:

\[
P(\text{path}) \propto 2^{-K_\text{path}},
\]

where $K_\text{path}$ is the accumulated spectral complexity required to describe the sequence of configurations along the path.

Paths that admit simpler spectral descriptions carry higher measure, while highly irregular or unstructured paths carry lower measure.


\section{The Emergence of Classical Regularity}

Within this framework, classical behavior corresponds to observer paths that exhibit stable and compressible spectral structure across successive configurations.

Such paths are characterized by continuity in their spectral representation: configurations along the path do not require large changes in descriptive cost.

More irregular or noise-like sequences require significantly greater descriptive resources and therefore contribute less to the observer measure.

As a result, observers predominantly find themselves embedded in paths that are spectrally simple and stable.


\section{Conclusion}

The universe is a static informational structure containing all configurations simultaneously.

Observers correspond to structured traversals through this space, and the measure over these traversals is determined by their spectral compressibility.

We experience a coherent and regular world because such structure provides the most efficient representation of information across observer paths.
