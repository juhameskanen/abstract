\chapter{The Problem of Emergent Fermions}

All particles in the Standard Model fall into two families: bosons (integer spin, can pile on top of each other) and fermions (half-integer spin, fiercely antisocial — no two identical fermions can occupy the same quantum state).
This is the Pauli exclusion principle, and it is what gives matter its rigidity, allows atoms to form, and ultimately makes complex structures (including us) possible.

In our early simulations the “particles” were simple Gaussian wavepackets.
They showed encouraging signs: inertia from minimum description length, interference from spectral compression, and even a weak statistical attraction.
But when left to interact freely, they always did the same thing — they merged into one big undifferentiated blob.
Distinct “Alices” lost their individual identities. No collisions, no bouncing, no stable separation.

We had emergent bosons. We did not have emergent fermions.

\subsection*{The Core Difficulty}

In standard quantum mechanics, exchanging two identical particles produces a simple sign change:

\begin{itemize}
\item Bosons: \(\psi(x_1, x_2) = +\psi(x_2, x_1)\)
\item Fermions: \(\psi(x_1, x_2) = -\psi(x_2, x_1)\)
\end{itemize}

The minus sign forces \(\psi(x,x) = 0\), creating the exclusion principle. The probability that two fermions
could occupy the same state is zero. 

Our informational measure, based purely on counting bits needed to describe spectral modes, naturally favors symmetric stacking (bosonic behavior) because it is cheaper to describe multiple identical excitations with shared modes.
Achieving a sharp antisymmetric cancellation that drives probability exactly to zero at overlap points appears to require either massive number of high-frequency modes (which our theory exponentially suppresses) or an extra rule we cannot yet derive from bit-counting alone.


\subsection*{Where To Go From Here?}

Hard-coding antisymmetry would be philosophically unacceptable. It would violate the very spirit of the project: everything must emerge from the informational cost measure.

We want everything (including the existence of fermions and the Pauli exclusion principle) to emerge purely from the information-content-driven measure, via something Solomonoff-like: configurations (or descriptions) that require fewer total bits to specify are exponentially favored in the induced probability \(P(c)\).
No extra primitives, no hand-added spinors on vertices/edges, no ad-hoc sign rules.

Only counting bits.


\subsection*{Where To Go From Here?}

Bosons emerge naturally because stacking identical spectral modes (same \(\omega, A, \phi\)) often reduces \(K_{\rm sym}\): one description can cover multiple indistinguishable excitations with almost no extra bits (symmetric wavefunction, unrestricted occupancy). The total \(K(d)\) stays low, so those configurations get high weight \(W(c)\).

Fermions require the opposite: multiple occupancy must become extremely expensive (or effectively forbidden) for identical states, and exchanging two such excitations should introduce a minus sign in the amplitude (antisymmetry). We need a mechanism where certain multi-excitation configurations suddenly have much higher joint description length, so they are exponentially suppressed in the observer-conditioned probability \(P(c \mid O)\).

The only acceptable path is to find a mechanism in which antisymmetric configurations are dramatically cheaper in total joint description length (spectral + geometric + relational) than symmetric ones, when a coherent observer is present.

Several directions deserve serious exploration:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Signed interpretations and relational cost}: Could certain modes carry an intrinsic “sign” that is cheap in the relational complexity \(K_{\rm rel}\) but expensive when symmetric overlap occurs? Could the geometric dual representation penalize symmetric piling because it destroys clean boundaries?

\item \textbf{Observer stability as the selector:} Stable observers may require sharp informational boundaries. Antisymmetric configurations naturally create nodal surfaces (regions of destructive interference) that make boundary maintenance cheaper. Symmetric overlap, by contrast, creates messy, leaking regions that cost extra bits to keep the observer intact.

\item \textbf{Non-commutative or ordered representations:} If the ordering or arrangement of modes itself carries informational cost (like endianness in computing), certain exchange symmetries could become significantly more expensive than others.

\item \textbf{Geometric singularity analogy:} As we saw in earlier chapters, zero-entropy configurations map to minimal geometric points, explaining
  GR singularities as geometric problems only, while informational picture remains fully descriptive. Could similar mechanics work here too? 

\item \textbf{Observer Filter:}  Maybe observer filter is that selects fermion way over bosons, because observers simply don't
  exist based on purely bosonic principle.
  
\end{itemize}

None of these ideas are fully developed yet. But they all stay within the rules we set: no new primitives, only differences in how many bits are required to produce a stable, compressible description that supports an observer.

\subsection*{Why This Matters}

Without some form of emergent exclusion, localized structures cannot remain distinct. Matter as we know it — atoms, molecules, stars, living beings — becomes impossible. The universe we actually experience seems to demand both bosonic waves and fermionic rigidity.

However, let’s look at the glass as half full: the fact that our simulation so cleanly produces one result but not the other is not a failure. It tells us exactly where the current measure is incomplete and where the next refinement must focus.

The hunt for emergent fermions is now the central open problem of the theory — and perhaps its most promising (and challenging) frontier.
