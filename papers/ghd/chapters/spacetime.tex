\section{The Schwarzschild Paradox: A Coordinate Conflict}

One of the most profound features of GR—and a challenge for any simulation—is the divergence of time at an event horizon. This is best illustrated by the "Black Hole Falling" paradox.

\subsection{The External Observer (The Infinite Wait)}

For an observer at a distance, time is governed by the metric component $g_{00} = -(1 - r_s/r)$. As an object approaches the Schwarzschild radius ($r \to r_s$), the value of $g_{00}$ approaches zero. 

In code, the time-dilation factor is:
\[ d\tau = dt \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_s}{r}} \]
As $r$ approaches $r_s$, $dt$ (the coordinate time) must go to infinity for any finite change in $d\tau$ (the object's proper time). To the external simulation, the object's "update" cycle slows down exponentially. It appears to freeze at the horizon, becoming infinitely redshifted. It never "reaches" the horizon in the external observer's coordinate system because the "index" $r_s$ is a coordinate singularity.

\subsection{The Falling Observer (The Finite Reality)}

Crucially, for the "falling" instance of the \texttt{Particle} class, the local $d\tau$ remains perfectly regular. By switching to \textbf{Eddington-Finkelstein coordinates}, we see that the object crosses the horizon without noticing anything unusual. 



Once inside, the roles of space and time effectively swap. The coordinate $r$ becomes timelike—moving toward $r=0$ is as inevitable as moving toward "tomorrow" is for us. Even for a supermassive black hole, the time from the horizon to the singularity is finite and brief:
\[ \tau_{fall} \approx \frac{\pi G M}{c^3} \]
For a black hole of 10 solar masses, this is a mere 0.0001 seconds. For a billion-solar-mass giant at the center of a galaxy, it's roughly 1.4 hours.

The event horizon is not a physical wall, but a \textbf{causal boundary}. It is the point where all future-directed paths (light cones) point inward.
