\chapter{Frequently Asked Questions}

\section*{Where did all the matter in the universe come from?}
This question implicitly assumes temporal ordering and an external origin. Both time and causality are emergent, observer-dependent phenomena (as established in Paper~1) \cite{meskanen2008}.
In the underlying informational substrate, the universe is abstract and timeless.

\section*{Why something rather than nothing?}
This is analogous to asking why ``heads'' rather than ``tails'': both are equally abstract and neither is privileged.

\section*{Why do particles follow a complex-valued wavefunction?}
Because we are observing compressed structure. The wavefunction is the \textbf{compression codec} of the underlying informational structure of reality. Observed wave-like behavior emerges from this compression.

Formally:
\[
\boxed{\text{Maximal Predictability}} \;\rightarrow\; \boxed{\text{Maximal Compression}} \;\rightarrow\; \boxed{\text{Maximal Probability}} 
\]

Configurations with minimal observer-indexed complexity \( \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma] \) dominate the probability measure:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) \propto \exp[-\lambda \, \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]].
\]
Hence, the ``laws'' of physics are statistical consequences of overwhelmingly probable, compressible configurations.

\section*{Why does the universe expand? Is there a link to entropy?}
Expansion corresponds to increasing entropy. Space and entropy are dual: as the underlying informational substrate becomes more disordered, the corresponding geometric mapping stretches. 

The trajectory of observer-compatible configurations toward higher entropy mirrors the Inflationary Epoch and subsequent cosmological expansion. Space is the \textbf{geometric interpretation} of total entropy; thus, as entropy increases, the universe appears to expand.

\section*{Why did the universe start with zero entropy?}
Observers exist only in low-complexity, observer-compatible configurations. Among all \(2^n\) possible arrangements of an \(n\)-bit universe, only a tiny subset can support stable, self-referential structures (observers).

A zero-entropy ``beginning'' does not imply a temporal origin in an external sense; it is a boundary condition favored by the observer-conditioned measure:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) \propto \exp[-\lambda \, \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]].
\]

Low-entropy configurations are maximally compressible and thus most probable. High-entropy starting states would require additional information to encode the observer, increasing \( \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma] \) and suppressing probability:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma_{\rm high-entropy} \mid O) \ll \mathbb{P}(\gamma_{\rm zero-entropy} \mid O).
\]

Consequently, the arrow of time and increasing entropy emerge naturally:
\[
\text{Zero entropy} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Maximal compressibility} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Observer-compatible universe}.
\]

\textit{Speculative remark:} Other observer-compatible configurations may exist with different geometric histories. Whether all must exhibit apparent low-entropy origins remains an open question.

\section*{Is the Universe Infinite?}
Based on all observational evidence, we observers are finite structures. However, if reality is fundamentally informational, a natural question arises: is the total information finite or infinite?

If it were finite, one would have to ask: finite by how many bits? And who, or what, set that limit? Any fixed bound would itself require explanation. A finite informational universe merely pushes the mystery back one level. 

The only non-arbitrary conclusion is that the deep nature of everything cannot be finitely bounded. At the most fundamental level, reality must be infinite—perhaps not even informational in the familiar sense, but beyond any finite description.

\section*{What is spacetime made of?}
Nothing. Observers find themselves within the most compressible (probable) configurations, and the path through these configurations is what observers sense as time. Each configuration is slightly different, which observers perceive as gravity (geometric spacetime).

We ``fall'' because there are more copies of us ``down there.'' Gravity is the statistical bias toward the most probable (compressible) configurations describing the observer.

\section*{If there are no metaphysical signals, how does the observer ``see'' anything?}
Observation does not require signals. The universe perceived by an observer is already encoded within their wavefunction:
\[
\Psi_\gamma = \Psi_{\rm self} \otimes \Psi_{\rm env},
\]
where \(\Psi_{\rm self}\) defines the persistent observer structure and \(\Psi_{\rm env}\) encodes the surrounding environment. 

\textbf{Temporal and spatial extrapolation:} These are purely indexical—observer-compatible histories are extended by minimal-complexity continuations:
\[
\Psi_{\rm self}(t+1) \sim \arg\min_{\gamma_{0:t+1}} \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma_{0:t+1}], \quad \Psi_{\rm env} \sim \text{compressible extensions of } \Psi_{\rm self}.
\]
Apparent laws, structures, and predictability emerge statistically from correlations within the observer-compatible configuration space.

\section*{How does Gödel incompleteness affect the theory?}
Gödel’s theorems constrain formal systems capable of arithmetic, but this framework targets ontological closure rather than formal deductive completeness. Embedded observers will encounter Gödel-style limits in reasoning, which manifest as constraints on compression and representation, not on the underlying informational substrate itself.

\section*{Is a singularity a point of infinite density?}
No. Singularities correspond to zero-entropy states with no degrees of freedom to encode microstructure. The probability of emergent microstructures at the singularity is zero \cite{meskanen2010}. Infinite curvature would also compress poorly and would be suppressed statistically. 

\section*{Are we living in a simulation?}
We are living in a simulation-like universe \cite{meskanen2008}. All observer-compatible configurations exist within a single informational substrate. Simulated and non-simulated realities are ontologically equivalent. Hence, the question of ``living in a simulation'' is ill-posed; everything arises from the same abstract information.

\section*{Is the recursive simulation hypothesis supported?}
Yes. Observers may discover how to simulate themselves as informational structures. Such sub-simulations correspond to configurations within the same underlying informational substrate, rather than requiring a separate ontological level.

However, fully faithful and unbounded recursive simulations are informationally expensive. Within this framework, where probability is tied to compressibility, such configurations are strongly suppressed. Highly complex, deeply nested simulations are less compressible and therefore less likely for an observer to inhabit.

\section*{Does the theory support QBism?}
QBism interprets the wavefunction as representing an agent’s subjective degrees of belief. This theory does not adopt a belief-based interpretation. Instead, it treats probabilities as arising from objective structure, grounded in compressibility and statistical typicality.

\section*{Why do we age and die?}
The observer’s future is limited by the spectral complexity of their wavefunction, \(\psi_O\). Let \(\Sigma[\gamma]\) denote the number of independent frequency-phase components required to encode future history \(\gamma\). The probability of persistence scales as:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) \propto \exp(-\alpha \, \Sigma[\gamma]).
\]
The accumulation of memories and internal structure increases \(\Sigma[\gamma]\), reducing the measure of compatible continuations. Aging and death are the geometric and statistical manifestations of combinatorial exhaustion.

\section*{Is this just Solipsism?}
The theory takes the observer’s existence as axiomatic, but it does not imply solipsism. The number of observers is a probabilistic variable. Multiple observers dominate the measure unless a single observer can be proven statistically favored.

\section*{What motivates the replacement of Kolmogorov with Spectral Complexity?}
Observational evidence. We observe that the micro-cosmos behaves as waves; we are observing compressed information. Furthermore, Kolmogorov complexity is non-computable and discontinuous. Small input changes can yield massive complexity spikes, which is difficult to reconcile with smooth physical observations. 

\section*{Does the theory explain why the expansion rate is near-critical?}
Conditional on increasing entropy and typical emergence, observers are most likely to find themselves near the peak of the microstructure count (maximum compressibility). This automatically corresponds to a near-critical expansion regime.

\section*{Is this AIT with Kolmogorov replaced by Spectral Complexity?}
No. However, it shares the principle of Solomonoff induction: simpler descriptions occur more frequently in the ensemble of possibilities.
