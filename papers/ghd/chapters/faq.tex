\chapter{Frequently Asked Questions}

\section*{Where did all the matter in the universe come from?}

Nowhere, more than number 2 or $2+2=4$ come from. In the underlying abstract, informational substrate, the universe is timeless; it exists because no constraints forbid it.
The question also implicitly assumes temporal ordering and an external origin. Both time and causality are emergent, observer-dependent phenomena (as established in Paper~1).


\section*{Why something rather than nothing?}

This is analogous to asking why “heads” rather than “tails”. Both are equally abstract and neither is privileged. Observers necessarily find themselves in configurations that are not empty.


\section*{Why do particles follow a complex-valued wavefunction?}

The wavefunction is compression algorithm, just like pixels in MPEG encoded video follow discrete cosine function. Observed wave-like behavior emerges from this compression.  

Formally:
\[
\boxed{\text{Maximal Predictability}} \;\rightarrow\; \boxed{\text{Maximal Compression}} \;\rightarrow\; \boxed{\text{Maximal Probability}} 
\]

Configurations with minimal observer-indexed complexity \( \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma] \) dominate the probability measure:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) \propto \exp[-\lambda \, \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]].
\]

Hence, the “laws” of physics are statistical consequences of overwhelmingly probable, compressible configurations.
    


\section{Is the Universe Infinite?}

Based on all observational evidence, we observers are finite structures.
However, if reality is fundamentally informational, a natural question arises:
is the total information finite or infinite?

If it were finite, one would have to ask: finite by how many bits?
And who, or what, set that limit? Any fixed bound would itself require explanation.
A finite informational universe merely pushes the mystery back one level.

The only non-arbitrary conclusion is that the deep nature of everything
cannot be finitely bounded. At the most fundamental level, reality must be infinite—
perhaps not even informational in the familiar sense, but beyond any finite description.

\section*{Why does the universe expand? Is there a link to entropy?}

Expansion corresponds to increasing entropy because space and entropy are two sides of the same coin. 
Space is the \textbf{geometric view} to total entropy, so as entropy increases, the universe appears to expand.


\section*{Why did the universe start with zero entropy?}

Observers exist only in low-complexity, observer-compatible configurations. Among all \(2^n\) possible arrangements of an \(n\)-bit universe, only a tiny subset can support stable, self-referential structures (observers.  

A zero-entropy “beginning” does not imply temporal origin in an external sense; it is a boundary condition favored by the observer-conditioned measure:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) \propto \exp[-\lambda \, \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]].
\]

Low-entropy configurations are maximally compressible and thus most probable. High-entropy starting states would require additional information to encode the observer, increasing \(\mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]\) and suppressing probability:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma_{\rm high-entropy} \mid O) \ll \mathbb{P}(\gamma_{\rm zero-entropy} \mid O).
\]

Consequently, the arrow of time and increasing entropy emerge naturally:
\[
\text{Zero entropy} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Maximal compressibility} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Observer-compatible universe}.
\]

\textit{Speculative remark:} Other observer-compatible configurations may exist with different geometric histories. Whether all must exhibit apparent low-entropy origins is an open question.


\section*{If there are no metaphysical signals or interaction, then how does the observer “see” anything?}

Observation does not require signals. The universe perceived by an observer is already encoded within their wavefunction:
\[
\Psi_\gamma = \Psi_{\rm self} \otimes \Psi_{\rm env},
\]
where \(\Psi_{\rm self}\) defines the persistent observer structure and \(\Psi_{\rm env}\) encodes the surrounding environment.  

\textbf{Temporal and spatial extrapolation:} These are purely indexical—observer-compatible histories are extended by minimal-complexity continuations:
\[
\Psi_{\rm self}(t+1) \sim \arg\min_{\gamma_{0:t+1}} \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma_{0:t+1}],
\quad
\Psi_{\rm env} \sim \text{compressible extensions of } \Psi_{\rm self}.
\]

Apparent laws, structures, and predictability emerge statistically:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) \propto \exp[-\lambda \, \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]].
\]

No external signals are required; perception is emergent from correlations within the observer-compatible configuration space.


\section*{Does the Theory support QBism?}

QBism interprets the wavefunction as representing an agent’s subjective degrees of belief about measurement outcomes, rather than an element of objective physical reality.

The present theory does not adopt a belief-based interpretation. Instead, it treats probabilities as arising from objective structure, grounded in compressibility and statistical typicality.

In this framework, observers find themselves in universes that admit efficient descriptions, i.e., those with low algorithmic complexity relative to their structure. Such universes are statistically favored.

Furthermore, observers capable of reasoning and forming models achieve greater compression of their environment. As a result, intelligent observers are more likely to persist,
not due to subjective belief, but due to their alignment with highly compressible and therefore more probable structures.

Thus, while QBism places the observer’s beliefs at the center of the formalism, this theory instead emphasizes objective probabilistic structure and informational efficiency.


\section*{How does Gödel incompleteness affect the theory?}

Gödel’s theorems constrain formal systems capable of arithmetic, but this theory targets ontological closure rather than formal deductive completeness.
Embedded observers will encounter Gödel-style limits in reasoning, which manifest as constraints on compression and representation, not on the underlying informational substrate itself.


\section*{Is a black hole singularity a point of infinite density?}

Its the opposite. Singularities correspond to zero-entropy states with no degrees of freedom to encode microstructure, as demonstrated in Paper-2.
Infinite-density interpretations would compress poorly and are suppressed statistically. This aligns with the informational mapping of geometry to entropy, independent of spacetime embedding.



\section*{Are we living in a simulation?}

We are living in a simulation-like universe, as derived in Paper-3. All observer-compatible configurations exist within a single informational substrate.
Simulated and non-simulated realities are ontologically equivalent.
Hence, the question of “living in a simulation” is ill-posed; everything arises from the same abstract information.



\section*{Is the recursive simulation hypothesis supported within the theory?}

Yes, but only in a restricted sense. Observers may discover how to simulate themselves as informational structures.
Such sub-simulations correspond to configurations within the same underlying informational substrate, rather than requiring a separate ontological level.

However, fully faithful and unbounded recursive simulations are informationally extremely costly.
Within this framework, where probability is tied to compressibility, such configurations are strongly suppressed.

This raises a natural question: what is the probability that an observer finds themselves in such a universe?
The theory suggests that this probability is low, as highly complex, deeply nested simulations are less compressible and therefore less likely.



\section*{Why do we age and die?}

The observer’s future is limited by spectral complexity of their wavefunction, \(\psi_O\).
Let \(\Sigma[\gamma]\) denote the number of independent frequency-phase components required to encode future history \(\gamma\).
The probability of persistence scales as:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) \propto \exp(-\alpha \, \Sigma[\gamma]).
\]

Accumulation of memories, skills, and internal structure increases \(\Sigma[\gamma]\), reducing the measure of compatible continuations.
Aging and death are the geometric and statistical manifestations of combinatorial exhaustion: the set of observer-compatible configurations eventually vanishes.


\section*{Does the theory explain why the expansion rate of the universe is near-critical?}

Conditional on increasing entropy and typical emergence, observers are most likely to find themselves near the peak of the microstructure count (maximum compressibility).
This automatically correspond to a near-critical expansion regime.



\section*{Is this just Solipsism? Am I the Only One Who Exists?}

No. The theory does not privilege a single observer, nor does it impose any constraint limiting the number of observers to one.
Instead, observers are treated as informational structures arising within a probabilistic ensemble of possible universes.

Within this framework, the number of observers is itself a variable determined by the structure of the underlying description.
Configurations that support multiple observers are not excluded; rather, they are expected whenever they admit efficient (i.e., compressible) descriptions.

Solipsism would require that only a single observer exists despite the absence of any structural constraint enforcing such uniqueness.
This would correspond to a highly specific and therefore less probable configuration.

By contrast, universes containing many similar or correlated observers can often be described more efficiently, and are therefore statistically favored.
The existence of multiple observers is thus not an additional assumption, but a natural consequence of the theory’s probabilistic and informational principles.


\section*{Does the Claim That Compression Implies Maximal Probability Depend on Kolmogorov Complexity?}

No. The claim does not rely on Kolmogorov complexity or any specific formal notion of algorithmic complexity.
What matters is the description length of a configuration: the shorter and more compressible the description, the higher its probability.

In this theory, description length is defined via the spectral content of the wavefunction: frequencies, phases, and minimal-resolution representations.
Any encoding could be used; the essential property is that more compressible configurations dominate the measure. 

Solomonoff induction provides the general principle: simpler descriptions occur more frequently in the ensemble of possibilities.
The specific compression algorithm or representation is irrelevant, as long as it consistently reflects the informational content of the system.

Therefore, compressed configurations are overwhelmingly probable, not because Kolmogorov complexity is assumed, but because shorter, simpler descriptions are favored in
the probabilistic measure of the theory.
