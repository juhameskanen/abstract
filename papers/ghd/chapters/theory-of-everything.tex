\chapter{A Zero-Parameter Theory of Everything}

\section{Unifying Quantum Mechanics and Gravity}

At the deepest level, the universe is static and informational. There is no dynamical generation, no external time, and no need for a simulator or metaphysical agent. Everything that exists is a configuration in an enormous information space. For $n$ bits, there are $2^n$ possible configurations. Observers experience a “flow of time” simply by traversing a sequence of these configurations:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Configuration Space:} All possible states exist as static information.  
\item \textbf{Traversal:} Time is the ordinal sequence an observer finds themselves in.  
\end{itemize}

In such a universe, the central question is: \emph{Which configurations are observers most likely to inhabit?} The answer lies in compression: observers overwhelmingly find themselves in the \textbf{most compressible} structures. This gives rise to both quantum mechanics and general relativity as emergent phenomena.  

We quantify this using two complementary notions of complexity:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Spectral Complexity} $C_Q$ — compression of microscopic wave-like correlations.  
\item \textbf{Geometric Complexity} $C_G$ — compression of macroscopic relational structures in spacetime.
\end{itemize}



\section{Spectral Complexity: Why Particles Wave}

The wavefunction is nature’s spectral compression of microscopic correlations. We penalize high-frequency components to favor smooth, predictable states:

\begin{equation}
C_Q = \sum_i k_{\text{eff}}^2 |A_i|^2,
\end{equation}

where $k_{\text{eff}} = \min(|k|, N-|k|)$ in a discrete domain $\mathbb{Z}_N$. Sharp gradients and high frequencies are costly, so wave-like behavior emerges naturally as the optimal spectral representation.  

Particles appear wave-like because “waves compress correlations best.”



\section{Geometric Complexity: Why Spacetime Curves}

Observers also require coherent, well-defined boundaries, which are efficiently encoded in a geometric manifold. The geometric complexity functional is:

\begin{equation}
C_G = \alpha \int R \sqrt{-g} \, d^4x + \beta \sum |a + \Gamma(v,v)|^2,
\end{equation}

where:  

\begin{itemize}
\item $R$ is the Ricci scalar curvature — the “cost” of bending spacetime.  
\item $\Gamma(v,v)$ represents acceleration along a trajectory; non-geodesic motion increases the description length.  
\item $\alpha, \beta$ are weighting constants.  
\end{itemize}

Smooth, geodesic motion compresses histories most efficiently.



\section{Resolving Singularities with Spectral Compression}

In classical GR, curvature can become infinite at singularities. In an informational universe, such singularities are impossible: they would require infinite spectral description. The probability of a configuration is weighted by $e^{-C_Q}$, so states needing infinite spectral resources are naturally suppressed:

\[
\text{Singularities } \Rightarrow 0 \text{ probability}.
\]

The spectral “codec” acts as a low-pass filter, smoothing out infinite curvature.  

Nature’s “compression algorithm” prevents infinities from appearing.



\section{The Joint Dominance Principle}

Observers experience reality according to the \emph{joint minimization} of spectral and geometric complexity. The probability of a particular history $\gamma$ for an observer $O$ is:

\begin{equation}
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) = \frac{1}{Z_O} \exp \Big[ -\lambda ( C_Q(\gamma) + C_G(\gamma) + C_{\text{int}}(\gamma,G) ) \Big],
\end{equation}

where $C_{\text{int}}$ accounts for the interaction between spectral and geometric compression. Physics emerges where spectral and geometric simplicity align, producing a coherent and smooth world.



\section{Emergent Physical Laws}

Treating total complexity as a generalized action \(S\), the observed dynamics correspond to stationary points \(\delta S = 0\):

\subsection*{Wave Equations}

Varying spectral complexity with respect to the wavefunction yields:

\begin{equation}
\nabla^2 \psi = 0,
\end{equation}

which, in the presence of a clock or sampling frequency, recovers the Schrödinger or Helmholtz form.  

\subsection*{Field Equations}

Varying geometric complexity with respect to the metric reproduces Einstein’s field equations:

\begin{equation}
R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2} g_{\mu\nu} R = 8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}

The laws of physics are \emph{stationary points of minimal information cost}.



\section{Predictions and Observational Consequences}

Because a single wavefunction can be embedded in multiple geometric configurations with nearly equal complexity, the observer sees apparent stochasticity or “noise” at the interface of scales. This produces:

\begin{itemize}
\item Fundamental variance or “quantization error” when compressing high-dimensional spectral information into spacetime.  
\item Potentially measurable effects in extreme events, e.g., black hole mergers or Hawking radiation.  
\end{itemize}



\section{Conclusion}

The universe is fundamentally informational. Observers inhabit the most compressible trajectories in configuration space. Quantum mechanics emerges from \textbf{spectral compression} of microscopic correlations. General relativity emerges from \textbf{geometric compression} of macroscopic relational structures.  

The overarching principle is simple yet profound:

\begin{quote}
\emph{The universe is the minimal information capable of containing observers.}
\end{quote}

From this single principle, both quantum mechanics and gravity arise naturally, without invoking dynamical axioms, external simulators, or metaphysical agents.  

Physics is just the statistical dominance of low-complexity, efficiently encoded histories.
