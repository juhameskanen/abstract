This theory proposes that the conflict arises because we treat GR and QM as fundamental laws. In this framework, they are not laws, but **emergent descriptions**. Unification is achieved not through new particles or extra dimensions, but through a single principle: \textbf{Universal Compression}.

\[
\mathbb{P}(\gamma \mid O) = \frac{1}{Z_O} \exp\Big[-\lambda\, \mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]\Big], \quad \gamma \in \Gamma_O

Where the total complexity \(\mathcal{C}_O[\gamma]\) is the sum of three compression costs:
\[
\mathcal{C}_O[\gamma] = C_Q[\gamma] + C_G[\gamma] + C_{\text{obs}}[\gamma; O]

\section{The Dual Nature of Compression}

Reality emerges from the tension between two ways of saving "space" in the informational substrate:

\subsection{Spectral Complexity ($C_Q$): Why Particles Wave}

At the microscopic scale, the universe is best represented in a spectral basis. Smooth, wave-like correlations compress efficiently, whereas sharp discontinuities are "expensive."

The cost is defined by the effective frequency \(k_{\text{eff}}\):
\begin{equation}
C_Q = \sum_i k_{\text{eff}}^2 |A_i|^2, \quad k_{\text{eff}} = \min(|k|, N-|k|)
\end{equation}

Particles appear as waves because waves are the optimal codec for microscopic correlations.


\subsection{Geometric Complexity ($C_G$): Why Spacetime Curves}

While waves handle micro-correlations, observers require macroscopic "arenas" to maintain boundaries. The most efficient way to encode these relational structures is through a smooth manifold.
The cost is represented by the Einstein-Hilbert term:
\begin{equation}
C_G = \alpha \int R \sqrt{-g} \, d^4x + \beta \sum |a + \Gamma(v,v)|^2
\end{equation}
Gravity is not a force; it is the \textbf{geometric interpretation} of the most probable (least complex) relational paths.

In a zero-parameter theory, these are not "inputs" to the universe. They are scaling ratios that emerge from the joint minimization of complexity for a specific observer $O$.

\item \textbf{The Gravitational Constant (G):} This represents the "stiffness" of the geometric codec. It is the ratio of how much spectral energy (information density) is required to justify a "bend" in the geometric manifold.
\end{itemize}
