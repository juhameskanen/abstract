\begin{abstract}
Paper~II established that gravitational collapse corresponds to monotonically
decreasing execution-trace entropy, extrapolating to a zero-entropy state
at the singularity. Here we investigate the time-reversed process.
Beginning from a perfectly smooth, zero-entropy bitstring --- the unique
all-zero state --- we evolve the system through random bit-flip mutations
that incrementally increase Shannon entropy, and interpret each state
geometrically as a three-dimensional spatial configuration.

As entropy increases, three phenomena emerge without any hard-coded physical
laws, equations of motion, or imposed initial irregularities: (i) exponential
spatial expansion from a structureless point; (ii) hierarchical particle-like
structures whose abundance follows a lognormal distribution as a function of
entropy level; and (iii) scale invariance of particle size under global
expansion. These results are robust across more than two hundred distinct
filter definitions, geometric decoding schemes, and representational levels,
including purely combinatorial pattern-matching on the raw bitstring with no
geometric embedding whatsoever.

The universality of the lognormal abundance profile across all tested
representations indicates that it is an intrinsic combinatorial property of
entropy-increasing bitstrings rather than an artefact of any particular
decoding scheme. We conclude that cosmic expansion is the geometric
interpretation of increasing informational entropy, and that a rich,
structured universe emerges generically from a perfectly smooth initial state.
This result is stronger than Penrose's Weyl curvature hypothesis, which
requires the initial state to be smooth only within quantum limits: no
residual quantum irregularities are needed here.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Paper~II showed that gravitational collapse, analysed through the execution
trace of a computational simulation, converges to a zero-entropy state
at the classical singularity. The geometric description diverges; the
informational description converges to the simplest possible state. The
singularity is not a pathological endpoint but a geometric point.

The present paper asks what happens when we begin at that geometric point
and move in the opposite direction. Starting from the unique zero-entropy state
--- the all-zero bitstring, the same informational state that
corresponds to the singularity --- and increasing entropy through random
mutations, what geometric and structural features emerge? Do they resemble
those of the observed universe?

The central hypothesis is that cosmic expansion and the formation of
structure are, from the informational perspective of the \iame{} framework,
the geometric interpretation of a monotonically increasing entropy process.
Entropy increase is not a consequence of expansion; expansion is the
geometric reading of entropy increase. The two are the same phenomenon seen
through different lenses.

This hypothesis has a direct implication for the initial conditions problem.
A longstanding puzzle in cosmology is why the universe began in such an
exceptionally ordered state. Standard approaches, including Penrose's Weyl
curvature hypothesis \cite{penrose1979}, assume that the initial state was
smooth only within the limits imposed by quantum uncertainty: some residual
irregularity at the quantum scale is taken as necessary. In the present
framework, this assumption is unnecessary. The initial state is perfectly
smooth by construction --- zero entropy, uniquely determined, admitting no
fluctuations of any kind. The richness of the universe that emerges from it
is not a consequence of imposed irregularities but of entropy increase alone.

The paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:setup} describes the
computational setup, the geometric and combinatorial filter definitions, and
the role of bit-flip mutations. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the
numerical results. Section~\ref{sec:robustness} establishes the
filter-independence of the lognormal result. Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
interprets the findings in the context of the \iame{} framework and compares
them with existing cosmological approaches. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}
concludes.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Configuration Space and Entropy Evolution}

We define an execution trace $S_t$, $t = 0, \ldots, n$, where each $S_t$
is a binary string of fixed length $L$. The initial state $S_0$ is the
unique zero-entropy configuration: the all-zero string $0^L$. We evolve
$S_t$ via random bit-flip mutations, each of which flips a single uniformly
selected bit. This generates a sequence of states traversing the
configuration space in approximate order of increasing Shannon entropy,
starting from the unique minimum.

The Shannon entropy of each state is computed as:
\[
H(S_t) = -p_0(t)\log_2 p_0(t) - p_1(t)\log_2 p_1(t),
\]
where $p_0(t)$ and $p_1(t)$ are the empirical frequencies of zero- and
one-bits in $S_t$. The entropy increases monotonically from $H(S_0) = 0$
toward the maximum $H = 1$ bit as the bit frequencies equalise.

\subsection{On the Role of Bit-Flip Mutations}

It is important to clarify what the bit-flip mutation process represents
within the \iame{} framework. Paper~I establishes that the universe is a
static, timeless ensemble containing all $2^n$ possible configurations
simultaneously. There is no fundamental external dynamics or evolution: all
configurations exist and no physical process generates one from another.

What we perceive as temporal evolution, cosmic expansion, or the arrow of
time emerges internally as observers navigate ordered sequences within this
fixed configuration space. The bit-flip mutations of the present paper are
not proposed as a physical law governing the universe. They are a
computational sampling strategy: a practical method for traversing the
configuration space in approximate order of increasing entropy, starting from
the unique zero-entropy state and moving toward higher-entropy configurations.

Any other sampling strategy that generates an entropy-ordered sequence from
the same initial state would produce qualitatively identical results. The
specific trajectory through configuration space does not matter; what matters
is the entropy level of each visited state, because it is the entropy level,
not the trajectory, that determines the geometric and structural properties
of the configuration.

\subsection{Geometric Particle Filter}

Each state $S_t$ is decoded into a three-dimensional geometric configuration
via a decoding map $D : \{0,1\}^L \to \mathbb{R}^3$. Fixed-length binary
segments of $S_t$ are decoded as integer coordinates, which are then scaled
to produce spatial positions. The resulting point cloud is the geometric
interpretation of $S_t$.

We define a minimal three-level hierarchy of emergent structures:

\textbf{Elementary particles} are defined as pairs of decoded points
$(p_1, p_2)$ whose spatial separation falls below a distance threshold
$\delta_1$:
\[
0 < \mathrm{dist}(p_1, p_2) < \delta_1.
\]

\textbf{Atoms} are defined as triplets of elementary particles
$(e_1, e_2, e_3)$ satisfying:
\[
0 < \mathrm{dist}(e_i, e_j) < \delta_2 \quad \text{for all } i \neq j,
\]
with the additional constraint that the three pairwise distances are
approximately equal, imposing a near-equilateral configuration. The centre
of an atom is the geometric mean of its constituent points:
\[
\mathrm{centre}(\mathrm{atom}) =
\frac{1}{3}\sum_{i=1}^3 (x_i, y_i, z_i).
\]

\textbf{Molecules} form when two atom centres are sufficiently close:
\[
\mathrm{dist}(\mathrm{centre}(\mathrm{atom}_1),
\mathrm{centre}(\mathrm{atom}_2)) < \delta_3.
\]

This hierarchy is intentionally minimal and its specific definitions are
intentionally arbitrary. The threshold values $\delta_1, \delta_2, \delta_3$
are free parameters. The point of the geometric filter is not that it
correctly describes physical particles --- it does not --- but that it
preserves two qualitative features characteristic of real matter: the
hierarchical composition of structures from simpler components, and the
scale invariance of particle size under global expansion (particle sizes are
set by the distance thresholds, which are fixed, while the decoded spatial
extent grows with entropy).

\subsection{Recursive Pattern-Matching Filter}

An alternative, more fundamental filter operates directly on the bitstring
$S_t$ without any geometric embedding.

\textbf{Elementary particles} are defined as short substrings
$p \in \{0,1\}^k$ that occur within $S_t$. Their abundance is the count of
non-overlapping occurrences of $p$ in $S_t$.

\textbf{Composite particles} are formed recursively by concatenating
previously recognised particles. A composite pattern $P = p_1 p_2$ is valid
if both $p_1$ and $p_2$ are recognised particles and $P$ itself occurs in
$S_t$.

This approach detects structure purely from informational redundancy: a
pattern is a particle if it recurs. The hierarchy is constructed bottom-up
from frequent substrings to higher-order motifs, with no spatial geometry
involved at any stage. It preserves the same two qualitative features as the
geometric filter: hierarchical composition and size invariance under global
entropy increase.

The recursive pattern-matching filter is in one sense more fundamental than
the geometric filter, because it requires no choice of decoding map $D$ and
no distance thresholds. Its agreement with the geometric filter on the
statistical properties of emergent structures (Section~\ref{sec:robustness})
is therefore a non-trivial result.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

Evolving the system from $H = 0$ through increasing entropy levels, three
consistent phenomena emerge.

\textbf{Zero entropy yields a structureless point.} At $H(S_0) = 0$, the
decoded geometry is a single point: the all-zero bitstring maps to a unique
configuration regardless of the decoding scheme $D$. No structures of any
kind are detected by either filter. This is consistent with the zero-entropy
result of Paper~II and with the Lemma of Section~2.4 of that
paper: a zero-entropy bitstring decodes to a single structureless state
under any map.

\textbf{Entropy increase drives spatial expansion.} As entropy increases,
the decoded spatial extent grows exponentially. The all-zero state maps to a
single point; small deviations from zero entropy map to small spatial
configurations; high-entropy states map to configurations spanning the full
decoded range. The rate of spatial growth is approximately exponential in
the entropy, reproducing the qualitative behaviour of an expanding universe.
Crucially, the sizes of detected structures --- set by the distance
thresholds $\delta_i$ --- do not grow with the global expansion. This is
analogous to the scale invariance of physical particles under Hubble
expansion.

\textbf{Structure counts follow a lognormal distribution.} As entropy
increases through intermediate levels, elementary particles, atoms, and
molecules begin to appear. Their counts, plotted as a function of entropy
level $H$, trace a lognormal profile: zero at $H = 0$, a super-exponential
rise to a peak, and a long damped tail at high entropy. The peak location
shifts systematically with hierarchy level: elementary particles peak at
lower entropy than atoms, which peak at lower entropy than molecules,
consistent with a bottom-up emergence mechanism in which higher-order
structures require their components to be abundant first.

\section{Filter Independence}
\label{sec:robustness}

The most significant result of this paper is not the lognormal distribution
itself but its universality. We tested more than two hundred distinct filter
definitions, including:

\begin{itemize}
\item Geometric filters with varying distance thresholds $\delta_1,
      \delta_2, \delta_3$ spanning two orders of magnitude;
\item Geometric filters using different hierarchy definitions (pairs,
      triplets, quadruplets; equilateral, isoceles, and arbitrary
      configurations);
\item Different decoding maps $D$, including linear, logarithmic, and
      random projections from bitstrings to spatial coordinates;
\item Recursive pattern-matching filters with varying substring lengths $k$
      and varying composition rules;
\item Arbitrary pattern counting: selecting a fixed pattern at random ---
      for example, $010110100010$ --- and counting its occurrences in $S_t$
      as a function of entropy level.
\end{itemize}

In every case, the qualitative result is the same: zero structure at zero
entropy, a lognormal-shaped emergence curve, and long-tailed decay at high
entropy. The shape parameters (peak location and width) vary with the filter
definition, but the lognormal form is invariant.

The filter-independence extends across representational levels. Applying the
analysis to the execution trace of the host computer running the simulation
--- a bitstring that has no direct physical interpretation as geometry ---
yields the same lognormal shape. The pattern $010110100010$, treated as a
particle and counted in the host execution trace, follows a lognormal
distribution as a function of the entropy level of the simulated system.

This representational independence is the key result. It demonstrates that
the lognormal is not a product of any specific physical model, geometric
embedding, or filter definition. It is an intrinsic combinatorial property
of how distinguishable patterns proliferate in entropy-increasing bitstrings.
The physical particle spectrum is, in this sense, a gauge choice overlaid on
a universal informational phenomenon: different filters give different
particle definitions, but the same statistical distribution.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Expansion as entropy increase}

The identification of cosmic expansion with entropy increase is not merely
analogical. In the \iame{} framework, the geometric and informational
descriptions of a physical configuration are the same object viewed through
different lenses. The statement that the universe is expanding is, in this
framework, the geometric reading of the statement that the entropy of the
underlying configuration is increasing. These are not two correlated
phenomena but one phenomenon with two descriptions.

This has a sharp implication. The arrow of time, the thermodynamic second
law, and the expansion of the universe are not three independent features of
the observed world. They are one feature: the monotonic increase of
informational entropy, which reads as temporal succession from the
observer's internal perspective, as expansion from the geometric perspective,
and as entropy increase from the thermodynamic perspective.

\subsection{Resolution of the initial conditions problem}

The standard formulation of the initial conditions problem asks why the
universe began in such a low-entropy, highly ordered state. This question
presupposes that low-entropy initial states are improbable and require
explanation. Within the \iame{} framework, the zero-entropy initial state
requires no explanation at all: it is the unique, maximally compressible
configuration. Under the compression measure $P(c) \propto 2^{-\mathcal{C}(c)}$,
the zero-entropy state has the shortest description of any state in the
configuration space. It dominates the measure not by accident but because it
is the simplest possible starting point. The question is not why the universe
began in a low-entropy state but why one would expect anything else.

\subsection{Comparison with Penrose's Weyl curvature hypothesis}

Penrose's Weyl curvature hypothesis \cite{penrose1979} proposes that the
thermodynamic arrow of time originates in the asymmetry between initial and
final singularities: the initial singularity had vanishing Weyl curvature
(smooth, ordered), while final singularities (black holes) have diverging
Weyl curvature (chaotic, disordered). This is a geometric statement about
the initial state, motivated by the need to explain the arrow of time.

The present results go further in two respects. First, the initial state
here is not merely smooth within quantum limits but perfectly smooth:
zero entropy, uniquely determined, admitting no fluctuations. The universe
this generates is already rich enough to reproduce the qualitative features
of matter organisation and spatial expansion. No residual quantum
irregularities are required. Second, the smoothness of the initial state is
not an assumption but a derivation: within the \iame{} framework, the
compression measure selects the zero-entropy initial state as the most
probable starting point, so a smooth beginning is the expected outcome, not
a lucky accident.

\subsection{The Boltzmann brain problem}

A persistent difficulty for frameworks that postulate a large or infinite
configuration space is the Boltzmann brain problem: in a sufficiently large
ensemble, random high-entropy fluctuations that momentarily produce a
conscious observer are exponentially more numerous than structured,
persistent observers embedded in a low-entropy universe. The compression
measure of the \iame{} framework suppresses these fluctuations directly:
high-entropy configurations have high description length and are
exponentially disfavoured. The lognormal emergence profile is the
quantitative signature of this suppression: structure peaks at intermediate
entropy and decays at high entropy, so the highest-entropy configurations
are precisely the ones with the least structure, not the most.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper establishes three results.

First, cosmic expansion is the geometric interpretation of increasing
informational entropy. Beginning from the unique zero-entropy state
identified in Paper~II as the informational singularity, entropy increase
drives exponential growth of the decoded spatial configuration without any
hard-coded dynamics, equations of motion, or cosmological constant.

Second, hierarchical particle-like structures emerge spontaneously from
entropy increase, with abundance statistics following a universal lognormal
distribution. This distribution is robust across more than two hundred filter
definitions, geometric decoding schemes, and representational levels,
including purely combinatorial pattern-matching on the raw bitstring with no
geometric embedding. The lognormal is an intrinsic combinatorial property of
entropy-increasing bitstrings, not an artefact of any particular physical
model.

Third, these results connect the forward process of this paper (expansion
from zero entropy) directly to the reverse process of Paper~II (collapse to
zero entropy). The singularity is the unique informational state at
which both processes terminate or originate. The richness of the universe
that emerges from it is not fine-tuned: it is generic.

Open questions include the identification of the structural
filter whose lognormal parameters constitute a derivation of the physical
particle spectrum, the quantitative comparison of the emergent expansion rate
with observed cosmological parameters, and the connection between the
lognormal microstructure distribution and the Dirac spectral unification
conjecture developed in Paper~I.
