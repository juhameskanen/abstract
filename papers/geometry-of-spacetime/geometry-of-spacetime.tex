\begin{abstract}
We derive the geometric structure of spacetime from the information-theoretic
framework established in Paper~I \dots III \cite{meskanen2001, meskanen2002, meskanen2003},
in which the universe is static, timeless, and finite, with bitstring length
$n$ --- the number of binary degrees of freedom available to the universe.
Starting from a zero-entropy initial state and evolving toward thermodynamic
equilibrium via random bit mutations, we identify the maximum spatial resolution
with $2^n$ Planck lengths --- because $2^n$ is the number of distinguishable
configurations of $n$ bits, each mapping to a distinct spatial cell ---
and the temporal resolution with $n \ln n$ Planck times, the expected number
of bit-flip steps to reach full entropy saturation by the coupon-collector
result. Their ratio defines a dimensionless \emph{aspect ratio}
$\mathcal{A}(n) = 2^n / (n \ln n)$ that encodes the complete geometric
structure of spacetime from $n$ alone.
Calibrating $\mathcal{A}$ against the inflationary expansion of the early
universe, expressed in Planck units, yields $n \approx 184$ --- meaning
the universe has 184 binary degrees of freedom, giving $2^{184} \approx
2.5 \times 10^{55}$ distinguishable configurations, consistent with the
$\approx 6.2 \times 10^{60}$ Planck-length radial reach of inflation
(up to the $4\pi$ spherical correction).
The e-fold count exceeds 60, consistent with
the independently derived lower bound from standard inflationary cosmology.
Extending the framework to black holes by identifying
$n_{\mathrm{bh}} = \log_2(r_s / \ell_P)$, where $r_s$ is the Schwarzschild
radius, we recover the Bekenstein--Hawking entropy of a solar-mass black hole.
These results suggest that the geometric structure of spacetime, including the
holographic scaling of black hole entropy, is a structural consequence of
finite bitstring capacity rather than an independent postulate.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

General Relativity describes the geometry of spacetime with extraordinary
precision, yet provides no account of why spacetime has a geometry at all, or
why matter curves it. The equations are accepted without a deep explanation of
what space is made of such that it \emph{can} curve. Similarly, the
Bekenstein--Hawking entropy formula \cite{bekenstein1973, hawking1975}
establishes that black hole entropy scales with horizon area, but the
microscopic origin of that entropy --- the nature of the degrees of freedom
being counted --- remains an open question.

The present paper addresses both questions within the framework developed in
Papers~I--III \cite{meskanen2001, meskanen2002, meskanen2003}.
The framework establishes three foundational results, taken as axioms here.
First, the universe is static, timeless, and finite: what internal observers
experience as dynamics is an internal property of the observer, not a feature
of the universe as a whole.
Second, the universe is characterised by a single integer $n$ --- the
\emph{bitstring length}, the number of binary degrees of freedom available to
the universe. This is not the same as the information content (entropy), which
ranges from 0 to $n$ as the system evolves; it is the \emph{capacity} of the
system, analogous to the number of memory cells in a computer, regardless of
what is stored in them.
Third, the initial state of the universe, and the interior state of a black
hole singularity, are zero-entropy states --- all $n$ bits are in a single
configuration.

From these axioms, Papers~II and III established that spacetime geometry is
the geometric interpretation of information: a zero-entropy state maps, under
any geometric encoding, to a structureless point, while increasing entropy maps
to expanding geometry. The present paper asks a more precise question: given
$n$, what is the \emph{resolution} of that geometry in both space and time,
and does the resulting structure reproduce known results from cosmology and
black hole thermodynamics?

\section{Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

\textbf{Axiom 1 (Static universe).} The universe is static, timeless, and
finite. Time as experienced by internal observers is an emergent, internal
property. The universe operates on a strict, finite bitstring of length $n$.

\textbf{Axiom 2 (Bitstring length).} The universe is characterised by a single
integer $n \in \mathbb{N}$, the \emph{bitstring length} or \emph{capacity}.
The phase space of the universe contains exactly $2^n$ distinct configurations.
The \emph{entropy} $S$ of any particular configuration ranges from $0$ (one
microstate --- total order) to $n$ (maximum disorder); $S$ is not the same as
$n$.

\textbf{Axiom 3 (Zero-entropy boundary conditions).} The initial state of the
universe is a zero-entropy state ($S = 0$, one microstate). The interior state
of a black hole singularity is a zero-entropy state.

\textbf{Corollary (Geometric point).} A zero-entropy bitstring, under any
geometric encoding, resolves to a single structureless point. Zero entropy
implies one microstate implies no internal distinctions implies no geometric
degrees of freedom. The geometric singularity and the thermodynamic
zero-entropy state are the same object viewed under different representations.

\textbf{Corollary (Expanding geometry).} Starting from the zero-entropy state
and evolving toward thermodynamic equilibrium via random bit mutations, the
entropy growth curve
\[
  S(t) = n \cdot \bigl(1 - e^{-k t}\bigr)
\]
generates, under any geometric encoding, a geometry that begins as a point
and expands monotonically toward the maximum spatial resolution $2^n \ell_P$.

\section{Spatial and Temporal Resolutions}
\label{sec:resolutions}

\subsection{Spatial Resolution}

A bitstring of length $n$ has exactly $2^n$ distinct configurations. If each
configuration maps to a distinct spatial position (the geometric encoding of
Paper~II), then the maximum spatial resolution of the system is $2^n$
Planck-length cells:
\[
  \Delta x_{\max} = 2^n \cdot \ell_P.
\]
This is the radial resolution: the observable universe has a single
characteristic length scale (the radius of the observable sphere), and
$2^n \ell_P$ is the maximum radius achievable for a universe of bitstring
length $n$.

The universe has $184$ binary degrees of freedom, giving a phase space
of $2^{184} \approx 2.5 \times 10^{55}$ distinguishable configurations and
hence a maximum spatial resolution of $2^{184}$ Planck lengths
$\approx 4 \times 10^{20}$ m. The \emph{current} information content (entropy)
$S(t)$ is much smaller than $n$ early in the evolution and approaches $n$ only
at full equilibrium. 

\subsection{Temporal Resolution}

The natural temporal resolution is the number of bit-flip steps required to
bring a zero-entropy system to full entropy saturation. By the coupon-collector
result from combinatorics \cite{coupon}, a random process that flips bits one
at a time requires, on average, $n \ln n$ steps to visit all $n$ positions at
least once. Identifying one bit-flip with one Planck time $t_P$:
\[
  \Delta t_{\max} = n \ln n \cdot t_P.
\]

\subsection{The Aspect Ratio}

Dividing the spatial resolution by the temporal resolution yields a
dimensionless quantity:
\[
  \mathcal{A}(n) = \frac{2^n}{n \ln n}.
\]
This \emph{aspect ratio} encodes the complete geometric structure of the
spacetime block from $n$ alone.

\textbf{Lemma (Monotonicity).} $\mathcal{A}(n)$ is strictly increasing in $n$
for $n \geq 3$.

\begin{proof}
The derivative of $2^n/(n\ln n)$ with respect to $n$ is proportional to
$\ln 2 \cdot n \ln n - (1 + \ln n)$, which is positive for all $n \geq 3$. \qed
\end{proof}

\textbf{Corollary.} A subsystem such as a black hole has smaller $n$ than the
full universe and therefore a strictly smaller aspect ratio.

\subsection*{From 1D resolution to 3D observable}

The spatial resolution $2^n \ell_P$ is a radial (1D) quantity. The physical
observable universe is a 3D sphere, so:
\[
  \boxed{\text{1D information radius} \;\xrightarrow{\;\times\, 4\pi R^2\;}\; \text{3D physical surface area}}
\]

\section{Calibration Against Inflationary Cosmology}
\label{sec:calibration}

\subsection{Converting to Planck Units}

Standard inflationary cosmology gives for the inflationary epoch \cite{inflation}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Duration: $\Delta t \approx 10^{-35}$ s
  \item Radial expansion: $\Delta x \approx 10^{26}$ m
\end{itemize}
In Planck units:
\[
  \Delta t_P = \frac{10^{-35}}{5.39\times10^{-44}} \approx 1.86\times10^{8}
  \;\text{Planck times (bit-flip steps)},
\]
\[
  \Delta x_P = \frac{10^{26}}{1.616\times10^{-35}} \approx 6.19\times10^{60}
  \;\text{Planck lengths (spatial cells)}.
\]
The dimensionless inflationary aspect ratio is:
\[
  \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{inf}}
  = \frac{\Delta x_P}{\Delta t_P}
  = \frac{6.19\times10^{60}}{1.86\times10^{8}}
  \approx 3.3\times10^{52}.
\]

\subsection{Solving for $n$}

Setting $\mathcal{A}(n) = \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{inf}}$:
\[
  \frac{2^n}{n \ln n} = 3.3\times10^{52}.
\]
This has no closed-form solution. Binary search over $n \in [1, 300]$ gives:
\[
  \boxed{n \approx 184.}
\]
\noindent
This means the universe has \textbf{184 binary degrees of freedom}, giving
$2^{184} \approx 2.5\times10^{55}$ distinguishable configurations and a
maximum radial resolution of $2^{184}$ Planck lengths. This is the
\emph{capacity} of the universe, not its current entropy.
Verification: $2^{184}/(184 \times \ln 184) \approx 2.6\times10^{52}$,
within the precision of the inflationary input data. \checkmark

\section{Correspondence with Inflationary E-Folds}
\label{sec:efolding}

Standard inflationary cosmology requires a minimum of 60 e-folds
\cite{inflation}. The value $n \approx 184$ corresponds to
$n \ln 2 \approx 127$ natural-log units of expansion, exceeding the
60-e-fold lower bound. This correspondence is non-trivial: $n$ was fixed by
the aspect ratio calibration, not by the e-fold count.

\section{Black Hole Entropy}
\label{sec:blackhole}

\subsection{Identifying $n_{\mathrm{bh}}$}

A black hole has a Schwarzschild radius $r_s$ encoding a radial length of
$r_s/\ell_P$ Planck cells. By the same identification as for the universe, the
bitstring length of the black hole is:
\[
  n_{\mathrm{bh}} = \log_2\!\left(\frac{r_s}{\ell_P}\right).
\]
For a solar-mass black hole, $r_s \approx 2950$ m, giving
$r_s/\ell_P \approx 1.83\times10^{38}$ and
$n_{\mathrm{bh}} \approx 127$.
Note $n_{\mathrm{bh}} \approx 127 < n \approx 184$: the black hole has fewer
binary degrees of freedom than the full universe, consistent with the
monotonicity corollary.

\subsection{Predicted Surface Area}

The event horizon is two-dimensional. By the same encoding, its area in
Planck units is $(2^{n_{\mathrm{bh}}})^2 = 2^{2n_{\mathrm{bh}}}$ Planck areas:
\[
  A_{\mathrm{model}}
  = 2^{2n_{\mathrm{bh}}} \cdot \ell_P^2
  = (1.83\times10^{38})^2 \times (1.616\times10^{-35})^2
  \approx 8.7\times10^{6}\;\mathrm{m}^2.
\]

\subsection{Comparison with Bekenstein--Hawking}

\[
  A_{\mathrm{BH}} = 4\pi r_s^2 \approx 1.09\times10^{8}\;\mathrm{m}^2,
  \qquad
  S_{\mathrm{BH}} = \frac{A_{\mathrm{BH}}}{4\ell_P^2} \approx 1.05\times10^{77}.
\]
The ratio:
\[
  \frac{A_{\mathrm{model}}}{A_{\mathrm{BH}}}
  = \frac{(r_s/\ell_P)^2}{4\pi(r_s/\ell_P)^2}
  = \frac{1}{4\pi}.
\]
The discrepancy is exactly $4\pi$ --- the ratio of a flat raster area to a
spherical surface area. Imposing spherical geometry (as GR requires):
\[
  A_{\mathrm{model}} \times 4\pi
  = 4\pi \times 8.7\times10^6
  \approx 1.09\times10^8\;\mathrm{m}^2
  = A_{\mathrm{BH}}. \quad \checkmark
\]

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The aspect ratio as a structural invariant}

The aspect ratio $\mathcal{A}(n) = 2^n/(n\ln n)$ is not a free parameter but
a structural invariant determined by $n$ alone. Different physical systems
--- the universe, a solar-mass black hole, a more massive black hole --- differ
only in $n$, and their geometric properties follow accordingly.

\subsection{The $4\pi$ factor}

The $4\pi$ discrepancy is the signature of spherical geometry: the model
computes a flat raster resolution, while GR demands a spherical horizon.
The $4\pi$ correction is fixed entirely by spherical symmetry, not introduced
by hand.

\subsection{Limitations}

The model does not yet account for the stress-energy content of the universe.
The comparison with inflation uses the vacuum-dominated epoch deliberately;
a full treatment must incorporate matter and energy.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that the geometric structure of spacetime follows from a single
integer $n$. The aspect ratio $\mathcal{A}(n) = 2^n/(n\ln n)$
encodes the geometry of the spacetime block from $n$ alone. Calibrated against
the inflationary expansion in Planck units, the model yields $n \approx 184$
binary degrees of freedom, giving $2^{184} \approx 2.5\times10^{55}$
distinguishable configurations and a maximum radial resolution consistent with
the size of the observable universe. The e-fold count of inflation emerges as
an independent consistency check.

Extended to black holes via $n_{\mathrm{bh}} = \log_2(r_s/\ell_P)$, the
framework recovers the Bekenstein--Hawking entropy up to the factor $4\pi$ from
spherical geometry. No constants of General Relativity ($G$, $c$, $\hbar$) are
introduced into the information-theoretic derivation; they enter only when
converting back to physical units.
