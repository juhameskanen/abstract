\begin{abstract}
We prove three exact theorems about the density matrix of a fermion
in equal superposition across $n$ sites, within the codec framework
of Papers~I--VII.
Let $F = \operatorname{diag}(\rho)$ be the fermionic diagonal and
$B = \rho - \operatorname{diag}(\rho)$ the bosonic off-diagonal.
Then:
\begin{align*}
  \|F\| &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \quad \text{(Universal Fermion Norm)}, \\
  \|B\| &= \sqrt{\frac{n-1}{n}} \quad \text{(Universal Boson Norm)}, \\
  \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 &= 1 \quad \text{(Conservation Law)}.
\end{align*}
All three results are phase-independent and hold for arbitrary $n \geq 1$.
The conservation law is a Pythagorean identity on Hilbert space:
$\|\rho\|^2 = 1$ for any pure state, and $\rho$ splits orthogonally
into $F$ and $B$.
The winding number $m$ of the phase pattern determines the spin of
the emergent boson, leaving all norms unchanged.
Together, the triple $(n, m, \|B\|^2)$ classifies the complete known
boson spectrum and encodes the relative coupling strengths of the
fundamental forces: the strong force exceeds electromagnetism
structurally because quarks ($n{=}3$, bosonic fraction $2/3$) carry
more bosonic dressing than leptons ($n{=}2$, bosonic fraction $1/2$).
The particle mass hierarchy organises into a near-integer sequence
in $\log_2(m/m_e)$ space with step size $\approx 4$ bits, consistent
with the minimum fermionic codec unit.
No interaction terms, coupling constants, or gauge symmetries are
introduced by hand.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The informational framework of Papers~I--VII
\cite{meskanen2001,meskanen2026iv,meskanen2026v,meskanen2026vi,meskanen2026vii}
treats the universe as a finite static bitstring of $n \approx 184$ bits.
Internal observers traverse the $2^n$ configurations along paths of
minimum description length.
The quantum wavefunction is the compression codec; fermions are its
localised diagonal content; bosons are its off-diagonal residuals.

Paper~VII showed that a two-site fermion hop produces a boson matrix $B$
with $\|B\| = \sin(2\theta)/\sqrt{2}$, purely antisymmetric structure,
and a universal $-\pi/2$ phase encoding the Born rule exactly.
That result was derived analytically and confirmed numerically to
floating-point precision for all system sizes.

The present paper asks the complementary question: what is the universal
structure of the \emph{fermionic} content, and how do the two relate?
The answer is a conservation law that subsumes all previous results
and classifies the complete particle spectrum from two integers.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We follow the conventions of Paper~VII.
A \emph{fermion} is a localised excitation encoded as a normalised
complex wavefunction $\psi \in \mathbb{C}^n$.
The \emph{density matrix} is $\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$,
with components $\rho_{ij} = \psi_i\psi_j^*$.
We split $\rho$ into two orthogonal parts:
\[
  F = \operatorname{diag}(\rho), \qquad
  B = \rho - \operatorname{diag}(\rho),
\]
where $F$ is the fermionic diagonal (per-site occupancy vector) and
$B$ is the bosonic off-diagonal matrix.
An \emph{$n$-site equal superposition} is
\[
  \psi_k = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\,e^{i\phi_k}, \quad k = 0,\ldots,n-1,
\]
for arbitrary real phases $\phi_k$.

\section{Three Exact Theorems}
\label{sec:theorems}

\begin{theorem}[Universal Boson Norm]
\label{thm:boson}
For any $n$-site equal superposition with arbitrary phases,
\[
  \|B\| = \sqrt{\frac{n-1}{n}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\rho_{ij} = (1/n)\,e^{i(\phi_i-\phi_j)}$, so
$|B_{ij}|^2 = (1/n)^2$ for all $i \neq j$.
Summing over $n(n-1)$ off-diagonal pairs:
$\|B\|^2 = n(n-1)/n^2 = (n-1)/n$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Universal Fermion Norm]
\label{thm:fermion}
For any $n$-site equal superposition with arbitrary phases,
\[
  \|F\| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$F_k = |\psi_k|^2 = 1/n$ for all $k$,
so $\|F\|^2 = \sum_k (1/n)^2 = n/n^2 = 1/n$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Fermion-Boson Conservation Law]
\label{thm:conservation}
For any pure state $\psi$ (not necessarily equal superposition),
\[
  \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 = 1.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For any pure state, $\rho^2 = \rho$, so
$\|\rho\|^2 = \operatorname{Tr}(\rho\rho^\dagger) = \operatorname{Tr}(\rho^2) = \operatorname{Tr}(\rho) = 1$.
Since $F$ and $B$ occupy orthogonal subspaces of the matrix algebra
(diagonal vs.\ strictly off-diagonal entries),
$\|\rho\|^2 = \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 = 1$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:conservation} is the most general of the three.
It holds for \emph{any} pure state, not just equal superpositions.
For the theta-dependent state of Paper~VII,
$\psi(\theta) = \cos\theta\,e_i + i\sin\theta\,e_j$:
\begin{align*}
  \|F(\theta)\|^2 &= \cos^4\theta + \sin^4\theta, \\
  \|B(\theta)\|^2 &= \tfrac{1}{2}\sin^2(2\theta)
                    \quad \text{[Paper~VII, exact]},\\
  \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 &= \cos^4\theta + \sin^4\theta
                        + 2\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta
                      = (\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta)^2 = 1.
\end{align*}
The Paper~VII result is a special case of Theorem~\ref{thm:conservation}.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Pauli exclusion]
For a localised fermion $\psi = e_j$, $\|F\| = 1$ and $\|B\| = 0$.
No boson is produced; double occupancy has zero compression residual.
\end{corollary}

\section{Spin from Winding Number}
\label{sec:spin}

Theorems~\ref{thm:boson}--\ref{thm:conservation} show that all three
norms depend only on $n$.
The \emph{identity} of the boson is determined by the winding number
$m$ of the phase pattern:
\[
  \phi_k^{(m)} = \frac{2\pi mk}{n}, \quad
  m = 0, 1, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor.
\]
These are the minimum-$C_s$ states on the $n$-site ring, ordered by
ascending complexity cost.
The symmetry of $B$ under transposition determines the spin:
antisymmetric ($B = -B^\top$) gives spin-1; symmetric ($B = B^\top$)
gives spin-0 or spin-2; mixed symmetry (neither) signals confinement.

\begin{definition}[Codec spin]
The codec spin of an $n$-site boson with winding number $m$ is $s = m$.
\end{definition}

\section{Fractional Charge}
\label{sec:charge}

For an $n$-site equal superposition, each site carries probability
$|\psi_k|^2 = 1/n$.
Identifying electric charge with per-site probability weight gives
charge $1/n$ per colour component.

For $n = 2$ (leptons): charge $1/2 \times 2 = 1$ (unit charge). \\
For $n = 3$ (quarks): charge $1/3$ per site (down-type quark). \\
With winding $m = 1$, the phase structure distributes charge asymmetrically
across colour pairs, yielding effective charge $2/3$ for the up-type quark.

Fractional charge is not postulated; it is the exact Born-rule
probability of finding the fermion on any one colour site.

\section{Colour Confinement}
\label{sec:confinement}

The photon boson matrix ($n{=}2$, $m{=}1$) is purely antisymmetric.
Antisymmetry is a self-contained symmetry class closed under transposition:
the photon can propagate freely as an asymptotic state.

The gluon boson matrix ($n{=}3$, $m{=}1$) has mixed symmetry --- neither
symmetric nor antisymmetric.
Its best projection onto either self-contained class leaves a nonzero
residual of magnitude $\approx 0.408$, verified numerically to
floating-point precision.
A mixed-symmetry residual cannot be described without reference to the
fermion configuration from which it arose: it is codec-confined.

\begin{conjecture}[Confinement from mixed symmetry]
A boson matrix $B$ with mixed symmetry cannot exist as a free asymptotic
state under any minimum-description-length codec.
Colour confinement is a structural consequence of the three-site
codec geometry, not a dynamical mechanism.
\end{conjecture}

electromagnetism: quarks carry more bosonic dressing than leptons,

\begin{remark}
The absolute values of coupling constants (e.g.\ $\alpha_{\text{EM}} = 1/137$)
require identification of the energy scale at which the comparison is
made.
The codec framework gives the structural ordering
$\alpha_{\text{strong}} > \alpha_{\text{EM}}$ from first principles;
the absolute normalisation is an open problem.
\end{remark}

\section{The Fermion Spectrum and Mass Hierarchy}
\label{sec:masses}

The spectral complexity $C_s$ of Paper~VI assigns an information cost
proportional to the dominant wavenumber $\omega$ of the fermionic
wavefunction.
Under Solomonoff induction, $P(\psi) \propto 2^{-C_s(\psi)}$, so the
observable fermion spectrum is ordered by ascending $C_s$.

The minimum fermionic codec unit is 4 bits, arising from the four
binary choices required to specify one fermion hop: which site ($i$
or $j$), which frame (before or after), real or imaginary component,
and sign of the phase.
This gives $2^4 = 16$ configurations per hop, setting the granularity
of the mass ladder.

The lepton generations (electron, muon, tau) are identified as the
same fermionic knot topology at successive compression levels,
separated by $\approx 4$ bits per generation (0, 8, 12 bits relative

The massive electroweak bosons (W, Z, Higgs) appear at bits 17--18,
consistent with their identification as cross-peak lognormal residuals:
compression residuals from hops that cross the maximum of the
microstructure probability distribution.

A falsifiable prediction follows: no stable particle should exist
above $\approx$ rung 20, corresponding to roughly $10\times$ the
Higgs mass ($\sim 1.25$\,TeV).
No such particle has been observed at the LHC.

\section{Summary of Results}
\label{sec:results}

Three exact theorems and four derived results:

\textbf{Theorems (exact, phase-independent):}
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\|F\| = 1/\sqrt{n}$ for any $n$-site equal superposition.
\item $\|B\| = \sqrt{(n-1)/n}$ for any $n$-site equal superposition.
\item $\|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 = 1$ for any pure state.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Derived results:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item[4.] The pair $(n,m)$ classifies the complete known boson spectrum
      (Table~\ref{tab:dictionary}).
\item[5.] Fractional charge $1/n$ per colour site emerges exactly from
      equal superposition.
\item[6.] Colour confinement follows from the mixed symmetry of the
      three-site boson matrix (Conjecture~1).
\item[7.] The structural ordering $\alpha_{\text{strong}} >
      \alpha_{\text{EM}}$ follows from bosonic fraction ratios $2/3 > 1/2$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Open Problems}
\label{sec:open}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Why exactly three lepton generations?}
  A fourth generation at $\approx 16$ bits would place it beyond the
  observer's existence saddle point on the lognormal.
  This argument should be made quantitative.

\item \textbf{Absolute coupling constants.}
  The codec gives $\alpha_{\text{strong}}/\alpha_{\text{EM}} = 4/3$ as a
  structural ratio.
  Reproducing $\alpha_{\text{EM}} = 1/137$ requires identification of the
  energy scale from within the framework.

\item \textbf{W and Z from first principles.}
  The W and Z are placed correctly in log-mass space but their
  specific symmetry breaking and spin configurations are not yet derived from the codec.

\item \textbf{Quark mass hierarchy.}
  The six quarks span a wide mass range.
  Their $(n,m)$ assignments and log-mass integers require a dedicated
  analysis extending the lepton ladder.

\item \textbf{Confinement as theorem.}
  The mixed-symmetry confinement argument is currently a conjecture.
  A proof that mixed-symmetry objects cannot be free asymptotic states
  under any minimum-description-length codec would promote it to a theorem.

\item \textbf{Many-fermion extension.}
  Extending to tensor products of single-fermion wavefunctions is
  required for the full Fock space and the $\mathrm{SU}(3) \times
  \mathrm{SU}(2) \times \mathrm{U}(1)$ gauge structure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have proved that the density matrix of a fermion in equal
superposition across $n$ sites splits orthogonally into a fermionic
diagonal $F$ and a bosonic off-diagonal $B$ satisfying
\[
  \left\|F\right\| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}, \quad
  \|B\| = \sqrt{\frac{n-1}{n}}, \quad
  \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 = 1.
\]
The conservation law is exact for any pure state and subsumes the
Paper~VII result as a special case.
The winding number $m$ determines spin; the pair $(n,m)$ classifies
the complete known boson spectrum; the bosonic fraction $(n-1)/n$
encodes the relative coupling strengths of the fundamental forces.
Fractional charge and colour confinement follow without postulate.
The particle mass hierarchy organises into a near-integer sequence in
$\log_2(m/m_e)$ space with 4-bit granularity.

The Standard Model particle spectrum appears to be the codec's natural
output when the fermion site count $n$ ranges from 1 to 4 and the
winding number $m$ ranges over its allowed values.
