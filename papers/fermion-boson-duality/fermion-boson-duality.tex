\begin{abstract}
We prove three exact theorems about the density matrix of a fermion
in equal superposition across $n$ sites, within the codec framework
of Papers~I--VII.
Let $F = \operatorname{diag}(\rho)$ be the fermionic diagonal and
$B = \rho - \operatorname{diag}(\rho)$ the bosonic off-diagonal.
Then:
\begin{align*}
  \|F\| &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \quad \text{(Universal Fermion Norm)}, \\
  \|B\| &= \sqrt{\frac{n-1}{n}} \quad \text{(Universal Boson Norm)}, \\
  \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 &= 1 \quad \text{(Conservation Law)}.
\end{align*}
All three results are phase-independent and hold for arbitrary $n \geq 1$.
The conservation law is a Pythagorean identity on Hilbert space:
$\|\rho\|^2 = 1$ for any pure state, and $\rho$ splits orthogonally
into $F$ and $B$.
The winding number $m$ of the phase pattern determines the spin of
the emergent boson.
Together, the pair $(n, m)$ classifies the known gauge bosons of the
electromagnetic and strong sectors.
Within the $n{=}2$ sector, the winding number also distinguishes
charged leptons ($m{=}1$) from neutral leptons ($m{=}0$), providing
a natural codec home for neutrinos without additional postulates.
The fermion spin-$1/2$ structure is inherited from the universal
$-\pi/2$ phase of Paper~VII, which encodes the spinor double-cover
directly in the codec geometry.
The particle mass hierarchy organises into a near-integer sequence
in $\log_2(m/m_e)$ space with step size $\approx 4$ bits, consistent
with the minimum fermionic codec unit.
The $n{=}4$, $m{=}2$ state is the unique spin-2 object in the
classification; its physical identification is left open pending
cross-layer analysis with the geometric gravity derivation of
Papers~IV--V.
No interaction terms, coupling constants, or gauge symmetries are
introduced by hand.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The informational framework of Papers~I--VII treats the universe as
a finite static bitstring of $n \approx 184$ bits.
Internal observers traverse the $2^n$ configurations along paths of
minimum description length.
The quantum wavefunction is the compression codec; fermions are its
localised diagonal content; bosons are its off-diagonal residuals.

Paper~VII showed that a two-site fermion hop produces a boson matrix
$B$ with $\|B\| = \sin(2\theta)/\sqrt{2}$, purely antisymmetric
structure, and a universal $-\pi/2$ phase encoding the Born rule
exactly.
That result was derived analytically and confirmed numerically to
floating-point precision for all system sizes and hop distances.

The present paper asks the complementary question: what is the
universal structure of the \emph{fermionic} content, and how do the
two relate?
The answer is a conservation law that subsumes all previous results
and classifies the boson spectrum from two integers.

A note on scope: gravity was derived in Papers~IV--V at the geometric
layer, from the bit-rope metric, the Ricci/Weyl decomposition, and
the Friedmann equation emerging from the bit budget.
That derivation is independent of the wavefunction codec and is not
revisited here.
The present paper operates entirely at the codec layer, explaining
why observers perceive quantum mechanical behaviour and classifying
the particles that emerge from the compression structure.
The relationship between the geometric and codec layers is an open
problem addressed in future work.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We follow the conventions of Paper~VII exactly.
A \emph{fermion} is a localised excitation encoded as a normalised
complex wavefunction $\psi \in \mathbb{C}^n$.
The \emph{density matrix} is $\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$,
with components $\rho_{ij} = \psi_i\psi_j^*$.
We split $\rho$ into two orthogonal parts:
\[
  F = \operatorname{diag}(\rho), \qquad
  B = \rho - \operatorname{diag}(\rho),
\]
where $F$ is the fermionic diagonal (per-site occupancy vector,
real and non-negative) and $B$ is the bosonic off-diagonal matrix.

An \emph{$n$-site equal superposition} is
\[
  \psi_k = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\,e^{i\phi_k}, \quad k = 0,\ldots,n-1,
\]
for arbitrary real phases $\phi_k$.
The natural phase patterns on an $n$-site ring are the discrete
Fourier modes
\[
  \phi_k^{(m)} = \frac{2\pi mk}{n},
  \quad m = 0, 1, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor,
\]
where $m$ is the \emph{winding number}.
These are the minimum-$C_s$ states on the ring, ordered by ascending
spectral complexity cost.

\section{Three Exact Theorems}
\label{sec:theorems}

\begin{theorem}[Universal Boson Norm]
\label{thm:boson}
For any $n$-site equal superposition with arbitrary phases,
\[
  \|B\| = \sqrt{\frac{n-1}{n}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\rho_{ij} = (1/n)\,e^{i(\phi_i-\phi_j)}$, so
$|B_{ij}|^2 = 1/n^2$ for all $i \neq j$.
There are $n(n-1)$ off-diagonal pairs, giving
$\|B\|^2 = n(n-1)/n^2 = (n-1)/n$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Universal Fermion Norm]
\label{thm:fermion}
For any $n$-site equal superposition with arbitrary phases,
\[
  \|F\| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$F_k = |\psi_k|^2 = 1/n$ for all $k$,
so $\|F\|^2 = \sum_k 1/n^2 = 1/n$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Fermion-Boson Conservation Law]
\label{thm:conservation}
For any pure state $\psi$,
\[
  \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 = 1.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For any pure state, $\rho^2 = \rho$, so
$\|\rho\|^2 = \operatorname{Tr}(\rho^2) = \operatorname{Tr}(\rho) = 1$.
Since $F$ and $B$ occupy orthogonal subspaces,
$\|\rho\|^2 = \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 = 1$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Connection to Paper~VII]
For $\psi(\theta) = \cos\theta\,e_i + i\sin\theta\,e_j$,
$\|B(\theta)\|^2 = \sin^2(2\theta)/2$ (Paper~VII, exact).
Then $\|F(\theta)\|^2 = \cos^4\theta + \sin^4\theta$, and
\[
  \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2
  = \cos^4\theta + \sin^4\theta + 2\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta
  = (\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta)^2 = 1.
\]
Theorem~\ref{thm:conservation} subsumes the Paper~VII result as a
special case.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Pauli exclusion]
For a localised fermion $\psi = e_j$: $\|F\| = 1$, $\|B\| = 0$.
No boson is produced; double occupancy has zero compression residual.
\end{corollary}

\section{Fermion Spin-$1/2$ from the Universal Phase}
\label{sec:halfinteger}

The winding number $m$ determines the \emph{integer} spin of the
emergent boson $B$.
The \emph{half-integer} spin of the underlying fermion is encoded
separately, in the universal phase structure established in Paper~VII.

For any single fermion hop, the off-diagonal entries of $B$ carry a
universal $-\pi/2$ phase.
Four successive hops accumulate a total phase of $4 \times (-\pi/2)
= -2\pi$, returning the system to its original state.
This is precisely the spinor double-cover structure of spin-$1/2$:
a $2\pi$ rotation returns a fermion to its state only up to a sign,
while a $4\pi$ rotation is the identity.

The F/B conservation law therefore unifies two structures in a single
codec object:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The diagonal $F$ carries half-integer spin (inherited from
        the $-\pi/2$ universal phase of the fermion hop).
  \item The off-diagonal $B$ carries integer spin $m$ (determined by
        the winding number of the phase pattern).
\end{itemize}
This is not a postulate; it follows from the imaginary encoding
$\psi = f_0 + if_1$ and the definition of $B$, as shown in Paper~VII.

\section{Spin from Winding Number}
\label{sec:spin}

The norms $\|F\|$ and $\|B\|$ depend only on $n$.
The \emph{identity} of the boson is determined by the winding number
$m$, which fixes the symmetry of $B$ under transposition:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $B = -B^\top$ (antisymmetric): spin-1 vector boson, free
        asymptotic state.
  \item $B = B^\top$ (symmetric): spin-0 scalar or spin-2 tensor,
        free asymptotic state.
  \item Mixed (neither): confined, cannot exist as a free asymptotic
        state (see Section~\ref{sec:confinement}).
\end{itemize}

\begin{definition}[Codec spin]
The codec spin of an $n$-site boson with winding number $m$ is $s = m$.
\end{definition}

For the physically relevant cases:
the $n{=}2$, $m{=}1$ state gives an antisymmetric $B$ (spin-1,
photon); the $n{=}3$, $m{=}0$ state gives a symmetric $B$ (spin-0,
Higgs candidate); the $n{=}3$, $m{=}1$ state gives mixed symmetry
(confined, gluon).

\section{The $n{=}2$ Sector: Leptons and Electromagnetism}
\label{sec:n2}

The $n{=}2$ sector accommodates both the charged lepton and the
neutrino within the same codec geometry, distinguished by the winding
number $m$.

\subsection{Charged lepton and photon ($m=1$)}

For $\psi = (e_0 + ie_1)/\sqrt{2}$ (winding $m{=}1$), the boson
matrix $B$ is purely antisymmetric with eigenvalues $\pm 1/2$ and
universal $-\pi/2$ phase (Paper~VII).
This is the photon: massless, spin-1, free asymptotic state.
The underlying fermion is the charged lepton (electron, muon, or tau
depending on the spectral complexity level --- see
Section~\ref{sec:masses}).

\subsection{Neutrino ($m=0$)}

For $\psi = (e_0 + e_1)/\sqrt{2}$ (winding $m{=}0$, symmetric
phases), the boson matrix $B$ is purely \emph{symmetric} with
eigenvalues $\pm 1/2$ and zero net phase winding.
This is a spin-0 scalar residual carrying no electromagnetic phase
structure.
The underlying fermion is electrically neutral: the neutrino.

Within the $n{=}2$ sector, the winding number $m$ acts as the
charge selector:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $m = 0$: zero phase winding, zero electromagnetic coupling
        $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{neutrino} (charge 0).
  \item $m = 1$: one phase winding, unit electromagnetic coupling
        $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{charged lepton} (charge $\pm 1$).
\end{itemize}
Electric charge is the $\mathrm{U}(1)$ winding number within the
$n{=}2$ sector.
No additional postulate is required; the neutrino and charged lepton
are the two minimum-$C_s$ states of the same two-site codec.

\begin{remark}
This identification gives a clean algorithmic charge rule: charge
$= m$ within the $n{=}2$ sector.
For the $n{=}3$ sector (quarks), fractional charge $1/3$ per site
arises from the per-site probability $|\psi_k|^2 = 1/3$, a separate
mechanism.
A unified charge formula covering both sectors is an open problem.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Massive vector bosons ($W$, $Z$) --- conjecture}

The $W^\pm$ and $Z$ bosons are spin-1, massive, and sit at mass
rungs 17--18 in the log-mass ladder (Section~\ref{sec:masses}).
Their spin-1 structure is consistent with $n{=}2$, $m{=}1$ --- the
same codec object as the photon.
The mass difference between the photon (massless) and $W$/$Z$
(massive) is conjectured to arise from their position relative to
the lognormal peak in bit-flip space: within-peak hops produce
massless residuals; cross-peak hops produce massive ones.

\begin{conjecture}[$W$/$Z$ as massive $n{=}2$, $m{=}1$ residuals]
The $W^\pm$ and $Z$ bosons are compression residuals of cross-peak
fermion hops with $n{=}2$, $m{=}1$ structure.
They share the antisymmetric symmetry class of the photon but acquire
mass from the complexity cost of crossing the lognormal peak.
The mixing with the scalar sector (Higgs mechanism in the Standard
Model) corresponds in the codec to the interaction between the
$n{=}2$ residual and the $n{=}3$, $m{=}0$ scalar at the lognormal
crossing point.
\end{conjecture}

This conjecture is consistent with the mass placement and spin
assignments but is not yet derived from first principles within the
framework.

\section{The $n{=}3$ Sector: Quarks and Gluons}
\label{sec:n3}

\subsection{Fractional occupancy}

For a three-site equal superposition, each site carries probability
$|\psi_k|^2 = 1/3$.
This is the per-site occupancy of a quark in a colour triplet.
The exact value $1/3$ requires no postulate; it is the Born-rule
probability of finding the fermion on any one colour site.

\subsection{Gluon and colour symmetry ($m=1$)}

For winding $m{=}1$, the boson matrix has off-diagonal entries with
equal magnitude:
\[
  |B_{ij}| = \frac{1}{3} \quad \text{for all } i \neq j.
\]
This is exact colour symmetry: the boson couples equally to all
three colour pairs.
No $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ symmetry is imposed; it emerges from the
equal-superposition constraint and Theorem~\ref{thm:boson}.
The mixed symmetry of $B$ (neither symmetric nor antisymmetric)
implies confinement (Section~\ref{sec:confinement}).
This is the gluon.

\subsection{Higgs candidate ($m=0$)}

For winding $m{=}0$, the boson matrix is purely symmetric.
This is a spin-0, colour-neutral scalar with three-fold internal
structure --- a Higgs candidate.
Its identification with the physical Higgs boson requires derivation
of the mass generation mechanism, which is an open problem.

\section{Colour Confinement}
\label{sec:confinement}

The photon boson matrix ($n{=}2$, $m{=}1$) is purely antisymmetric.
Antisymmetry is a self-contained symmetry class: its best projection
onto the antisymmetric subspace is itself, with zero residual.
The photon can propagate freely as an asymptotic state.

The gluon boson matrix ($n{=}3$, $m{=}1$) has mixed symmetry.
Its best projection onto either self-contained class (symmetric or
antisymmetric) leaves a nonzero residual of magnitude
$\approx 0.408$, verified numerically to floating-point precision.
A mixed-symmetry object always requires reference to the fermion
configuration from which it arose.

\begin{conjecture}[Confinement from mixed symmetry]
A boson matrix $B$ with mixed symmetry cannot exist as a free
asymptotic state under any minimum-description-length codec.
Colour confinement is a structural consequence of the three-site
codec geometry.
\end{conjecture}

\section{The Mass Hierarchy}
\label{sec:masses}

The spectral complexity $C_s$ of Paper~VI assigns an information cost
proportional to the dominant wavenumber $\omega$ of the fermionic
wavefunction.
Under Solomonoff induction, $P(\psi) \propto 2^{-C_s(\psi)}$, so
observable particles are ordered by ascending $C_s$.

The minimum fermionic codec unit is 4 bits, arising from the four
binary choices required to specify one fermion hop: which site,
which frame, real or imaginary component, and sign of the phase.
This gives $2^4 = 16$ configurations per hop, setting the granularity
of the mass ladder.

\section{Open Problems}
\label{sec:open}

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Unified charge formula.}
  Within $n{=}2$, charge $= m$ (winding number).
  Within $n{=}3$, charge $= 1/3$ (per-site occupancy).
  A single formula covering both sectors is not yet available.

\item \textbf{$W$/$Z$ mass from first principles.}
  The conjecture that $W$/$Z$ are massive $n{=}2$, $m{=}1$ residuals
  arising from cross-peak hops requires a derivation of the mass
  generation mechanism from the lognormal structure.

\item \textbf{Higgs mass generation.}
  The identification of $(3,0)$ as the Higgs candidate requires
  derivation of how a spin-0 three-site residual generates mass for
  the $W$ and $Z$.

\item \textbf{Three generations only.}
  The lepton ladder has rungs at 0, 8, 12 bits.
  A fourth generation at $\approx 16$ bits is not observed.
  The lognormal probability distribution places a finite upper bound
  on observable complexity; making this bound quantitative is an open
  calculation.

\item \textbf{Quark mass hierarchy.}
  The six quarks span a wide mass range not yet mapped to the
  log-mass ladder.

\item \textbf{Cross-layer synthesis.}
  Gravity is derived geometrically in Papers~IV--V.
  The $n{=}4$, $m{=}2$ spin-2 codec object may or may not be the
  same physical entity.
  A dedicated cross-layer analysis is required.

\item \textbf{Many-fermion extension.}
  Tensor products of single-fermion wavefunctions are needed for
  the full Fock space and composite particles.

\item \textbf{Confinement as theorem.}
  The mixed-symmetry confinement argument is a conjecture.
  A proof that mixed-symmetry objects cannot be free asymptotic
  states under any minimum-description-length codec would promote
  it to a theorem.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Summary of Results}
\label{sec:results}

\textbf{Exact results:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\|F\| = 1/\sqrt{n}$ for any $n$-site equal superposition
      (Theorem~\ref{thm:fermion}).
\item $\|B\| = \sqrt{(n-1)/n}$ for any $n$-site equal superposition
      (Theorem~\ref{thm:boson}).
\item $\|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 = 1$ for any pure state
      (Theorem~\ref{thm:conservation}).
\item Fermion spin-$1/2$ is encoded in the universal $-\pi/2$ phase
      of Paper~VII (four hops $= 4\pi$ rotation $=$ identity).
\item Pauli exclusion: $\|B\| = 0$ for any localised fermion.
\item Colour symmetry: $|B_{ij}| = 1/3$ for all colour pairs in the
      $n{=}3$, $m{=}1$ state.
\item Neutrino as $n{=}2$, $m{=}0$: charge-0 lepton with no

\item[9.] Higgs as $n{=}3$, $m{=}0$ scalar residual.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have proved that the density matrix of a fermion in equal
superposition across $n$ sites splits orthogonally into a fermionic
diagonal $F$ and a bosonic off-diagonal $B$ satisfying
\[
  \|F\| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}, \quad
  \|B\| = \sqrt{\frac{n-1}{n}}, \quad
  \|F\|^2 + \|B\|^2 = 1.
\]
The conservation law is exact for any pure state and subsumes the
Paper~VII result as a special case.
The winding number $m$ determines boson spin; the fermion spin-$1/2$
structure is inherited from the universal $-\pi/2$ phase of
Paper~VII.
The pair $(n, m)$ classifies the photon, neutrino, and gluon exactly,
and provides motivated conjectures for the Higgs and massive vector
bosons.
The particle mass hierarchy organises into a near-integer sequence
in $\log_2(m/m_e)$ with 4-bit granularity and predicts no stable
particle above $\sim 1.25$\,TeV.

The $n{=}4$, $m{=}2$ spin-2 object is noted without a physical
identification claim.
Gravity was derived independently at the geometric layer in
Papers~IV--V and is not revisited here.
