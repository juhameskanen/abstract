\begin{abstract}
Papers~I--V established that the universe is a finite static bitstring
of length $n \approx 2^{184}$ bits; that gravitational collapse converges to a
zero-entropy singularity; that expansion is the geometric reading of
entropy increase; that the aspect ratio of spacetime is fixed by $n$
alone; and that a relational scale factor $R(t)$ emerges from strict
information conservation via the counting equation
$R(t) = (\rho_{\text{fabric}}(t) + k(t))/n$.
The present paper applies this framework at cosmological scale and
establishes two results.
First, the three-phase expansion profile of standard cosmology ---
early rapid growth, matter-driven deceleration, late-time acceleration
--- emerges from $R(t)$ when matter abundance follows lognormal curves
in bit-flip time, with no cosmological constant, inflaton field, or
dark energy term. Lognormal emergence of micro-structures
in entropy-increasing bitstrings was demonstrated in Paper~III.
Second, the informational level hierarchy $L_0 \supset L_{\rm DM}
\supset L_{\nu} \supset L_{\rm bar}$ provides a natural mechanism for
dark matter: $L_{\rm DM}$ structures are the first-order lognormal
condensates of the entropy gradient, gravitating via the counting
equation by exactly the same mechanism as ordinary matter.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The present paper addresses two questions that standard cosmology
answers with separate, independently postulated mechanisms: why does
the universe expand in three phases (early inflationary growth,
matter-driven deceleration, late-time acceleration), and what is dark
matter?

In $\Lambda$CDM, late-time acceleration requires a cosmological
constant $\Lambda$ whose physical origin remains unexplained; dark
matter requires a new particle species whose nature has not been
determined after decades of direct-detection effort.

The present paper shows that both phenomena are consequences of the
single informational counting principle established in Papers~I--V.
No new parameters are introduced. No new particles are postulated.
The cosmological constant and dark matter both dissolve into the
level structure of the bit budget.

The paper is structured as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:hierarchy} establishes the informational level
hierarchy and identifies dark matter with the first emergent level.
Section~\ref{sec:bosons} states the boson mechanism that makes
$L_{\rm DM}$ electromagnetically invisible.
Section~\ref{sec:lognormal} recalls the lognormal abundance law from
Paper~III and applies it to each level.
Section~\ref{sec:expansion} derives the three-phase expansion profile.
Section~\ref{sec:data} discusses the relationship to $\Lambda$CDM
observational data.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} states the three foundational results.
Section~\ref{sec:future} identifies open problems.

\section{The Informational Level Hierarchy}
\label{sec:hierarchy}

The bit budget $n$ is partitioned at each moment between the
spacetime fabric $L_0$ (free, independently addressable bits) and a
nested sequence of composite structures $L_{\rm DM}, L_\nu, L_{\rm bar},
\ldots$ formed by the entropy gradient $dS/dt$.
The partition is exact at every tick:

\begin{equation}
  n = \rho_{\text{fabric}}(t) + \sum_k w_k \cdot N_k(t),
  \label{eq:budget}
\end{equation}

where $w_k$ is the bit-width of a level-$k$ entity and $N_k(t)$ is
its instantaneous count. Information is strictly conserved; no bits
enter or leave the system.

The four levels relevant at cosmological scale are:

\begin{description}
  \item[$L_0$ --- Spacetime fabric.]
    Bits not consumed by any composite structure.
    This is the rope of Paper~IV: its length at time $t$ is
    $\rho_{\text{fabric}}(t) = S(t) \cdot n - m(t)$, where
    $S(t) = I_{\max}(1 - e^{-kt})$ is the total unfolded entropy
    and $m(t) = \sum_k w_k N_k(t)$ is the total bits consumed by
    structures.

  \item[$L_{\rm DM}$ --- Dark matter.]
    The first-order condensates of the entropy gradient: the earliest
    and simplest structures to emerge as $dS/dt$ rises from zero.
    Their existence is a \textbf{prediction} of the framework (see
    Section~\ref{sec:lognormal}), not a postulate.
    Their bit-width $w_{\rm DM}$ is smaller than that of hadronic
    structures.

  \item[$L_\nu$ --- Neutrinos.]
    Second-order structures, emerging later than $L_{\rm DM}$ along
    the entropy gradient.

  \item[$L_{\rm bar}$ --- Baryons.]
    Third-order structures (protons, neutrons), emerging later still.
    Higher composite states (atoms, molecules) are relabellings of the
    same baryon number and do not enter the counting equation
    separately: a water molecule withdraws the same number of bits
    from $L_0$ as its constituent hadrons, because it is the same
    bits restructured, not new bits consumed.
\end{description}

The relational scale factor is:

\begin{equation}
  R(t) = \frac{\rho_{\text{fabric}}(t) + k(t)}{n},
  \label{eq:scalefactor}
\end{equation}

where $k(t) = \sum_k N_k(t)$ is the total count of composite entities
(each counts as one addressable token, regardless of its bit-width).

\section{Why Dark Matter is Dark: Bosons as Compression Residuals}
\label{sec:bosons}

In the \theory{} framework there is no electromagnetic force as a
separately postulated interaction. Bosons --- including photons ---
are compression residuals: the information shed at the boundary of a
fermionic compression event. When a fermion structure forms, it
compresses a region of the bitstring; the bits that cannot be absorbed
into the compressed structure are emitted as a boson. The boson is
the exhaust of the compression, not a mediator exchanged between
pre-existing charges.

This framing has a direct consequence for dark matter. $L_{\rm DM}$
structures are first-order condensates: they form earlier, from a
simpler compression template, than the $L_\nu$ and $L_{\rm bar}$
structures that give rise to photon-type residuals.
An $L_{\rm DM}$ compression event sheds residuals of a different
symmetry class --- or no residual detectable by an $L_{\rm bar}$-level
instrument at all --- because the compression geometry that produces
photon-type bosons has not yet emerged at that level of the hierarchy.

Consequently:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $L_{\rm DM}$ gravitates by the counting equation
    (equation~\ref{eq:scalefactor}): it withdraws bits from $L_0$,
    reducing $\rho_{\text{fabric}}(t)$, which an internal observer
    reads as curvature. This is identical to how $L_{\rm bar}$
    gravitates; the mechanism is level-independent.
  \item $L_{\rm DM}$ does not interact with $L_{\rm bar}$-level
    detectors electromagnetically, not because it is ``hidden'' or
    ``weakly coupled'', but because the boson residuals it sheds do
    not belong to the compression class that $L_{\rm bar}$ instruments
    are constituted to register.
\end{itemize}

This is not a new postulate. It follows from the boson-as-residual
principle established in Papers~VIII--XI for the particle spectrum,
applied to the cosmological hierarchy. The ``darkness'' of dark matter
is a structural consequence of its level, not a property requiring
separate explanation.

\section{Lognormal Matter Abundance}
\label{sec:lognormal}

Paper~III established that the abundance of any extractable structure
in an entropy-increasing bitstring follows a lognormal-like
distribution as a function of bit-flip time $t$, independently of
the filter used to define the structure (verified across more than
two hundred filter definitions). This result is stated here as an
\textbf{exact result} for the class of finite bitstring models; its
extension to the physical universe is a \textbf{hypothesis} that the
simulations are designed to test.

The entropy gradient $dS/dt = I_{\max} k e^{-kt}$ acts as the sole
driving force for structure formation: once $dS/dt \to 0$ (white
noise, entropy saturated), no new structures can emerge and all
existing ones dissolve toward the ground state. This is enforced
analytically by gating the lognormal with the entropy gradient:

\begin{equation}
  N_k(t) = A_k \cdot
    \frac{\exp\!\left(-\frac{(\ln t - \mu_k)^2}{2\sigma_k^2}\right)}
         {t\,\sigma_k\sqrt{2\pi}}
    \cdot e^{-k_{\rm rate}\,t},
  \label{eq:lognormal}
\end{equation}

where $A_k$, $\mu_k$, $\sigma_k$ are level-specific amplitude, peak
location, and width in bit-flip time, and the factor $e^{-k_{\rm rate}t}$
ensures $N_k \to 0$ as the entropy gradient vanishes. The gate is not
an additional postulate; it is the mathematical statement that
$dS/dt$ is the only source of order.

The saturation timescale is $t_{\rm sat} = n \ln n \approx 960$
bit-flip ticks for $n = 184$, and the unfolded rope length at time
$t$ is $S(t) \cdot n$. The constraint

\begin{equation}
  m(t) = \sum_k w_k N_k(t) \leq S(t) \cdot n
  \label{eq:constraint}
\end{equation}

must hold at all times: structure formation cannot exceed the entropy
that has been released. Violation of this constraint --- more knots
than rope length --- produces the informational analogue of a
singularity and is treated as a parameter boundary condition, not
a physical prediction.

\paragraph{Status of the lognormal hypothesis for each level.}

\begin{description}
  \item[$L_{\rm DM}$.]
    \textbf{Prediction of the framework.} Paper~III's filter-independence
    result guarantees lognormal emergence for any first-order structure
    in an entropy-increasing bitstring. No external data constrains
    $\mu_{\rm DM}$, $\sigma_{\rm DM}$, or $A_{\rm DM}$; these are
    open parameters to be fixed by matching to observational anchors
    once $E = mc^2$ is derived within the framework.

  \item[$L_\nu$, $L_{\rm bar}$.]
    \textbf{Hypothesis under test.} $\Lambda$CDM predicts monotonically
    diluting number densities $n(t) = n_0 (1+z(t))^3$ for both
    species after their respective freeze-out epochs: no rise, no
    peak, no fall. The lognormal form in equation~(\ref{eq:lognormal})
    is a genuinely different prediction that disagrees with $\Lambda$CDM.
    Section~\ref{sec:data} discusses what would be required for an
    honest falsifiability test.
\end{description}

\section{The Three-Phase Expansion Profile}
\label{sec:expansion}

Figure~\ref{fig:expansion} shows $R(t)$, $S(t)$, and the three
matter abundance curves for one representative parameter set.
Three phases are immediately visible.

\textbf{Phase 1 --- Early rapid expansion.}
Before significant matter formation ($N_k \approx 0$ for all $k$),
$\rho_{\text{fabric}}(t) \approx S(t) \cdot n$ and
$R(t) \approx S(t)$.
The system is in the De~Sitter regime: the scale factor tracks the
entropy saturation curve, which rises steeply from the singularity.
No inflaton field is required; the rapid early growth is the
geometric reading of maximum entropy gradient.

\textbf{Phase 2 --- Matter-driven deceleration.}
As $L_{\rm DM}$, $L_\nu$, and $L_{\rm bar}$ structures rise along
their lognormal curves, bits are withdrawn from $L_0$.
$\rho_{\text{fabric}}(t)$ falls below $S(t) \cdot n$ and $R(t)$
departs downward from the De~Sitter background.
An internal observer whose measuring rods are built from the same
structures perceives this withdrawal as gravitational deceleration.
No force law is imposed; the geometry tightens because information
is redistributed from fabric to structure.

\textbf{Phase 3 --- Late-time re-expansion.}
The falling tails of the lognormal curves are combinatorially
inevitable: as entropy approaches saturation, the gradient that
drives structure formation vanishes, and structures dissolve back
toward white noise.
Bits are released to $L_0$; $\rho_{\text{fabric}}(t)$ recovers and
$R(t)$ rises again.
An internal observer perceives this release as accelerating expansion.
No cosmological constant is introduced; the acceleration is the
geometric reading of structural evaporation in a saturating
information field.

The three-phase profile is not tuned. It is the generic shape of
$R(t)$ whenever (i) matter abundance follows equation~(\ref{eq:lognormal})
and (ii) the constraint~(\ref{eq:constraint}) is satisfied.
Its qualitative features are insensitive to the specific values of
$\mu_k$, $\sigma_k$, $A_k$, provided the parameter set does not
violate the rope constraint.

\section{Relationship to \texorpdfstring{$\Lambda$}{Lambda}CDM Observational Data}
\label{sec:data}

The $\Lambda$CDM number-density curves used as a reference baseline
for $L_\nu$ and $L_{\rm bar}$ in Figure~\ref{fig:expansion} are not
theory-independent observational facts. They are derived quantities
within a specific theoretical framework: $n(t) = n_0(1+z(t))^3$,
where $z(t)$ is obtained by integrating the Friedmann equation with
Planck~2018 parameters ($H_0 = 67.4$~km/s/Mpc, $\Omega_m = 0.315$,
$\Omega_\Lambda = 0.685$).
Feeding these curves into the \theory{} simulation tests the \theory{}
framework against a $\Lambda$CDM prediction, not against raw data.

The actual observational anchors that $\Lambda$CDM is itself fitted
to --- the CMB power spectrum, the Type~Ia supernova distance-redshift
relation, the baryon acoustic oscillation scale, the light element
abundances from BBN --- are what the \theory{} framework must ultimately
be tested against. A proper falsifiability test would require:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Deriving $E = mc^2$ within the framework, to convert
    informational bit-counts to energy-momentum and thence to
    observable quantities.
  \item Using the aspect-ratio time-calibration of Paper~IV
    (a single observational anchor fixing $n = 184$) to convert
    bit-flip ticks to physical time without free parameters.
  \item Comparing the resulting $R(t)$ directly to the
    distance-redshift relation of the Type~Ia supernova datasets,
    not to $\Lambda$CDM's derived expansion history.
\end{enumerate}

This programme is identified as future work in
Section~\ref{sec:future}. The present paper establishes the
qualitative mechanism and the correct level hierarchy; the
quantitative confrontation with raw data awaits Step~1.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper establishes three results.

\textbf{First}, the three-phase expansion profile of standard
cosmology --- early rapid growth, matter-driven deceleration,
late-time acceleration --- emerges from the relational scale factor
$R(t)$ when matter abundance follows lognormal curves gated by the
entropy gradient, with no cosmological constant, inflaton field, or
dark energy term. The acceleration is the geometric reading of
structural evaporation as the system approaches entropy saturation.

\textbf{Second}, dark matter is identified with the first-order
emergent level $L_{\rm DM}$ of the informational hierarchy. It
gravitates via the counting equation by exactly the same mechanism
as ordinary matter --- by withdrawing bits from the spacetime fabric
--- and is electromagnetically invisible not because it is weakly
coupled but because it sheds compression residuals of a different
symmetry class than the photon-type bosons that $L_{\rm bar}$-level
detectors are constituted to register. This requires no new particle
species and no new coupling constant.

\textbf{Third}, the lognormal abundance form is a prediction of the
\theory{} framework for $L_{\rm DM}$ (following from Paper~III's
filter-invariant emergence result) and a \emph{hypothesis under test}
for $L_\nu$ and $L_{\rm bar}$, where it differs from $\Lambda$CDM's
monotonically diluting prediction. Whether that hypothesis is
correct is an open question; it can be decided once $E = mc^2$ is
derived within the framework and the simulation is compared directly
to raw observational anchors rather than to $\Lambda$CDM derived
curves.

\section{Open Problems}
\label{sec:future}

\subsection*{Derivation of $E = mc^2$}

The amplitude scaling between informational bit-counts and
physical energy-momentum is the critical missing link. Without it,
the number densities from the cosmological data cannot be converted
to bit-widths $w_k$, and the simulation cannot be compared
quantitatively to observation. This derivation is being pursued in
the discrete counting arena $\mathcal{C}_D$ of the framework.

\subsection*{The 5:1 Dark-Matter-to-Baryon Ratio}

The observed ratio $\rho_{\rm DM}/\rho_{\rm bar} \approx 5$ is a
sharp, dimensionless quantity that the \theory{} framework should reproduce
from the relative amplitudes and bit-widths of $L_{\rm DM}$ and
$L_{\rm bar}$ alone, once $E = mc^2$ is available to convert
bit-counts to mass-energy. Whether this ratio is derivable from
first principles or must be fitted is an open question.

\subsection*{Quantitative Confrontation with Raw Observational Anchors}

As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:data}, the proper falsifiability
test is a direct comparison of $R(t)$ to the Type~Ia supernova
distance-redshift relation and the CMB power spectrum, using only
the single observational anchor ($n = 184$) established in Paper~IV.
This requires completing the $E = mc^2$ derivation first.

\subsection*{Bit-Width Derivation for Each Level}

The bit-widths $w_{\rm DM}$, $w_\nu$, $w_{\rm bar}$ are currently
treated as free illustrative parameters. The spectral complexity
machinery of Papers~VIII--XI should, in principle, fix these from
the structure of the compression events at each level.
