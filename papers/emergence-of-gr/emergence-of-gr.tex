\begin{abstract}
Papers~I--IV \cite{meskanen2001, meskanen2002, meskanen2003, meskanen2026iv}
established that the universe can be modelled as a finite, static
bitstring of $n$ bits; that an internal observer's entropy coordinate
$\tau$ follows the Ehrenfest bit-flip saturation curve
$S(\tau) = \tfrac{n}{2}\bigl(1-e^{-2\tau}\bigr)$; and that micro-structures
emerge from this walk following a lognormal-like abundance curve that
vanishes at zero entropy and remains small but non-zero at maximum
entropy. The present paper shows that two different ways of reading this
single, already-established entropy curve reproduce, as limits, the two
matter-free solutions that bound the expanding side of the Friedmann
equation: the \emph{direct} reading reproduces the power-law expansion of
the empty Milne universe near $\tau=0$; the \emph{reciprocal} reading
reproduces the exactly constant expansion rate of de~Sitter space at
every $\tau$, not merely asymptotically. No new mechanism is introduced
to obtain the second limit from the first --- both are properties of the
one entropy-saturation curve already derived in Paper~III.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The Friedmann equation governs the expansion of a homogeneous, isotropic
universe. Two matter-free limits bound its expanding solutions on
opposite sides of the parameter space it covers:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Milne universe}: $\rho_{\text{matter}}=0$,
        $\Lambda=0$. Genuinely empty spacetime --- flat Minkowski space
        written in the coordinates of a family of freely receding
        inertial observers. The scale factor grows as $a(t)\propto t$,
        decelerating, with negative spatial curvature $k=-1$ an artefact
        of the coordinate choice rather than real geometry.
  \item \textbf{De~Sitter universe}: $\rho_{\text{matter}}=0$,
        $\Lambda>0$. Also free of ordinary matter, but not of curvature:
        vacuum energy sources a constant, non-zero expansion rate
        $H=\dot a/a=\text{const}$, giving exponential, accelerating
        growth $a(t)\propto e^{Ht}$.
\end{itemize}

These two limits are often treated as unrelated special cases singled
out for their mathematical simplicity. The present paper shows that both
emerge, without additional assumptions, from a single object already
established in this series: the Ehrenfest entropy-saturation curve
$S(\tau)$ derived in Paper~III. Which of the two limits appears depends
only on whether an internal observer's notion of spatial resolution
tracks the entropy consumed so far, or the inverse of the entropy
remaining before saturation.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{The Entropy Walk}

From Paper~III \cite{meskanen2003}, an internal observer's entropy
coordinate $\tau$ (measured in units of bit-flip ticks per bit, $n$
being the total bit count) follows the Ehrenfest saturation curve:

\begin{equation}
  S(\tau) = \frac{n}{2}\Bigl(1 - e^{-2\tau}\Bigr).
  \label{eq:ehrenfest}
\end{equation}

This is a bounded, relaxing process: $S(0)=0$ (zero entropy, the
founding premise's singularity point) and $S(\tau)\to n/2$ as
$\tau\to\infty$ (maximum entropy, the founding premise's horizon-like
equilibrium). It never reaches $S=n$; saturation occurs at exactly half
the bit budget, consistent with the equilibrium of a fair Ehrenfest
process.

Micro-structures (matter) emerge from this walk following a
lognormal-like abundance curve established in Paper~III: zero at
$S=0$, rising to a peak at intermediate entropy, and settling to a
small but non-zero value as $S\to n/2$. Writing this abundance function
as $m(S)$, the fabric/matter partition of Eq.~\eqref{eq:conservation}
below is understood to be $m(\tau) := m\bigl(S(\tau)\bigr)$, a
\emph{non-monotonic} function of $\tau$. The closed form of $m(S)$ is not yet derived; see Section~\ref{sec:future}.

\subsection{Information Conservation}

At any entropy coordinate $\tau$, the $n$ bits are partitioned between
free spacetime fabric and matter structures:

\begin{equation}
  \rho_{\text{fabric}}(\tau) = n - m(\tau),
  \label{eq:conservation}
\end{equation}

with $m(\tau)$ as defined above. Because $m(\tau)\approx 0$ at both
$\tau=0$ and $\tau\to\infty$, this paper's two limiting cases are
\emph{vacuum} limits in the literal sense --- not merely "$m=0$ by
assumption" as before, but $m\approx 0$ as a consequence of the
abundance curve's own shape at both entropy extremes.

\subsection{Two Readings of One Curve}
\label{sec:tworeadings}

In the matter-free limit ($m\approx0$), define two candidate relational
scale factors from the entropy curve $S(\tau)$ alone:

\begin{align}
  R_{\text{dir}}(\tau) &= \frac{S(\tau)}{n/2}
    &&\text{(direct reading: consumed entropy)}
    \label{eq:Rdir}\\[4pt]
  R_{\text{rec}}(\tau) &= \frac{n/2}{\,n/2 - S(\tau)\,}
    &&\text{(reciprocal reading: inverse of remaining gap)}
    \label{eq:Rrec}
\end{align}

\textbf{Direct reading, early $\tau$.} Near $\tau=0$, $S(\tau)\approx
n\tau$ (each tick flips a fresh bit with near-certainty before
collisions become likely), so $R_{\text{dir}}(\tau)\propto\tau$ to
leading order. Numerical fitting of $R_{\text{dir}}(\tau)\sim\tau^{p}$
over $\tau\in(0,\,0.1)$ gives $p\approx0.97$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:milne}),
consistent with the Milne exponent $p=1$ and clearly distinct from the
radiation-dominated ($p=0.5$) or matter-dominated ($p=2/3$)
alternatives. The residual gap from exactly $1$ is attributable to the
finite fitting window already including some curvature from the
approach to saturation; a narrower early window brings the fit closer
to $1$.

\textbf{Reciprocal reading, all $\tau$.} The gap-to-equilibrium
$g(\tau) = n/2 - S(\tau) = \tfrac{n}{2}e^{-2\tau}$ is \emph{exactly}
exponential at every $\tau$, not merely asymptotically. Its reciprocal
therefore gives an exactly constant fractional growth rate:

\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{R_{\text{rec}}}\frac{dR_{\text{rec}}}{d\tau} = 2
  \qquad\text{for all } \tau,
  \label{eq:constantH}
\end{equation}

verified numerically to $10^{-6}$ at $\tau\in\{0.1,0.5,1.0,2.0,2.9\}$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:desitter}). This is the defining property of de~Sitter
expansion: a genuinely constant Hubble parameter, here obtained as an
identity of the same curve used for the direct reading, not through a
separately introduced exponential ansatz.

Both readings originate from the single entropy curve of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ehrenfest}; no additional free parameter or mechanism
distinguishes them beyond the choice of which quantity --- entropy
consumed, or the inverse of entropy remaining --- an internal observer's
resolution is built from.

\section{Mapping to General Relativity}
\label{sec:gr}

The Friedmann equation is:

\[
\left(\frac{\dot{a}}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho_{\text{matter}}
  + \frac{\Lambda}{3}.
\]

The two matter-free limits identified above map onto its boundary
solutions as follows.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
\textbf{Regime} & \textbf{Information picture} & \textbf{GR solution} \\
\hline
$\tau\to 0$, direct reading & $m\approx0$, $R_{\text{dir}}\propto\tau$
  & Milne-like ($\rho=0$, $\Lambda=0$) \\
$\tau\to\infty$, reciprocal reading & $m\approx0$,
  $\dot R_{\text{rec}}/R_{\text{rec}}=\text{const}$
  & De~Sitter-like ($\rho=0$, $\Lambda>0$) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Under the reciprocal reading, the identification with the cosmological
constant is direct:

\begin{equation}
  \Lambda \;\longleftrightarrow\; \frac{1}{R_{\text{rec}}}
  \frac{dR_{\text{rec}}}{d\tau} = 2,
  \label{eq:lambda_map}
\end{equation}

a constant fixed entirely by the Ehrenfest saturation rate already
established in Paper~III, not introduced as a free parameter here.

\textbf{What this section does not claim.} The matter-filled regime
between these two limits --- where $m(\tau)>0$ and the Friedmann
equation is genuinely modulated by matter density, i.e.\ the actual
observed history of our universe --- requires the abundance function
$m(S)$ referenced in Section~\ref{sec:setup}. Its closed form is not yet
derived, so no claim is made here about reproducing the intermediate,
matter-dominated Friedmann era quantitatively; only the two bounding
vacuum limits are established.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why Two Readings, Not Two Mechanisms}

It is tempting to treat the direct and reciprocal readings as two
different physical regimes requiring different physics. They are not:
they are two different quantities computed from the identical entropy
trajectory $S(\tau)$. This mirrors, in miniature, the real cosmological
picture: standard $\Lambda$CDM cosmology begins in a hot, dense,
decelerating phase and is expected to asymptote toward a de~Sitter phase
in the far future as matter dilutes and the cosmological constant comes
to dominate. That the present framework's two vacuum limits sit at the
two ends of the \emph{same} entropy walk, in the same order (Milne-like
near the origin, de~Sitter-like near saturation), is consistent with
that overall history, though it does not by itself establish the
matter-dominated middle era connecting them.


\subsection{An Open Tension: Power Law versus Curvature}

The direct-reading match to Milne has been checked only at the level of
the scale-factor \emph{power law} ($R_{\text{dir}}\propto\tau^{p}$,
$p\approx0.97$). The Milne universe is additionally characterised by
identically \emph{zero} spacetime curvature everywhere --- it is flat
Minkowski space in unusual coordinates. Whether the early-$\tau$ limit
of this framework is genuinely flat, or instead carries a curvature
divergence more reminiscent of an ordinary Big-Bang-type singularity
that merely happens to share Milne's power-law exponent, has not been
checked and is left as an open question. The two possibilities are
observationally and structurally different, and the present numerics do
not yet distinguish them.

\subsection{The Cosmological Constant}

Under the reciprocal reading, $\Lambda$ is not a property of the vacuum
requiring cancellation against a much larger quantum field theory
estimate; it is the constant fractional rate at which the
gap-to-saturation shrinks, fixed by the Ehrenfest process itself. $\Lambda$ is an emergent
counting quantity rather than a fundamental input.

\subsection{No External Time}

As before, $\tau$ is not an external clock but the internal observer's
reading of progress along the entropy gradient established in
Papers~I--IV. The direct and reciprocal readings are two different
functions of that same internal coordinate, not two different clocks.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper establishes one result: the Milne and de~Sitter vacuum limits that bound the
expanding side of the Friedmann equation both emerge from the single
Ehrenfest entropy-saturation curve of Paper~III, as two different
readings (direct and reciprocal) of the same underlying quantity, with
no additional mechanism introduced between them. The reciprocal
reading's match to constant-$H$ de~Sitter expansion is exact at every
$\tau$ tested, not merely asymptotic. The direct reading's match to
Milne's power law is numerically close ($p\approx0.97$ against an ideal
$1$) with the residual attributed to finite-window fitting bias.

The Schwarzschild singularity is explicitly not claimed here; it belongs
to the spatial, black-hole treatment elsewhere in this series. The
matter-filled Friedmann era connecting the two vacuum limits established
here remains open, pending a derivation of the abundance function
$m(S)$.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

\subsection*{Deriving $m(S)$ From First Principles}

The single largest open problem carried over from this revision: a
closed-form derivation of the lognormal-like abundance function $m(S)$,
from the same counting/complexity principles used elsewhere in this
series, rather than assumed as a qualitative shape. This is required
before the matter-filled Friedmann era can be addressed quantitatively.

\subsection*{Curvature, Not Just Power Law, at Early $\tau$}

Section~\ref{sec:discussion} flags that only the scale-factor exponent,
not the curvature itself, has been checked against Milne at early
$\tau$. Resolving this requires a genuine curvature analogue within the
counting framework, which has not yet been constructed.

\subsection*{Finite-$n$ Stochastic Verification}

The results of Section~\ref{sec:tworeadings} use the closed-form,
infinite-precision Ehrenfest curve. A finite bit budget $n$ fluctuates
around equilibrium rather than approaching it smoothly; the reciprocal
reading's exact constancy should be re-checked against actual stochastic
trajectories, where it is expected to hold only approximately, with
fluctuations growing as $\tau\to\infty$ and the gap $g(\tau)$ becomes
comparable to the chain's natural fluctuation scale.
